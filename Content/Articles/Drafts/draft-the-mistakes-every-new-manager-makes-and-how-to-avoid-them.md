# The Mistakes Every New Manager Makes (And How to Avoid Them)

The day I became a manager, nobody sat me down and explained what the job was.

One Friday I was a senior engineer writing code and solving technical problems. The following Monday I had five people reporting to me, a calendar full of meetings I didn't understand, and a vague directive to "lead the team." Nobody told me that the skills that got me promoted were almost entirely different from the skills I'd now need to succeed.

That first year was rough. I made nearly every mistake on this list. Some of them cost me people. All of them cost me time. And the frustrating part is that every one of them was predictable and preventable, if someone had just told me what to watch for.

So that's what this article is. The field guide I wish someone had handed me on day one.

## Why New Managers Struggle

Before we get into specifics, let me frame the problem.

The transition from individual contributor to manager is one of the hardest career shifts that exists. It's not a promotion in the traditional sense. It's a career change. The thing you were good at, the thing that got you noticed, the thing that earned you this role, is no longer your primary job.

Your primary job is now other people. Their output, their growth, their problems, their careers. And nobody teaches you how to do that. Most companies treat management like a natural extension of being good at your craft. It isn't. It's a completely different discipline, and treating it otherwise is how you end up with brilliant engineers who are terrible managers.

Here are the mistakes I see every time.

## Mistake 1: You Keep Doing the Individual Contributor Work

This is the most common mistake, and it's the hardest to let go of.

You got promoted because you were great at the work. Solving hard technical problems, closing deals, designing systems, whatever it was. That work felt satisfying. You were good at it. You could see the results. And now, as a manager, you still have access to it. The codebase is right there. The deals are right there.

So you keep doing it. You tell yourself it's because the team needs you, or because this particular problem is too complex to delegate, or because you can do it faster. All of those things might even be true. And all of them are traps.

Here's what happens when you keep doing IC work as a manager: your team stops growing. Why would they stretch into hard problems when you're going to swoop in and solve them anyway? You become the bottleneck on everything. And the actual management work, the 1:1s, the career development conversations, the team dynamics, gets squeezed into the margins.

I had to force myself to stop writing code in my first management role. It was physically uncomfortable. I would see a problem in a code review and my fingers would itch to just fix it myself. Instead, I learned to write a comment explaining the issue and let the engineer figure it out. It took three times as long. It was the right call every time.

Your job is no longer to do the work. Your job is to make sure the work gets done, by building people who can do it without you.

## Mistake 2: Trying to Be Friends with Everyone

New managers, especially those promoted from within the team, often try to maintain the peer relationships they had before. They want to still be "one of the team." They joke around, avoid hard conversations, go easy on feedback, and hope that being liked will translate to being effective.

It won't.

This doesn't mean you need to become cold or distant. But the relationship has fundamentally changed. You now make decisions about their raises, their promotions, their project assignments, and potentially their employment. Pretending that dynamic doesn't exist insults everyone's intelligence.

The best managers I've ever worked with were warm, caring, and invested in their people. They were also clear about expectations, honest about performance, and willing to have uncomfortable conversations. Those two things are not in conflict. In fact, the willingness to be honest is the deepest form of respect.

You can be a manager your team likes. But being liked cannot be the goal. The goal is to be trusted. Trust comes from consistency, honesty, and follow-through. Likability is a side effect of good management, not a prerequisite.

## Mistake 3: Avoiding Hard Conversations Until They Become Crises

I call this the "hope it resolves itself" strategy. It doesn't.

New managers see a performance issue, a conflict between team members, a behavior that's dragging the team down, and they wait. They tell themselves they need more data. They tell themselves it might get better on its own. They tell themselves it's not that bad.

Then three months later, the issue has metastasized. The underperformer has dragged down the whole team's output. The conflict has split the team into factions. The problematic behavior has become the culture. And now the conversation that would have been a 15-minute feedback session is an hour-long crisis intervention.

Here's the rule I live by now: if something needs to be said, say it within a week. Not within a quarter. Not at the next performance review. Within a week. The longer you wait, the harder it gets and the less effective it becomes.

I once waited four months to address an engineer who was consistently dismissive in code reviews. By the time I raised it, two other engineers had already asked to transfer off the team. I could have prevented that with one honest conversation in month one. The cost of my avoidance was two lost team members and three months of toxic dynamics.

Have the conversation. Have it early. Have it with specifics, with care, and with the expectation that the person can improve. But have it.

## Mistake 4: Not Listening Enough

New managers feel pressure to prove they belong. They want to show their team, their boss, and themselves that they deserve the role. So they talk. They share their vision. They make decisions. They lead, in the way they think leading looks like.

But leading, especially at first, looks a lot more like listening.

Your team knows more about their work than you do. They know where the problems are. They know what's broken. They know what the previous manager got wrong. All of that information is available to you, for free, if you just ask and then shut up long enough to hear the answer.

In my first 1:1s as a new manager, I asked every person on the team the same five questions:

1. What's working well that I should absolutely not change?
2. What's broken that you wish someone would fix?
3. What do you need from me that you're not getting?
4. What's one thing you want to be working on six months from now?
5. Is there anything else you want me to know?

Then I listened. I took notes. I didn't defend the previous decisions. I didn't promise changes. I just listened. The amount of useful information I got from that exercise was staggering. And the act of listening, of genuinely wanting to hear what they had to say, built more credibility than any decision I could have made.

## Mistake 5: Managing Everyone the Same Way

This is the mistake that took me the longest to recognize.

New managers often create a system: a consistent process for 1:1s, for feedback, for project assignments. And they apply it uniformly to everyone. Same cadence, same questions, same expectations. It feels fair. It feels scalable. It's wrong.

People are different. Some of your people want daily check-ins. Some want to be left alone for a week and then have a deep conversation. Some need explicit praise to stay motivated. Some find public praise embarrassing. Some want stretch assignments. Some want to go deep on what they're already doing.

Management is not a one-size-fits-all discipline. It requires you to learn each person individually and adjust your approach accordingly. This takes more work than applying a uniform system. It is also the only way to actually develop people.

I had two engineers once who were at the same level with similar technical skills. One needed me to clearly scope every project and check in frequently. The other needed me to describe the outcome and then disappear until they were done. If I'd managed them the same way, I would have either suffocated the second one or abandoned the first one.

The way you figure this out is by asking. Literally ask each person: "How do you like to work? How much oversight do you want? How do you prefer to receive feedback? What does support from me look like for you?" Then listen, and actually do what they say.

## Mistake 6: Not Setting Clear Expectations

New managers often assume expectations are obvious. The team should know what "good" looks like. They should know the quality bar. They should know the deadlines. They should know how to communicate about their work.

None of this is obvious. None of it.

If you haven't explicitly stated what you expect, you have no right to be disappointed when it isn't met. Unstated expectations are resentments in waiting.

Be painfully explicit. "I expect you to flag blockers within 24 hours, not wait until standup." "I expect design reviews to happen before engineering starts, not during." "I expect that if a deadline is going to slip, I hear about it a week in advance, not the day of."

Write them down. Share them with the team. Revisit them. And when someone falls short, reference the expectation specifically. "We agreed that blockers would be flagged within 24 hours. This one sat for three days. What happened?"

Clear expectations are not micromanagement. They are the foundation of trust. When people know exactly what's expected, they can either meet the bar or have an honest conversation about why they can't. That clarity is a gift.

## Mistake 7: Neglecting Your Own Development

New managers pour all their energy into their team and forget to invest in themselves. They stop reading, stop learning, stop seeking feedback on their own performance.

This is a slow-motion disaster.

Management is a skill. It improves with deliberate practice, reflection, and input. If you're not actively working on getting better at it, you're stagnating. And a stagnating manager creates a stagnating team.

Seek out a mentor. Read one management book a quarter. Ask your own manager for feedback. Ask your team for feedback (this is terrifying and essential). Reflect on what went well and what didn't at the end of each week.

And here's the uncomfortable truth: you will make mistakes. All of them. The goal isn't perfection. It's awareness. Know when you've messed up, own it, fix it, and move on.

## Mistake 8: Thinking You Need to Have All the Answers

The IC mindset is about solving problems. You see a question, you find the answer. That's how you added value.

As a manager, trying to have all the answers is a disaster. First, you won't. The problems are bigger, more ambiguous, and more human than the ones you solved as an IC. Second, even when you do know the answer, giving it directly robs your team of the chance to figure it out.

The manager's superpower is asking the right questions, not providing the right answers.

"What have you tried so far?"
"What's your instinct on this?"
"What would you do if I weren't here?"
"What are the risks of that approach?"

These questions develop your team's judgment. Over time, they stop needing you for answers. That's not a failure of your management. That's the entire point.

## The 90-Day Rule

I tell every new manager I mentor: give yourself 90 days. That's how long it takes to start feeling like you know what you're doing. The first 30 days will be disorienting. The second 30 will be exhausting. The third 30, it starts to click.

During those 90 days, focus on three things:

1. **Build relationships.** 1:1s with every team member, every week, no exceptions. Learn who they are, what they need, and what motivates them.
2. **Listen more than you talk.** Your opinion on how things should work is worth very little in month one. Absorb first.
3. **Set clear expectations.** For the team and for yourself. Be explicit about how you plan to operate and what you need from them.

Everything else can wait. The systems, the process improvements, the reorgs, the new tools. All of that can come after you've built the foundation of trust and understanding that makes everything else possible.

## The Most Important Thing Nobody Tells You

Here is the thing I wish someone had said to me on my first day as a manager.

The job is not about you anymore.

Your success is no longer measured by your output. It's measured by your team's output, their growth, their engagement, their retention. You might go an entire week without producing anything tangible yourself, and that might be the best week your team has ever had.

That shift is hard. It requires a kind of ego sacrifice that nobody prepares you for. You stop getting credit for the brilliant solution. You start getting satisfaction from watching someone else arrive at it.

When you make that shift, when you genuinely internalize that your job is to build people, everything else falls into place. The 1:1s become natural. The feedback becomes easier. The hard conversations feel less scary. Because all of it serves the same purpose: helping your people become the best versions of themselves.

That's the job. It's the best job there is. And it starts with not making the mistakes that every new manager makes.
