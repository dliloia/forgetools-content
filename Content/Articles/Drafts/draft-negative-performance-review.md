## If This Is a Surprise, Own That First

Here's the scenario: Casey is a mid-level project manager who has struggled with stakeholder communication for six months, missed internal deadlines, and generated complaints from two cross-functional partners about follow-through. You've addressed some of it in one-on-ones. But looking back at your notes, you gave hints. You made suggestions. You never said, clearly and without softening, "Casey, this needs to change or it's going to affect your review."

Tomorrow morning you're sitting across from someone who might not know how serious this is.

That's a management failure, and it belongs to you before anything else. Not because you're a bad manager, but because most managers make this exact mistake. We soften hard feedback to protect the relationship, and in doing so we protect the relationship so well that the employee has no idea there's a problem. Then review time comes and the rating lands like a grenade.

So before you figure out how to deliver the review, get honest about what you're actually delivering: not just a performance rating, but a correction to a six-month miscommunication that you participated in creating.

## Structure the Conversation, Don't Wing It

You have the written review ready. Good. But the document isn't the conversation, it's the reference. The conversation is what matters.

Start by setting context, not to soften the blow, but so Casey knows this is a serious conversation and not a formality. Something like: "I want to be straightforward with you today. This review is going to reflect some challenges we've had over the last six months, and I want to walk through it carefully because I think it's important that we're on the same page about where things stand."

Then deliver the rating. Clearly. Don't bury it. Don't put three paragraphs of strengths before it and three paragraphs of strengths after it so the whole thing becomes noise. Name the rating, name what it means, and give Casey a moment to absorb it before you move forward.

After that, walk through the specific issues with specific examples. Not generalizations. Not "stakeholder communication has been a challenge." Actual events. The deadline that slipped on the Henderson project. The two partners who reached out with concerns, what they said, what the impact was. Specific examples tied to clear expectations are the only thing that makes a performance conversation credible. Without them, you're just delivering an opinion.

Then, and this is where a lot of managers stop when they shouldn't, you own your part. You tell Casey directly that when you addressed these issues in your one-on-ones, you weren't direct enough. That you gave suggestions when you should have given warnings. That Casey may not have understood the severity of where things stood, and that's on you.

This is not an apology for the rating. The rating is accurate and it stands. But accountability flows both directions in a real management relationship, and Casey deserves to hear that you see your own role in how this unfolded.

## Separate the Person From the Performance

Casey works hard. Casey is well-liked. The issues are about execution and communication at the level the role requires, not about attitude or effort.

Say that out loud in the conversation. Not as padding around the hard news, but as a genuine distinction that Casey needs to understand. Because when people receive a below-expectations rating, the first place their brain goes is "my manager thinks I'm a bad person" or "I'm going to get fired." Neither of those may be true, and if neither is true, Casey needs to hear it clearly.

"This is not about your work ethic. You put in the hours and people on this team genuinely like working with you. The gap is in execution against the requirements of the role, specifically in stakeholder communication and follow-through on commitments. That's a fixable problem, and I want to work with you on fixing it."

That's not softening the rating. That's being precise about what the problem actually is, which is both fairer to Casey and more useful for improvement.

## When Casey Gets Emotional or Defensive

It's going to happen. Either Casey will be genuinely surprised by the rating and the surprise turns into hurt or anger, or Casey suspected something was coming and the confirmation triggers defensiveness. Either way, your job is to stay steady.

Don't rush through the discomfort. The instinct when someone gets emotional is to speed up, wrap it up, move to the positive parts. Resist that. Let there be silence. Let Casey feel what Casey feels. You can acknowledge it without derailing the conversation: "I understand this is hard to hear. Take a moment."

If Casey pushes back on the specific examples, listen. Don't immediately capitulate because you feel bad, but also don't get defensive. If Casey has context you didn't have, take it in. If the pushback is deflection or frustration talking, acknowledge the feeling and return to the facts. "I hear that this feels unfair. What I can tell you is that these are the situations I have documented and the feedback I received from your cross-functional partners. That's what I'm basing this on."

What you absolutely cannot do is walk back the rating in the room because the conversation got uncomfortable. That would be worse than being too soft in the one-on-ones. If the rating is accurate, it stays.

## The Improvement Plan Has to Be Real

This is where a lot of performance conversations die. The manager delivers the hard news, gets through the emotional part, and then says something vague like "I want to support you going forward" and closes the laptop.

That's not a plan. That's an exit strategy from a difficult meeting.

Casey needs a clear picture of what success looks like over the next 60 to 90 days. What specifically needs to change in stakeholder communication? What does "follow-through" look like measured, not felt? Pick two or three concrete, observable behaviors and attach them to timelines and checkpoints.

"By the end of Q2, I want to see you proactively updating your cross-functional partners at least weekly on active projects without being asked. I want to see zero missed internal deadlines over the next 60 days. And I want us to do bi-weekly check-ins where we're explicitly reviewing both of those things together."

Then hold the checkpoints. Put them on the calendar before you leave the room. If you set a 60-day review and then cancel it twice because you got busy, you've told Casey the plan wasn't real.

## Be Honest About Consequences

The kindest thing you can do for Casey right now is tell the truth clearly enough that Casey actually understands it.

That includes the consequences of not improving. Not as a threat, not delivered with heat, but as information Casey deserves to have. "If we don't see meaningful improvement in these areas over the next 90 days, this becomes a more serious conversation about whether this role is the right fit."

You may feel like that's harsh. It isn't. What's harsh is letting Casey spend another six months uncertain about where they stand, then getting blindsided again.

Casey is a grown professional. Give Casey the real picture, the specific gaps, the clear path, and the honest stakes. Then do your part. Show up to the check-ins, give real-time feedback when you see the behaviors slip or improve, and don't disappear after the hard conversation is over.

## The Meeting After the Meeting

The review conversation ends, but the management work doesn't. Check in with Casey the next day, briefly, not to re-litigate the review but just to check in as a human. See how Casey is doing. That 30-second check-in tells Casey that you're in this with them, not just delivering verdicts.

The managers I've seen handle this well are the ones who treat the review as the start of a better management relationship, not the conclusion of a bad one. The ones who handle it poorly close the laptop, file the paperwork, and go back to avoiding the hard conversations until the next review cycle forces their hand again.

Casey has a real shot at turning this around. Whether that happens depends as much on how you manage the next 90 days as it does on Casey.
