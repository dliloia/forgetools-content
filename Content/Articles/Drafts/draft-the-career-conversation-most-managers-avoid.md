# The Career Development Conversation Most Managers Avoid (And How to Have It)

Let me describe a conversation that happens on most teams, except it doesn't.

A manager knows they should ask their direct report about career goals. They think about it before the 1:1. Then the 1:1 starts, there's a project issue to discuss, the employee mentions a blocker, and the career conversation gets pushed to "next time." Next time, the same thing happens. And the time after that. And eventually, the manager stops thinking about it, because the absence of the conversation has become the norm.

Months pass. A year passes. The employee, who has been waiting for their manager to bring it up, concludes that either their manager doesn't care about their career, or there's no path forward, or both. They start looking at other companies. Not because the work is bad. Because nobody asked them where they wanted to go.

I know this pattern because I've been on both sides of it. I've been the manager who avoided the conversation. I've been the employee who left because it never happened. And after managing hundreds of people, I can tell you with certainty: the career conversation is one of the highest-leverage discussions a manager can have, and it's the one that gets skipped most often.

## Why Managers Avoid This Conversation

The avoidance isn't laziness. It's fear. Specifically, it's three fears.

### Fear 1: They'll Ask for Something I Can't Give

This is the big one. The manager imagines the employee saying "I want to be promoted to senior engineer in six months" or "I want to move to the data science team" or "I want to manage people." And the manager doesn't know if any of those things are possible. So rather than open a door they can't control, they leave it shut.

This fear is based on a misunderstanding. The career conversation is not a negotiation. You are not making promises. You're understanding what the person wants, so you can help them move toward it. There is a massive difference between "I'll promote you in six months" and "Let's figure out what you'd need to demonstrate to be ready for promotion, and create opportunities for you to demonstrate it."

The first is a commitment you may not be able to keep. The second is development, which is always in your control.

### Fear 2: They'll Say They Want to Leave

Some managers worry that asking "where do you want to go?" will remind the employee that "elsewhere" is an option. This is magical thinking. The employee already knows that elsewhere is an option. Every recruiter in their LinkedIn inbox is reminding them daily.

The question isn't whether they're thinking about other options. They are. The question is whether they feel like their current role is part of the path toward what they want. If you don't know what they want, you can't possibly make the case that your team is the right place for them.

I had an engineer tell me, in a career conversation, that he eventually wanted to be a CTO. On my team, he was a mid-level engineer. The gap was huge. But by knowing his goal, I could tailor his work to build the skills he'd need: cross-team projects, stakeholder communication, architectural decisions. He stayed on my team for three more years. Not because I could make him a CTO. But because he could see that the path led in the right direction.

### Fear 3: The Conversation Will Be Awkward

This one is just true. Career conversations can be awkward. People sometimes don't know what they want. Sometimes they want things that aren't realistic. Sometimes the honest answer involves telling someone they're not ready for what they think they're ready for.

The awkwardness is real. It's also irrelevant. The cost of the awkwardness is one uncomfortable conversation. The cost of avoidance is an employee who disengages, stops growing, and eventually leaves. The math is not close.

## What Employees Actually Want from This Conversation

Employees don't need their manager to have all the answers. They need three things:

1. **To feel seen.** They want to know that their manager recognizes them as a person with ambitions, not just a resource with a task list. The simple act of asking "what do you want your career to look like?" signals that you see them as a whole person.

2. **To have a direction.** Even if the destination is unclear, people want to feel like they're moving. Forward momentum is motivating. Stagnation is corrosive. The career conversation creates direction, even when the path isn't perfectly defined.

3. **To know their manager is invested.** When a manager says "I want to help you get where you want to go," and then backs it up with actions, it transforms the relationship. The employee stops seeing the manager as someone who assigns tasks and starts seeing them as someone who is actively building their career.

That investment is the single strongest retention tool available to any manager. Stronger than compensation. Stronger than perks. Stronger than title.

## The Five Questions That Open a Real Career Conversation

Here are the five questions I use. They're simple, but each one is designed to reveal something important.

### Question 1: "Where do you want to be in two to three years?"

Not five years, which feels too abstract. Not one year, which feels too tactical. Two to three years is the sweet spot: close enough to feel real, far enough to be aspirational.

Listen for specifics. "I want to be a tech lead" is specific. "I want to grow" is not. If the answer is vague, push gently: "What would that look like day to day? What kind of work would you be doing?"

### Question 2: "What kind of work makes you lose track of time?"

This question gets at intrinsic motivation. The work that energizes someone is usually the work they're best at and the work they should be doing more of. If they light up talking about system design but drag their feet on project management, that tells you something important about where to point them.

### Question 3: "What skills do you want to develop that your current role isn't giving you?"

This reveals the gap between where they are and where they want to be. And it's the gap you can actually address. If they want to develop leadership skills, you can give them a mentorship opportunity. If they want to develop technical depth in a new area, you can route a relevant project their way.

The answer to this question is your development roadmap for this person.

### Question 4: "What's holding you back right now?"

Sometimes the answer is obvious: "I need more experience with X." Sometimes it's surprising: "I feel like I'm always working on maintenance and never get the greenfield projects." Sometimes it's uncomfortable: "I don't feel like my contributions are visible to leadership."

Whatever the answer, it's gold. It tells you exactly where to invest your energy as their manager.

### Question 5: "How can I help?"

This is the most important question and the one you need to actually act on. Not "how can I help in theory." But "what specific thing can I do in the next month to move you closer to where you want to go?"

And then do it.

## Career Development vs. Career Planning

Let me draw an important distinction.

Career planning is mapping out a specific trajectory: "In 18 months you'll be promoted to senior, then in three years you'll move into management, then in five years you'll be a director." This is mostly fiction. Careers don't follow linear plans. Companies change. Interests evolve. Opportunities appear and disappear unpredictably.

Career development is building capabilities. It's about expanding what someone can do, increasing their range, and creating options. You can't control whether a specific promotion opens up. You can control whether your person is developing the skills that make them ready for it when it does.

Focus on development, not planning. Ask: "What can we work on together over the next quarter that makes you stronger, regardless of what opportunities emerge?" That framing takes the pressure off both of you. You're not promising a specific outcome. You're committing to growth. And growth is always within your control.

## Making It a Recurring Practice, Not a One-Time Event

The career conversation is not something you have once and check off. It's a thread that runs through your 1:1s all year.

I dedicate the last ten minutes of every 1:1 to growth and development. Not every session goes deep. Sometimes it's a quick check-in: "How's the mentoring project going? Are you getting what you hoped out of it?" Sometimes it's a deeper conversation about goals and direction.

The key is consistency. When career development is a regular topic, it becomes normal. The employee knows it's okay to bring it up. They know you're thinking about it. They know it's not something that only gets attention during performance reviews.

Here's a practical cadence that works:

- **Weekly:** A quick growth check-in in your 1:1. "What did you learn this week? What do you want to work on next?"
- **Monthly:** A deeper conversation about progress toward development goals. "How are you feeling about the direction? Anything we should adjust?"
- **Quarterly:** A career-level conversation. "Let's revisit where you want to go and make sure we're pointed in the right direction."

This cadence ensures that career development isn't an event. It's an ongoing practice. And it takes almost no extra time because it's woven into meetings you're already having.

## What to Do When You Can't Give Them What They Want

Sometimes the conversation reveals that what the employee wants isn't available on your team. Maybe they want to move into a role that doesn't exist in your organization. Maybe they want to work on technology that your team doesn't use. Maybe they want to manage people and there's no management track open.

This is where most managers panic. Don't.

First, acknowledge it honestly. "I hear you. That's not something I can offer right now, and I want to be upfront about that."

Then explore what you can offer. "There are some elements of that work I can get you closer to. Here's what I'm thinking." Maybe you can't make them a data scientist, but you can give them a project that involves data analysis. Maybe you can't promote them to management, but you can have them lead a project and practice the skills.

And if the gap is truly unbridgeable, be honest about that too. "I want to help you get where you want to go, even if that means eventually it's not on this team." This feels counterintuitive. You're acknowledging that they might leave. But here's the paradox: the manager who helps someone plan for their next step, even if it's somewhere else, earns more loyalty and longer tenure than the manager who pretends the question doesn't exist.

People stay longer with managers who are invested in their career, even when the next step is unclear. They leave fastest from managers who never ask.

## The Cost of Not Having This Conversation

Let me put it in concrete terms.

Replacing a high-performing employee costs between 50% and 200% of their annual salary when you factor in recruiting, onboarding, ramp-up time, and the institutional knowledge that walks out the door with them.

A 30-minute career conversation in a 1:1, held once a quarter, costs nothing. It requires no budget, no approval, no headcount. Just a manager who cares enough to ask.

The employee who believes their manager is invested in their career stays longer. They work harder. They engage more deeply. They recommend your team to other talented people. They forgive the bad quarters, the tough projects, the imperfect decisions, because they trust that the overall trajectory is positive.

The employee who never has this conversation draws their own conclusions. And those conclusions are rarely in your favor.

## Start This Week

Pick one person on your team. The person you've been meaning to have this conversation with but haven't. Schedule 15 extra minutes on your next 1:1. And ask the first question: "Where do you want to be in two to three years?"

Then listen. Really listen. Not to respond, not to problem-solve, not to manage expectations. Just listen.

What you hear will change how you manage that person. And the fact that you asked will change how they feel about working for you.

That's the conversation. It's not complicated. It's not risky. It's just uncomfortable enough that most managers avoid it. Don't be most managers.
