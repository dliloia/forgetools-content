## The Situation Nobody Wants to Deal With

You have a team of five. Four of them are solid. The fifth, Morgan, has been inconsistent for two months. Missed self-imposed deadlines, work that requires more review cycles than anyone else's, two unexplained absences from team meetings. In your 1:1s, Morgan says everything is "on track," but the output tells a different story.

Your most senior person pulled you aside and said something you can't unhear: "I don't want to throw anyone under the bus, but I've picked up three of Morgan's tasks this month and I'm getting burned out." Another team member made a pointed comment in standup about "some people" not updating the project board.

You like Morgan. They were strong in their first year. Something has shifted. You're not sure what.

This is one of the most common scenarios managers face and one of the most mishandled. What you do in the next week will either start fixing the problem or make it worse. Both paths are easy to stumble onto by accident, so let's be deliberate.

## Before You Talk to Morgan, Do This

The worst version of this conversation starts with you walking in armed with secondhand complaints and vague impressions. That's not a performance conversation, that's an ambush, and Morgan will feel it. Even if you're right about the underperformance, you'll lose the conversation.

Before you sit down with Morgan, get your own evidence in order. Look at the actual record: which deadlines were missed, by how much, and how many times? How many additional review cycles has their work required, and compared to what baseline? What were the two meetings they skipped, and did they give any notice? Write it down. Specifics, not impressions.

Then ask yourself an honest question: do you actually know what's causing this? Two months of declining performance has a lot of possible explanations. Unclear expectations. A personal situation Morgan hasn't mentioned. A skills gap that wasn't visible until now. Being in the wrong role. Disengagement. Burnout. You don't know which one it is yet, and that matters a lot, because the conversation you have with someone who's struggling silently is different from the conversation you have with someone who's checked out.

Go in curious, not certain.

## The Conversation Itself

This is a direct conversation, not a coaching session that works its way around to the problem. Morgan deserves to know why you're having it.

Open with what you've observed, not what you've heard. "Over the past two months, I've noticed some patterns I want to talk through. You've missed several of your own deadlines, the review cycle on your work has been longer than usual, and you weren't in two team meetings with no heads-up. I want to understand what's going on."

That's it. Then you stop talking.

Give Morgan room to respond before you add anything else. What they say next will tell you a lot. If they're defensive, that's information. If they're relieved someone finally asked, that's different information. If they break down and tell you something is going on at home, that's a third path entirely. You need to hear whatever is actually there before you decide how to proceed.

What you avoid: basing any part of this conversation on what your other team members told you. "The team has noticed" is not something you can use here without eroding trust in both directions. You either observed it yourself or you didn't bring it up. The senior team member who came to you was right to do so, and you'll address the team's frustration separately. Morgan doesn't need to know that conversation happened.

You also avoid hedging the problem to death. Saying "I just wanted to check in and see how things are going" is not the same conversation. Morgan will leave thinking it was a routine 1:1 and nothing will change. Be direct about the gap. That's what Morgan needs from you.

## Set Clear Expectations Before You Leave the Room

If Morgan's response opens up a real reason, a personal situation, a workload problem you didn't know about, a skills gap you can close, then you adjust the conversation accordingly. But even then, you leave with specific, documented expectations and a timeline.

"Here's what I need to see in the next 30 days" is not optional. It's the most important thing you can say. Vague improvement goals are not improvement goals. They're wishes.

Write it down afterward and send it to Morgan in a short email. Not a formal warning, just a clear record of what you discussed and what you both agreed to. This protects Morgan and it protects you. It also signals that this conversation was real, not a chat you'll both pretend didn't happen.

## What About the Rest of the Team?

You have two problems happening at the same time, and they require different conversations.

The problem with Morgan is a performance gap that may or may not have an explainable cause. That conversation belongs in a private 1:1.

The problem with your team is that they're carrying extra load and starting to express frustration in ways that could damage the team's culture if left alone. That problem belongs with you. It's not Morgan's job to explain themselves to the team, and it's not the team's job to manage Morgan's output.

What you can do is address the workload directly. Go back to the senior team member and acknowledge what they told you. "I heard you, and I'm taking it seriously" goes a long way. If Morgan's tasks are genuinely overloading others, redistribute some of them short term. Don't make your best contributors suffer through an open-ended situation without any signal that you're paying attention.

The pointed standup comment about "some people" is a different issue. Let that one go for now. Calling it out publicly turns it into a bigger thing than it needs to be. If it keeps happening, address it privately with the person who said it.

## If the First Conversation Doesn't Move the Needle

One conversation is not a plan. It's a starting point.

Follow up consistently. Check in at every 1:1. Track whether the specific things you named are actually improving. If two weeks pass and nothing has changed, have the conversation again, and this time be more explicit about what's at stake.

At some point, if the pattern continues despite clear expectations and consistent follow-up, the conversation changes. That's when you're in a formal performance discussion, and depending on your organization's process, HR should be in the loop before you get there.

Most managers either act too slowly or skip straight to formal discipline without doing the coaching work in between. Both are mistakes. The path is: observe, name the gap, set expectations, follow up, and escalate only when you've genuinely done those things and the situation hasn't changed.

## The Thing You Can't Lose Sight Of

You like Morgan. That's fine. It's actually a good thing, it means you're motivated to handle this well rather than just manage them out for convenience.

But liking Morgan doesn't change what's fair to the rest of your team. The four solid contributors are watching what you do here. If Morgan's underperformance continues without consequence, you've told every other person on your team that standards are optional. That's a much bigger problem than one person's rough two months.

Being fair to Morgan means being honest with them, giving them a real chance to turn it around, and actually trying to understand what's going on. Being fair to your team means not letting their frustration fester while you wait and hope.

You can do both. That's the job.
