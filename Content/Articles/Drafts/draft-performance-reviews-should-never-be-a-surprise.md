# Performance Reviews Should Never Be a Surprise: How Consistent 1:1s Make Review Season Painless

Every six months, the same thing happens across every company. Review season arrives, and managers panic.

They open a blank document and stare at it. They try to remember what happened four months ago. They skim through Slack messages looking for evidence of accomplishments. They write vague sentences like "consistently demonstrates strong communication skills" because they can't remember a single specific example.

Meanwhile, the employee sits in the review meeting and hears a bunch of generalities that could apply to anyone. Or worse, they hear feedback about something that happened in March for the first time in October. They walk out confused, frustrated, or both.

I used to be this manager. Then I learned the single most important principle in my entire management career:

**If anything in a performance review is a surprise to the employee, the manager has failed.**

Not the employee. The manager. Because the review is not where information should be delivered. It's where information should be summarized.

## The Two Types of Managers at Review Time

I've seen this play out hundreds of times, across multiple companies, at every level. Managers divide neatly into two categories at review time.

**Manager A** has been running consistent weekly 1:1s. They take notes. They give feedback in real time. They discuss career goals regularly. They document wins and areas for development as they happen.

When review season arrives, Manager A opens their 1:1 notes from the past six months. They have 26 weeks of documented conversations. Specific examples of strong performance. Specific instances where feedback was given. A clear record of goals set and progress made. The review practically writes itself. It takes them maybe an hour per person.

**Manager B** has been busy. They've been canceling 1:1s, or running them as status updates with no notes. They haven't given meaningful feedback since the last review cycle. They have a vague impression of each person's performance based on whatever stands out in their memory (which, thanks to recency bias, is whatever happened in the last two weeks).

Manager B spends a full weekend writing reviews. Each one takes hours because they're working from a blank page. The reviews are generic, not because Manager B doesn't care, but because they literally don't have the data. They end up writing the same thing for three different people and hoping nobody compares notes.

I've been both of these managers. Being Manager A is better. For you, for your team, and for the quality of the review itself.

## Why Memory Is a Terrible Basis for a Performance Review

Let me explain why "I'll remember" is the biggest lie managers tell themselves.

You will not remember. Human memory is terrible at this. It's subject to recency bias (overweighting what happened recently), the halo effect (letting one strong impression color everything), availability bias (remembering what was emotionally charged rather than what was representative), and plain forgetting.

I had a team member who delivered the most important project of the quarter in month two. By month five, when reviews were due, I had almost forgotten about it. Not because it didn't matter. Because forty other things had happened since then, and my brain had moved on.

If I hadn't had notes from our 1:1s that specifically documented the project, its impact, and the skill the employee showed, that accomplishment would have been a footnote in the review instead of the centerpiece it deserved to be.

Notes are not bureaucracy. Notes are insurance against the limitations of your own brain.

## The 1:1 as a Performance Review Insurance Policy

Here's the system that changed everything for me.

Every week, in every 1:1, I capture three things:

1. **What's going well.** Specific work, specific behaviors, specific impact.
2. **What needs development.** Again, specific. Not "communication." But "struggled to articulate the tradeoffs in Tuesday's architecture review."
3. **Action items.** What did we agree they'd work on? What did I commit to?

This takes about five minutes of note-taking per 1:1. That's 130 minutes over a six-month review cycle, spread across 26 sessions. Less than two and a half hours total.

Now compare that to the 6-8 hours Manager B spends scrambling at review time, producing a worse result.

The math is obvious. A small investment every week yields a dramatically better outcome than a panic investment twice a year.

## What a Good Performance Review Actually Looks Like

A good performance review has five characteristics:

### 1. It Contains Specific Examples

Not "demonstrated strong leadership" but "led the migration project in Q2, coordinating across three teams and delivering two weeks ahead of schedule despite losing a team member mid-project."

Specific examples are credible. They tell the employee that you were paying attention. They make the review feel earned, not templated.

### 2. Nothing in It Is New Information

Every piece of feedback in the review should be something the employee has heard before. The wins should have been celebrated when they happened. The areas for development should have been discussed in 1:1s. The career direction should be a continuation of conversations you've been having all year.

If your employee reads their review and says "this is a good summary of where we've been," you've done your job. If they read it and say "this is the first I'm hearing of this," you've failed them.

### 3. It Connects Past Performance to Future Growth

A review isn't just a report card. It's a bridge between where someone has been and where they're going. "You've gotten significantly better at cross-team communication this cycle. Next cycle, I'd like to see you lead a project that requires executive-level communication, as a stretch toward the senior role."

This only works if you've been having career development conversations in your 1:1s all along.

### 4. It's Honest

I've read thousands of performance reviews, both as a manager, a skip-level, and as a Bar Raiser evaluating management candidates. The most common failure is dishonesty through omission.

The manager knows the person has a development area. But they don't write it down because they don't want the conversation. So the review is all sunshine, the employee thinks everything is great, and six months later nothing has changed.

If you care about someone's growth, you put the hard stuff in writing. You owe them that.

### 5. The Rating Matches the Words

This sounds obvious but it happens constantly: the narrative is full of praise and the rating is "meets expectations." Or the narrative includes significant development feedback and the rating is "exceeds expectations." The disconnect destroys credibility.

The words and the rating need to tell the same story. And if you've been giving consistent feedback all year, the employee should already know what rating is coming before they walk into the room.

## The Retrospective Rescue: What If You're Already Behind

Maybe you're reading this and thinking: "Great, but I haven't been doing any of this and reviews are in three weeks."

Here's your emergency plan.

**Step 1: Mine what you have.** Pull up Slack messages, emails, Jira tickets, code reviews, project postmortems. Look for concrete evidence of what each person did. It's not as good as 1:1 notes, but it's better than memory.

**Step 2: Talk to your people before you write.** Schedule a pre-review 1:1 with each person. Ask them to walk you through their top accomplishments, biggest challenges, and areas they want to develop. You're not outsourcing the review to them. You're filling in the gaps in your data. Most employees will be able to articulate their contributions far better than your memory can.

**Step 3: Be honest about the gap.** If you haven't given someone feedback all cycle and the review contains development areas, acknowledge it. "I should have raised this earlier. I didn't, and that's on me. I'm raising it now because I want to be honest about where I see room for growth." That's uncomfortable. It's also respectful.

**Step 4: Start the new system now.** Whatever you do this review cycle, start taking 1:1 notes this week. Six months from now, you'll be Manager A instead of Manager B.

## The Employee's Experience

Let me tell you what it feels like from the other side, because I think managers forget.

I once received a performance review that said I "needed to improve my strategic thinking." That was the entire feedback. No examples. No context. No prior conversation about it. I had no idea what it meant. Improve it how? In what situations? Was there a specific incident? I'll never know, because the manager couldn't tell me. They didn't have specifics. They had an impression.

I spent three months trying to figure out what that feedback meant. I changed nothing, because I couldn't. The feedback was too vague to be actionable.

Compare that to the best review I ever received. My manager walked through five specific projects from the prior six months. For each one, she described what I did well and where I could have been stronger. She referenced conversations we'd had about each project in our 1:1s. She connected my performance to the career goals we'd been discussing all year. The review took 30 minutes to discuss because we'd been having the conversation all along.

That review changed my trajectory. Not because of what it said, but because of what it proved: my manager was paying attention, cared about my growth, and had the receipts to back it up.

## The Math: 26 Sessions vs. Zero

Let me make the case as simply as I can.

A weekly 1:1 with good notes gives you 26 data points per review cycle per person. Each one captured in context, with specifics, close to the event.

No 1:1s give you zero data points and a lot of guessing.

With 26 data points, you can write a review that's specific, honest, and useful in under an hour.

With zero data points, you spend hours producing something generic and potentially inaccurate.

With 26 data points, the employee has already heard everything in the review. Nothing is a surprise.

With zero data points, the review is full of surprises. And surprises in performance reviews are never good ones.

The 1:1 is the insurance policy. It guarantees that when review season arrives, you have everything you need. The investment is small: 30 minutes a week per person, with five minutes of notes. The payoff is enormous: better reviews, more trust, and the elimination of the most dreaded week on every manager's calendar.

## The Standard

I'll leave you with a standard I hold myself to and that I think every manager should adopt.

Before you submit a performance review, ask yourself: "Has this person heard all of this before?"

If the answer is yes, submit it.

If the answer is no, you have work to do. Not on the review. On your management practice. The review is the symptom. The 1:1 is the cure.

Start now. Your next review cycle will thank you.
