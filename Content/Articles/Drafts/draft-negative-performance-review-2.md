## The Conversation You Owe Them

Here's the scenario. You have a mid-level project manager, Casey, who has struggled with stakeholder communication, missed deadlines, and gotten flagged by two cross-functional partners for lack of follow-through. You have a "needs improvement" rating ready to deliver tomorrow morning. And when you look back at your one-on-ones over the past six months, you gave hints. You made suggestions. You didn't say the thing you needed to say.

That's the real problem you're walking into the room with.

The rating itself is the easy part. The hard part is that Casey may not know how serious this is, and that's on you. How you handle the next 24 hours, and specifically that meeting tomorrow, will either course-correct the situation or make it worse.

Let me walk you through exactly how to do this.

## Own Your Part Before You Walk In

Before we talk about the conversation structure, you need to get clear on something. If Casey is surprised tomorrow morning, that surprise belongs to you. Not because you're a bad manager, but because directness is a skill and most of us learned to soften things to avoid discomfort, and that habit costs the people we manage.

You're going to acknowledge that in the meeting. Not as a lengthy confession, not as an apology tour, but as a direct statement of fact. Something like: "I want to be honest with you. I've addressed some of these issues in our one-on-ones, but I don't think I was as direct as I should have been. That's on me, and I want to correct that now."

That sentence matters for two reasons. First, it's true. Second, it gives Casey something to hold onto. Instead of feeling blindsided and confused, they hear that their manager is being straight with them, maybe for the first time in a while.

Do not skip this. Managers who skip this step usually do it because saying it out loud is uncomfortable. That discomfort is the point.

## Structure the Conversation, Don't Wing It

Walk in with a clear structure in your head. You don't need a script, but you need an arc.

Start by setting the tone without burying the lead. Tell Casey this is a review conversation and that you want to be direct with them. Don't spend five minutes warming up with small talk. That's not respect, it's avoidance. Get in.

Then deliver the rating clearly. "Casey, your rating this review period is 'needs improvement.'" Say it like that. Not "we had some challenges" or "things were a bit mixed this period." The actual words. Casey deserves to hear them without having to decode your meaning.

From there, move to the specifics. The missed internal deadlines, the stakeholder communication gaps, the two cross-functional partners who flagged follow-through issues. If you documented these during the review period, use your notes. If you didn't, use what you have. Concrete examples are the only thing that makes a performance conversation real. Without them, it's just an opinion.

Here's what you're not going to do: you're not going to sandwich the rating between compliments. Casey's effort and likability are real, and they matter, but leading with them before delivering bad news teaches people to tune out the bad news. Acknowledge effort and attitude after the rating lands, not before.

## When Casey Gets Emotional or Defensive

This is the part most managers fear, so let's address it directly.

Casey may push back. Casey may get quiet. Casey may cry. All of that is okay and none of it changes what you're there to do.

If Casey gets defensive and argues with specific examples, listen. Actually listen. If there's context you don't have, take it in. But don't let the conversation drift into a negotiation about whether the problems existed. If two cross-functional partners independently flagged the same issue, that's data. You can acknowledge Casey's perspective without reversing course on the assessment.

If Casey gets emotional, give them a moment. You don't need to fill every silence. "I can see this is hard to hear" is fine. Sitting quietly for ten seconds is also fine. What's not fine is pivoting away from the conversation to make Casey feel better in the short term. Your discomfort with their emotion is your problem to manage, not theirs.

The goal of this conversation is not for Casey to feel good at the end of it. The goal is for Casey to leave the room knowing exactly where they stand, exactly what needs to change, and exactly what support they have. That's a much better gift than a softened message that lets them walk out confused.

## The Improvement Plan Is Not Optional

A "needs improvement" rating without a specific path forward is just punishment. That's not what this is supposed to be.

Before the meeting, have a clear improvement plan ready. It should include specific behaviors that need to change, not general aspirations. Not "improve stakeholder communication" but "send project status updates to cross-functional partners every Friday by noon and confirm receipt on Slack." Measurable. Observable. Tied to a timeline.

Set a 30-day checkpoint. Tell Casey you're going to check in more frequently, not because you're watching them, but because you should have been doing this all along and you're correcting that now. That's also you owning your part again, this time through action.

Be clear about what success looks like at the 90-day mark. And be honest about what happens if things don't change. Not threatening, not dramatic, just honest. "If we get to 90 days and these patterns haven't shifted, we'll need to have a harder conversation about fit." Casey deserves to know that, because they might want to start thinking about their options, and that's their right.

## Separate the Person from the Performance

Casey works hard. Casey is well-liked. Casey's attitude isn't the problem. Say that. Not as a cushion before the bad news, but as a real, separate point after you've delivered the rating and gone through the specifics.

"I want to be clear that none of this is about your effort or how people feel about you. The issues are about execution and communication at the level this role requires. Those are things that can change."

That distinction matters. A performance problem is not a verdict on someone's worth. The best managers I've ever seen deliver hard news without collapsing the person. They hold both things at once: this didn't meet the bar, and I believe you can raise your game. Not as a manipulation tactic, but as a genuine belief backed by a real plan.

If you don't actually believe Casey can improve, that's a different conversation entirely, one about role fit or exit. But if you're delivering a "needs improvement" with an improvement path, mean it.

## After the Meeting

Send a written summary the same day. What you discussed, what the specific improvement areas are, what the plan is, and when the first checkpoint is. Not as a CYA exercise, though documentation does matter, but because people process difficult news slowly and Casey deserves something concrete to return to.

Then show up for the 30-day check-in. This is where most managers drop the ball after a hard review. They deliver the message, they feel relieved it's over, and then they go back to the same indirect feedback patterns that created this situation. Don't do that.

The review conversation is not the hard part. The hard part is the six months of consistent, direct management that follows it. That's what actually changes the outcome.
