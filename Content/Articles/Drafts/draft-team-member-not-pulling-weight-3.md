## The Problem Is Already Bigger Than Morgan

Someone on your team is underdelivering. You know it. Your best people know it. And the longer you wait to address it directly, the more it costs you, not just in missed deadlines, but in the trust of the people who are picking up the slack.

This is one of the most common situations managers face, and most of them handle it badly. They either avoid the conversation entirely and hope the situation self-corrects, or they walk into it loaded up on secondhand frustration and say something they regret. Neither approach solves the problem. Both make it worse.

Here's how to do it right.

## Start With What You Actually Know

Before you say a word to Morgan, get your facts straight. Not the team's facts. Yours.

Pull up the last two months of work. Write down the specific examples: the deadlines Morgan set and missed, the work items that required extra review cycles, the meetings where Morgan was absent without explanation. You are not building a case for prosecution. You are building a clear picture for a conversation. There's a difference.

The reason this matters is that Morgan is going to respond to your characterization of the problem. If your characterization is vague ("I've been hearing some concerns" or "it feels like things have slipped a bit"), you've given Morgan nothing concrete to respond to, and you've opened the door for a conversation that drifts into feelings rather than facts. If your characterization is specific ("In the last two months, you've missed four self-imposed deadlines, and three of your deliverables required a second review cycle"), the conversation stays anchored.

Also, be honest with yourself about one thing: how much of what you know comes from your own observation versus what the team has told you? The team's frustration is real and worth paying attention to, but hearsay is not evidence. Base the conversation on what you have personally observed.

## Have the Conversation, and Have It Soon

This is not a conversation to schedule for next week's 1:1. Book time this week, separate from the regular cadence, so Morgan knows this is a specific discussion and not a casual check-in.

When you sit down, name the gap clearly. Not cruelly, not apologetically. Just clearly.

Something like: "I want to talk about your work over the last couple of months because I've noticed a pattern that concerns me. Specifically, I've seen several deadlines slip, more revision cycles than I'd expect, and you've missed two team meetings without reaching out. I want to understand what's going on."

Then stop talking.

The goal of the first part of this conversation is not to deliver a verdict. It's to open a door. You genuinely do not know what's happening with Morgan. There could be something personal. There could be a skills gap that nobody caught. There could be unclear expectations you contributed to. There could be disengagement because Morgan feels overlooked or frustrated about something on the team. You don't know yet.

What you say specifically avoid: do not bring up what other team members have said. The moment you say "the team has noticed" or "someone told me they picked up three of your tasks," you've shifted the conversation from a manager-employee discussion to something that feels like an ambush. Morgan will spend the rest of the meeting trying to figure out who said what instead of actually engaging with you. Use what you observed. Leave the team out of it.

## Curiosity First, Consequences Second

Give Morgan room to explain. Ask questions that are genuinely open. "What's been going on?" is better than "Why are you missing deadlines?" The first invites honesty. The second puts someone on the defensive before they've had a chance to speak.

Listen to what comes back. If Morgan has something real going on, a health situation, something at home, a problem with a tool or a process, that changes what support looks like. It does not eliminate the need for improvement, but it changes the path to get there.

If Morgan says everything is fine, or minimizes the pattern, you hold the line. "I hear you, but the data I'm looking at tells a different story. I want to work through this together, and I need us to be honest with each other about where things stand."

After the conversation, regardless of what you learn, you need to land on specific and measurable expectations with a clear timeline. Not "let's see how the next few weeks go." More like: "For the next four weeks, I want to see deliverables hit on the dates you commit to, and I want you to flag to me before a deadline moves, not after. Let's check in on this specifically in our 1:1s."

Write it down. Share it with Morgan after the meeting. The documentation protects both of you.

## Handle the Team Separately

Your senior team member burned down some political capital to come to you with that feedback. That takes trust. You owe them an acknowledgment, even if you can't share specifics.

Something simple: "I heard you, and I'm taking it seriously. I can't get into details, but I want you to know I'm on it." That's it. You're not throwing Morgan under the bus, you're not making promises about outcomes, and you're not ignoring the person who came to you in good faith.

For the pointed standup comment, address it in the moment or immediately after. Something like: "Hey, let's keep standup focused on the project board itself. If there are blockers or process issues, bring them to me and we'll sort them out." You're not dismissing the frustration, you're redirecting it to a productive channel.

The team needs to see that you're managing the situation. They don't need the details. If they see you moving with purpose and not tolerating the status quo indefinitely, most of the resentment will quiet down on its own.

## If the First Conversation Doesn't Move the Needle

One conversation is not a plan. It's a start.

If you do the follow-up check-ins and the pattern continues, the next conversation is more direct. You name that you had an earlier discussion, you describe what you expected to change, and you describe what actually happened. You tell Morgan plainly that the current trajectory is not sustainable, and you bring in HR to put a formal performance plan in place if needed.

The formal plan is not a punishment. It's a documented process with clear expectations, check-in intervals, and explicit consequences. Some people turn around when it gets that real. Others don't. Either way, you now have a fair process behind you.

The kindest thing you can do for Morgan is to be clear early, before the situation deteriorates into something that ends badly for everyone. Vague feedback delivered too late is not kindness. It's avoidance dressed up as empathy.

## The Broader Point

When one person stops pulling their weight and you don't address it, you're making a decision about your standards whether you intend to or not. Your best people are watching what you tolerate. They will calibrate their own effort to the floor you set.

Morgan's performance is a management problem, which means it's your problem to solve. The team noticed it before you acted on it. That's a signal to move faster next time, but it's not too late now. Have the conversation this week, be specific, be fair, and follow through.

That's the job.
