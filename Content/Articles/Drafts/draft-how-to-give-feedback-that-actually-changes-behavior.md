# How to Give Feedback That Actually Changes Behavior

I once watched a manager deliver what he thought was devastating constructive feedback. He sat across from his direct report, cleared his throat, and said: "I think there's some room for improvement in how you communicate with stakeholders."

The employee nodded, said "totally, I agree," and walked out of the room having absorbed exactly zero useful information. Nothing changed. Three months later, the same issue came up in their performance review, and the employee was genuinely surprised.

That manager would tell you he gave feedback. He did not. He offered a vague observation wrapped in so much hedging language that the actual message never landed. And that pattern, which I see constantly, is why most feedback fails.

## Why Most Feedback Doesn't Work

Let me be direct about something. The problem with feedback in most organizations is not that managers give too much of it. It's that they give almost none of it, and the little they give is so diluted by fear and politeness that it might as well not exist.

There are three reasons for this.

**First, managers are afraid of the reaction.** They don't want to hurt someone's feelings. They don't want to create conflict. They don't want to be the bad guy. So they soften, hedge, hint, and hope the employee reads between the lines.

**Second, most feedback is delivered too late.** The event happened weeks or months ago. The employee can barely remember the specifics. The manager is working from a vague impression rather than a clear memory. The feedback becomes abstract, and abstract feedback is useless feedback.

**Third, most feedback is delivered without a relationship to support it.** When someone you trust and respect tells you something hard, you lean in. When someone you barely know or don't trust drops criticism on you, you get defensive. The relationship is the delivery mechanism. Without it, the message bounces off.

## The Difference Between Feedback and Criticism

Before we go further, let me draw a line that most managers miss entirely.

Feedback says: "I noticed something specific, and I'm telling you because I believe you can improve and I want to help." The orientation is forward. The intent is growth.

Criticism says: "You did something wrong and I need you to know it." The orientation is backward. The intent is judgment.

Employees can feel the difference immediately. They feel it in your tone, in your word choice, in whether you seem invested in them or frustrated with them. If the energy behind your feedback is "I need to fix this person," they will feel that. If the energy is "I want to develop this person," they will feel that too.

Feedback is a gift. I mean that literally. It's one of the most valuable things a manager can give, and most managers are too afraid to give it.

## The SBI Framework: Specific, Timely, Useful

I've tried a lot of feedback models over the years. The one I keep coming back to is SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact. Not because it's clever, but because it forces you to be specific. And specificity is what makes feedback work.

Here's how it works.

### Situation

Start by anchoring the feedback to a specific moment in time. Not "generally" or "sometimes" or "I've noticed a pattern." A specific event.

"In yesterday's design review..."
"During the client call on Tuesday..."
"In the Slack thread about the deployment this morning..."

This matters because it gives the employee something concrete to remember. It takes the conversation out of the realm of opinion and into the realm of observation.

### Behavior

Describe the specific behavior you observed. Not your interpretation of it. Not your assumption about why they did it. Just what happened.

"You interrupted Sarah three times during her presentation."
"You sent the proposal without running it by the team first."
"You raised your concern about the timeline after the decision had already been made."

Notice: no adjectives. No "you were aggressive" or "you were careless." Just the behavior. The moment you layer judgment onto the observation, the employee stops listening and starts defending.

### Impact

Explain the effect the behavior had. On the project, on the team, on you, on the person themselves.

"When you interrupted Sarah, she lost her train of thought and the team didn't get to hear her full analysis. I also noticed she didn't speak up again for the rest of the meeting."

"When the proposal went out without team review, we missed a pricing error that the client caught. It created extra work and dinged our credibility."

The impact is where the learning lives. Most people don't set out to cause harm. When you show them the connection between their behavior and its consequences, they get it. They can't unsee it.

## Real Examples of SBI in Action

Let me walk through a few scenarios to make this concrete.

### The Engineer Who Steamrolls in Meetings

**Bad feedback:** "You need to be more collaborative in meetings."

The engineer hears: "Something about meetings?" There's nothing to act on.

**Good feedback (SBI):** "In this morning's sprint planning, you jumped in to solve every problem that came up before anyone else had a chance to respond. By the end of the meeting, three people hadn't said a word. The impact is that we're not getting their perspectives, and they're starting to disengage from planning altogether."

The engineer hears: specific meeting, specific behavior, specific consequence. Now they know exactly what to do differently.

### The Designer Who Misses Deadlines

**Bad feedback:** "You need to work on your time management."

**Good feedback (SBI):** "The mockups for the Settings page were due last Friday and came in Tuesday. The impact was that engineering had three days with nothing to work on for that feature, and we had to push the sprint. I want to figure out what happened so we can prevent it next time."

Notice the last sentence. "I want to figure out what happened" signals partnership, not punishment. Maybe there's a workload issue. Maybe the scope was unclear. You don't know until you ask.

### The Product Manager Who Communicates Poorly with Eng

**Bad feedback:** "Engineers are frustrated with your specs."

**Good feedback (SBI):** "In the spec for the notification system, the success criteria section was blank and three of the acceptance criteria conflicted with each other. The engineers spent two days building something and then had to rewrite it when they realized the requirements were ambiguous. That cost us about 40 hours of engineering time."

Specific document. Specific problems. Specific cost. The PM can't dismiss this as a matter of opinion.

## When to Give Feedback

Timing matters as much as content. Here are the rules I follow.

**Give feedback as close to the event as possible.** Ideally within 24 hours. The further you get from the moment, the less the feedback means. By the time it shows up in a quarterly review, it's archaeology.

**Never give feedback when emotions are high.** Yours or theirs. If you're angry, wait. If they just came out of a stressful meeting, wait. Feedback delivered in emotional moments gets processed as an attack, no matter how well-crafted it is.

**Give feedback in private.** Always. No exceptions. Public feedback, even "positive" public feedback, changes the dynamic. Hard feedback in public is humiliation. Keep it in the 1:1.

**Make feedback a regular rhythm, not an event.** If you only give feedback when something goes wrong, employees learn to dread it. If feedback is part of every 1:1, both the hard and the encouraging kind, it becomes normal. It becomes how you operate together.

## The Relationship Prerequisite

Here's the part most feedback advice skips, and it's the most important part.

Feedback only lands when it's delivered inside a relationship of trust. If your employee doesn't believe you care about them, doesn't believe you're invested in their growth, doesn't believe you see their strengths along with their areas for development, then your feedback will bounce off every time.

This is why I insist that 1:1s are the foundation of everything. The 1:1 is where you build the relationship. The relationship is what makes the feedback land. You can't skip the first step and expect the second to work.

I had a team member once who was brilliant but abrasive. Sharp with colleagues, dismissive in code reviews, impatient in meetings. I needed to give him hard feedback about this pattern. But first, I spent three months building the relationship. I learned about his family, his career goals, his frustrations. I gave him consistent, specific positive feedback about his technical contributions. I showed up every week, on time, prepared, present.

When I finally had the hard conversation, he listened. Not because my feedback was perfectly crafted (it wasn't). But because he knew, from months of evidence, that I was on his side. He knew the feedback was coming from someone who wanted him to succeed, not someone who wanted to fix him.

He changed the behavior within a month. Not because I told him to. Because he trusted the source.

## Positive Feedback Matters Just as Much

Managers tend to think of feedback as corrective. Something to give when there's a problem. But positive feedback is just as important, and most managers are even worse at it.

"Great job" is not feedback. It's a pleasantry. It tells the employee nothing about what they did well or why it mattered.

Apply SBI to positive feedback too.

"In the client presentation yesterday, you anticipated every one of their objections and had data ready to address each one. The client's CTO told me afterward that it was the most prepared vendor pitch he'd seen this quarter. That preparation is exactly the kind of work that builds trust with enterprise accounts."

Now the employee knows specifically what worked and why it matters. They'll do it again. They'll do it more. They'll do it on purpose instead of by accident.

## What to Do When Feedback Doesn't Land

Sometimes you deliver good feedback and nothing changes. That's information, not failure.

When feedback doesn't land, ask yourself three questions:

1. **Was I specific enough?** If the person doesn't know exactly what to do differently, the feedback wasn't actionable.
2. **Is the relationship strong enough?** If trust is low, the message won't penetrate no matter how well it's crafted.
3. **Does the person agree that this matters?** Sometimes the disconnect isn't about understanding, it's about priority. They heard you. They just don't think it's important. That's a different conversation.

If you've given the same feedback three times with no change, it's time to escalate the conversation from feedback to expectation-setting. "I've brought this up three times. This isn't optional. Here's what I need to see by [date]." That's not feedback anymore. That's performance management. And the earlier you recognize when you've crossed that line, the better for everyone.

## The Cost of Withholding Feedback

I want to end with this, because it's the thing I wish someone had told me earlier in my career.

When you avoid giving feedback, you think you're being kind. You're not. You're being selfish. You're prioritizing your own comfort over your employee's growth.

The absence of feedback is its own message. It says: "I don't notice your work. I don't care enough to help you improve. I'm not willing to have an uncomfortable conversation for your benefit."

Employees who don't receive feedback don't feel protected. They feel invisible.

Every person on your team deserves to know where they stand. They deserve specifics. They deserve it in time to actually do something about it. And they deserve to hear it from someone who has earned the right to say it through consistent, invested, trustworthy leadership.

That's the standard. Everything else is just meetings.
