## Summary
Most feedback fails not because it's wrong but because it's vague, delayed, or delivered in a way that triggers defensiveness instead of change. This article covers what effective feedback actually looks like.

## Outline

### Introduction
Managers know they're supposed to give feedback. Most do it badly, or not at all.

### Why Most Feedback Fails
Too vague: "you need to communicate better"
Too late: saved for the annual review
Too personal: attacks the person, not the behavior
Too rare: people don't know where they stand
The fear that drives all of it

### What Feedback Is Actually For
Not punishment. Course correction.
The goal is a behavior change, not an acknowledgment of a problem.
Feedback that doesn't lead to change isn't feedback, it's commentary.

### The Anatomy of Effective Feedback
Specific: tied to an observable behavior or event
Timely: within 24-48 hours of the moment
Impact-focused: what effect did it have?
Forward-looking: what does good look like?

### The SBI Model
Situation: the context
Behavior: what you observed, not your interpretation
Impact: what happened as a result
What to do with it: a concrete path forward

### Positive Feedback Done Right
Most positive feedback is weak: "great job"
What makes it land: specific, tied to observable behavior, explained impact
Why the specificity matters for development, not just morale

### Delivering Hard Feedback
The mental prep: you're doing this for them, not to them
The setting: private, not in front of others
The opening: direct but not brutal
Managing the emotional reaction
What to do if they push back

### Feedback Conversations vs. Feedback Moments
Sometimes feedback is a brief observation. Sometimes it needs a real conversation.
How to tell the difference.
When to schedule time vs. when to say it in the moment.

### Building a Feedback Culture
What managers who give good feedback regularly do differently
Why the best teams have high-frequency, low-drama feedback
How to create safety for receiving feedback as a leader

### Feedback and the 1:1
Why the 1:1 is the natural home for ongoing feedback
How to integrate feedback into your weekly rhythm
The documentation that makes feedback development over time

### Conclusion
Feedback is the primary tool of development. Most managers are afraid of it. The managers who master it build the best teams.
