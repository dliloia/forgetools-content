## Hook
Most people think getting promoted is about doing your job really well.

It's about that, plus three other things.

## Body
Let's be honest about how promotions actually work.

Doing excellent work is the entry fee. It is necessary but not sufficient.

The people I've seen get promoted consistently do four things:

They make their work visible. Not in an annoying self-promotional way. In a "here's what we accomplished and why it mattered" way. They close the loop, they document results, they share wins with attribution. If no one knows what you did, it didn't happen for promotion purposes.

They operate at the next level before they have the title. The promotion is the recognition that you're already doing the job, not the permission to start. The people who wait for the title before taking on the scope are usually waiting a long time.

They have an advocate in the room. Promotions get decided in calibration meetings where managers fight for their people. If your manager doesn't know your story, doesn't believe in your next level, or isn't willing to go to bat for you, your work alone won't get you there.

They develop people and relationships, not just skills. As you move up, your ability to influence others, develop your team, and navigate stakeholders becomes increasingly important. The person who can do the job but can't bring anyone with them hits a ceiling.

## CTA / Closing
If you're a manager, your job is to develop the people who trust you.

That includes being honest about what promotion actually requires in your organization.

Are you having that conversation with your team?
