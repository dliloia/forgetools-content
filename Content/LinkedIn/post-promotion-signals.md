Doing your job well is the entry fee for promotion, not the whole ticket.

Let's be honest about how promotions actually work. Excellent work is necessary but not sufficient. The people I've seen get promoted consistently do four things, and most of them have nothing to do with working harder. They make their work visible, they operate at the next level before they have the title, they have an advocate in the room, and they develop people and relationships alongside their technical skills.

Making your work visible doesn't mean self-promotion in an obnoxious way. It means closing the loop, documenting results, and sharing wins with attribution. If no one knows what you did, it didn't happen for promotion purposes. Operating at the next level means the promotion is recognition that you're already doing the job, not permission to start. The people who wait for the title before taking on the scope are usually waiting a long time. I've seen this pattern at Amazon and everywhere else I've worked.

Having an advocate in the room is critical because promotions get decided in calibration meetings where managers fight for their people. If your manager doesn't know your story, doesn't believe in your next level, or isn't willing to go to bat for you, your work alone won't get you there. And as you move up, developing people and relationships becomes increasingly important. The person who can do the job but can't bring anyone with them hits a ceiling every time.

If you're a manager, your job is to be honest with your people about what promotion actually requires in your organization. Are you having that conversation with your team?
