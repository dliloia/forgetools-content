I became an Eagle Scout at 17 and I still lead by what it taught me.

"Be Prepared" is the most famous Scout motto, but the one that changed how I lead is less famous: leave it better than you found it. I got my Eagle rank by working with a team to build something lasting for a community I'd be leaving. That's leadership in its purest form. Build something that serves people even after you're gone. Twenty-five years later, that principle still drives every decision I make as a manager.

Preparation is the job, not just the preliminary. In Scouts, you don't wait for rain to check your tent. In management, you don't wait for the performance review to give feedback. The whole point of a 1:1 is to catch things early, when you can still do something about them. The managers I've seen fail most consistently are the ones who react instead of prepare. They let small problems become big ones because they weren't paying attention until the situation demanded it.

Serving because it matters, not because someone's watching, is what separates real leaders from people performing leadership. The best leaders I've known do the right thing when nobody's looking. They give the credit away. They take the blame. They show up for the team on hard days because that's the deal, not because it builds their personal brand. Recovery communities, volunteer organizations, and Scouting all teach this same lesson: service without transaction is the foundation of trust.

Leave every person better than you found them. Not every project, not every quarter. Every person. If someone worked for you for two years and their career didn't grow and their skills didn't sharpen, that's a leadership failure. You are responsible for what you leave behind. What's the most important thing you're building right now that will outlast you?
