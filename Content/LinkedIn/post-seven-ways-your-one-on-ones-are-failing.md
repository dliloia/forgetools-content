Most managers think they're running 1:1s.

They're not.

They're holding status meetings with a friendlier name. And the difference matters more than they realize.

After managing hundreds of people, I've seen the same seven failure modes destroy 1:1s over and over:

1. Turning them into status updates. If you could replace the meeting with a Slack message and lose nothing, it's not a 1:1.

2. Canceling when things get busy. Every cancellation sends the same message: something else matters more than you do.

3. Doing all the talking. The 1:1 is the employee's meeting. If you're talking for 25 of the 30 minutes, you're broadcasting, not listening.

4. Zero follow-through. When action items go nowhere, people stop raising issues. You've turned the 1:1 into a place where problems go to die.

5. Asking "how's everything going?" and accepting "good" as an answer. Generic questions get generic answers. Every time.

6. No structure at all. "Let's just chat" is not a management strategy.

7. Treating it as optional. "My door is always open" does not replace dedicated, protected time for real conversation.

Here's the part that stings: a manager running bad 1:1s for one year with a team of six wastes 300+ hours of combined time.

But the real cost is what never happens. The problems that don't surface. The trust that's never built. The resignation you never saw coming.

Good 1:1s are the highest-leverage 30 minutes a manager has each week.

Stop checking the box. Start doing the work.
