## Hook
The most dangerous employee on your team isn't the loud one.

It's the quiet high performer who's stopped telling you things.

## Body
Loud problems announce themselves. You deal with them.

Quiet problems compound in silence until the day they walk into your office and resign.

I've seen it a dozen times. The person who keeps delivering, stays professional, never complains, and seems fine. And then one day gives you two weeks notice. And when you ask why, you hear things you've never heard before:

They felt invisible.
They didn't see a path forward.
They had been thinking about leaving for six months.
They tried to have the conversation once but felt like you weren't really listening.

The hardest part isn't losing them. It's realizing the signals were there. You just weren't reading them.

Quiet high performers don't ask for much. They don't need a lot of management. So it's easy to take them for granted.

The check-in you skip with them feels fine because they're handling everything. Until they're not. Until they've already decided.

What would have changed things:
A real conversation about their career, not just their current project.
Asking "What are you not telling me that I should know?"
Noticing the smaller engagement, the shorter answers, the meetings they stopped contributing in.
Advocating for them before they had to ask.

## CTA / Closing
Your best people have options. They're only choosing you every day.

When did you last have a real conversation about what your quiet high performer actually wants?
