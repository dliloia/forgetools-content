A flawless track record should make you curious, not confident.

I've managed a lot of people over the years, and the ones who've never made a mistake, or never admitted to one, almost always fall into one of three categories: they haven't been tested hard enough, they're coasting to avoid failure rather than achieve something, or they're hiding. That third one is where things get expensive.

The hiding problem compounds quietly. A small mistake disclosed on day one costs you an afternoon. The same mistake concealed for two weeks costs you far more, and now you have a trust problem sitting on top of the original issue.

If you're a manager and your culture makes mistakes feel dangerous to admit, you're not running a high-performing team. You're running a team that's very good at concealment. The fix isn't celebrating failure, it's making disclosure and ownership the behavior that actually gets rewarded. The fastest way to start is to go first.
