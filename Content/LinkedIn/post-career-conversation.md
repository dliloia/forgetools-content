Most managers never ask their people where they want to go.

Not explicitly. Not with real curiosity about the answer. Not in a way that invites honesty. They're afraid of what they'll hear. "I want your job." That feels threatening. "I want to leave this department." That's uncomfortable. "I don't know." That's awkward. "I want to be promoted in 6 months." That feels impossible to respond to. So they don't ask. They manage the work instead of the person, and the employee eventually concludes that their manager doesn't care about their future.

Even if that's not true, the absence of the conversation IS the message. I've watched talented people leave organizations not because they were unhappy with the work, but because nobody ever asked them what they actually wanted. Nobody invested ten minutes in understanding their ambitions. That's a retention failure that has nothing to do with compensation or company culture. It's a manager who skipped the most important part of the job.

Here's what I've learned over 20+ years of managing people. The managers who ask about career goals, and then actually help, keep their people longer, get more discretionary effort, and build the kind of loyalty that doesn't show up in engagement surveys. You don't have to have all the answers. You just have to care enough to ask. "Where do you want to be in 2 years?" "What kind of work energizes you most?" "What's the one thing I could do to help your career right now?" That's it. Ask. Listen. Act on what you hear.

The 1:1 growth segment, the last 10 minutes, is where this conversation lives. When did you last ask your people where they want to go?
