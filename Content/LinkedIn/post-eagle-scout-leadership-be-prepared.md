Most managers wait for problems to find them.

The best managers have already solved those problems before they arrive.

I earned my Eagle Scout rank at sixteen. Didn't think much about it for twenty years. Then I became a manager, started interviewing hundreds of people, built teams, watched careers flourish and crash.

Suddenly those three years in the woods started making a lot of sense.

Eagle Scouts operate by three principles. And every elite manager I've ever worked with lives by the same three, whether they ever wore the uniform or not.

**1. Be prepared.**

Not "have a plan." Think through what could go wrong before it does.

I kept a running document on every person I managed. Notes from 1:1s. Development goals. Feedback given and what happened after. When review season came, I wasn't guessing. I already knew.

Prepared managers document. Not because they're paranoid. Because memory is unreliable and decisions should be based on facts.

**2. Serve because it matters.**

In interviews, I can tell the difference between a title-chasing manager and a people-first manager in about ten minutes.

The title-chasers talk about their accomplishments. The good ones talk about their people. "She was ready for a stretch assignment and I pushed hard to get her one." "He was struggling with communication, so we worked on it every week until he had it."

Serving your people also means telling them the truth. Avoiding hard feedback isn't kindness. It's cowardice dressed up as consideration.

**3. Leave things better than you found them.**

Every quarter, your team should be a little stronger than it was. Your documentation cleaner. Your processes smoother.

If you left tomorrow, would your team be okay? Is someone ready to step up? Is the work documented?

Most managers can't say yes to all of that. That's not a judgment. It's a starting point.

Here's the check I run, and teach others to run:

Am I prepared for the problems that haven't arrived yet?
Am I serving my people, or serving my own comfort?
Is my team better than it was ninety days ago?

Simple questions. Hard to consistently answer yes.

The managers who do? They stand out in every room they walk into.
