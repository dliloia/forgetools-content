"I don't know" is almost never true.

It's a stall. Sometimes deference, sometimes fear of being wrong. But rarely an accurate report that there's nothing going on in their head.

The problem is what managers do next: they fill the silence. They give a suggestion, offer a direction, reframe the problem. It feels helpful. It is, in the narrowest sense. It's also teaching your team that they don't have to develop a point of view because you'll always supply one.

Over a year, that pattern looks like a team with no initiative and a manager buried in meetings they shouldn't be in.

The fix is uncomfortable and surprisingly simple. Next time someone says "I don't know," say "just give me one idea" and then wait. Fifteen seconds. Most people will find something, often the actual answer, because they knew all along. They just weren't ready to commit to it.

I wrote out the full mechanics of how to do this, when to hold the silence, when to offer a foothold instead, and when to just tell them the answer outright.
