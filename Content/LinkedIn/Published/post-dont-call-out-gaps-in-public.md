---
title: "Don't Call Out Gaps in Public"
date: 2026-06-08

---

Calling someone out in a group isn't feedback. It's a demonstration.

The problem isn't that the correction is wrong or that the standard is too high. The problem is that everyone in the room learns something you didn't intend to teach. They learn what happens when they fall short in your presence, and that lesson shapes how much they hide from you going forward.

Public callouts feel efficient. One conversation, whole team hears it, done. But you're trading a short-term correction for a long-term erosion of the trust that makes feedback actually work. The person you corrected will seem fine. The gap might even close temporarily. What you won't see is that they've started optimizing for not getting caught rather than for getting better.

The private conversation is harder to prepare, harder to structure, and takes more of your actual time. It's also the only version where someone can ask a question without looking weak, admit they're struggling without it entering the team's social memory, and genuinely hear you. That's not the soft path. It's the more demanding one.

If the whole group needs to hear something, address the issue, not the person. You can do both. They're just different conversations with different objectives.
