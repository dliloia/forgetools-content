Empathy didn't make me a nicer manager. It made me less wrong.

When someone's performance changes, that change is data. But you don't know what it's data about yet. The managers who skip straight to evaluation are making decisions on incomplete information, and most of them don't realize it because their teams have learned to hand them a cleaned-up version of reality.

That information gap is where real organizational costs live. Delayed problem escalation, avoidable turnover, decisions made on filtered signals. Empathy closes that gap because people tell you more when they believe you're trying to understand their situation rather than judge it.

The version that doesn't work is empathy without accountability. Excusing missed deadlines because someone's going through a hard time isn't empathy. It's conflict avoidance, and your high performers will notice before you do.

The version that works is being accurate. Accurate about context, accurate about what's actually driving performance, accurate about what the conversation needs to be.
