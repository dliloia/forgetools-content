Saving feedback for Friday turns a conversation into a verdict.

Most managers I've worked with aren't doing this out of laziness. They're doing it because they genuinely believe careful and delayed is more responsible than immediate and imperfect. The problem is that by Friday, the context has evaporated. What you deliver feels like criticism about something your direct report barely remembers, and the learning that could have happened on Tuesday never does.

The skill that makes real-time feedback work is separating observation from judgment. "You interrupted the client twice and they went quiet after that" is something a person can work with. "You were rude to that client" needs a lot more runway. Lead with what you saw, keep it short, and ask if they have context you're missing. That's most of what real-time feedback actually requires.

The 1:1 still matters, just not as the first delivery. Use it to follow up on conversations you've already had, not to open ones you've been storing. That shift alone changes the whole tone of the meeting.
