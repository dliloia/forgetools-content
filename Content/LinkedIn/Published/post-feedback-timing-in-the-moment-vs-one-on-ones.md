Feedback doesn't expire on a schedule. It expires the moment the context fades.

I spent years watching managers treat their 1:1s like a feedback inbox, holding observations all week to "give them proper attention." What actually happens is the behavior repeats, the person has no idea, and by Thursday the manager has turned a thirty-second note into a narrative they've been building in their head for five days.

The real-time stuff doesn't need to be a big conversation. Brief, specific, while the moment is still alive. That's the whole job. "Hey, I noticed you cut Sarah off a few times in there, worth being aware of." Thirty seconds. Done.

The 1:1 is the right home for patterns, trends, and anything sensitive. It's not the right home for everything just because it feels safer to wait.

Managers who give feedback in the moment tend to earn more trust, not less. When people only hear from you in scheduled meetings, they start wondering what you're not saying. Candor in real time removes that uncertainty, and teams that trust their manager's candor move faster and recover from mistakes faster.
