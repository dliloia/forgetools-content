The vendor who nails the demo almost never nails the relationship.

I've watched teams sign contracts based on a polished pitch and a live demo that somehow worked perfectly, then find themselves six months later wondering what happened. The vendor knew their own strengths better than the team knew their own requirements. That's the gap that gets expensive.

The fix isn't complicated, but it requires doing uncomfortable work before you talk to anyone. Document your actual requirements first. Weight them honestly. Lock a scoring rubric before you see a single proposal. The discipline of doing that work upfront is what keeps the slickest pitch in the room from walking away with the contract.

One thing most evaluations skip entirely: the three-year cost model. First-year pricing is a marketing tool. The real picture shows up in year two renewal terms, implementation overages, and support costs that weren't in the original number.

The vendor you select is a working relationship for years, not an afternoon presentation. Those require different evaluation criteria.
