Most managers learn to give feedback down. Almost nobody learns to give it up.

The fear is legitimate. There's a real power dynamic, and the risk feels asymmetric in a way that's hard to dismiss. But the cost of staying quiet is almost always higher than people account for. Teams that can't tell the truth to senior leaders end up operating on incomplete information, and the leaders themselves often don't understand why they keep getting blindsided.

The part that doesn't get said enough: doing this well is actually a credibility accelerator. Leaders worth working for recognize when someone delivers honest information without ego or agenda. That's rare, and it gets remembered.

The key is directness with context, not courage followed by rambling. State the observation, name the evidence, separate the behavior from the intent. Skip the hedges. If you've built enough baseline trust and the thing actually matters, say it plainly and let them sit with it.
