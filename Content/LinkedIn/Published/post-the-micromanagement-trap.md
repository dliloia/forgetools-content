Most micromanagers think they're just thorough managers with high standards.

I've been in management for over three decades. I've never once had a manager describe themselves as a micromanager. What I hear instead is "I'm very hands-on" or "I like to stay close to the work." The behavior is identical. The self-perception is completely different. That gap is exactly how good managers lose their best people without ever seeing it coming.

Here's what nobody talks about clearly enough: micromanagement isn't a personality flaw. It's a stress response. The most telling sign you've crossed the line isn't something you feel. It's something your team does. When capable people stop making decisions without your sign-off, you've trained autonomy out of them. That's not thoroughness. That's a team that has learned to be helpless.

The fix isn't willpower. The article explains what it actually takes.
