Most managers develop their best people in private. Then they wonder why leadership doesn't see what they see.

I've been in thousands of calibration meetings over 33 years of management. I can tell you with certainty: if your high-potential person is unknown to the senior leaders in that room, they are already at a disadvantage before a single word is spoken about them. Visibility isn't a nice-to-have. It's a strategy you have to execute deliberately, months before any opportunity opens up. Bringing your person into a skip-level meeting, having them present team results instead of you, sending them to represent the team in a cross-functional forum. These are not gestures. They are investments.

The other thing most managers skip is the honest promotion conversation. Not the vague "you're doing great, keep it up" version. The real one: here's exactly what ready looks like from where I sit, here are the two or three things I need to see from you, and here's what I'm doing to advocate for you in the rooms you're not in. People can work with clarity. They cannot work with reassurance that turns out to mean nothing when the promotion cycle comes around and they're passed over.

I've also watched what happens when managers don't do this development work. A strong senior IC has been quietly exceeding their role for 18 months. A manager-level opening comes up. Leadership hires externally because, in their view, there's no obvious internal candidate. The senior IC leaves within six months. Two other strong performers follow. The external hire spends a year rebuilding a demoralized team. That failure was entirely preventable. Not by luck, but by doing the intentional development work that makes an internal candidate undeniable.

The signals I look for early are pretty simple. In 1:1s, I'm watching for three things: do they bring problems with context and a point of view, not just the complaint? How do they talk about teammates when things go wrong? Do they ask questions about the business that go beyond their own role? Those patterns, when you see them consistently, are telling you something. Flag those people early. Start talking about them to your peers and your own manager. Make them visible before they need to be.

The stretch assignment piece is where a lot of managers get it wrong. A stretch assignment isn't a side project with low stakes. The risk has to be real enough that success actually means something. I once had a senior IC who was technically excellent but folded under pressure from leadership whenever someone challenged her recommendation. I gave her a build-vs-buy decision to present and defend to a VP in a 45-minute working session. We prepped hard for two weeks. She ran it herself. She held her position under pressure. She was promoted four months later. That's what a real stretch looks like.

You don't need a formal program to start. You need one person you believe in, one honest conversation about what the next level requires, and one real stretch assignment in the next 90 days. The managers who build the best teams aren't the ones who hire the best. They're the ones who grow people relentlessly and advocate for them loudly. That reputation compounds over time. Top performers seek those managers out. That's the real return on this work.
