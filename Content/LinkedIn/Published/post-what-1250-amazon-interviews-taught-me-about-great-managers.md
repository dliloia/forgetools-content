After 1,250 Amazon Bar Raiser interviews, one pattern separated great managers from everyone else.

It wasn't intelligence. It wasn't how polished their answers were. It wasn't even how long they'd been managing people. The thing that separated genuinely great managers from managers-in-title was one specific quality that showed up the same way every single time: depth of ownership. Not claimed ownership, where someone says "I own outcomes on my team" as a positioning statement. Actual demonstrated ownership, where they could tell me precisely what they did, precisely what they got wrong, and precisely what they would change.

The tell I watched for most closely was how candidates used the phrase "my team." Some candidates said "my team did X" for almost every answer, and when I'd probe with "what was your specific contribution?" the answers would dissolve into vague talk about setting vision and creating alignment. That's real work, but if that's all you can point to, you're describing management as a status rather than a practice.

The great managers were different. They could tell me the exact conversation they had with a direct report that changed the trajectory of a project. They remembered the actual numbers, the actual timeline, the actual moment where a decision they personally made shifted the outcome. They could also tell me where they were wrong, with the same specificity, and what they'd do differently. That combination of precision and accountability was the single most consistent signal I saw across the full 1,250.

The other thing I watched for was how candidates handled Amazon's Leadership Principles. A lot of people prepare for those interviews by assembling a story portfolio. And honestly, that preparation helps. But what I was really measuring wasn't whether someone had a story for each principle. It was whether their thinking during the interview naturally reflected those principles. When someone has genuinely internalized "Dive Deep," they don't just tell you a story about diving deep. They dive deep in the interview room itself. The details are real. The numbers are real. And the story doesn't end at the prepared version. They can keep going, because it actually happened to them.

The principle I found most predictive of management quality was "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit." It requires two things at once: the courage to hold a position under pressure, and the discipline to fully execute on a decision after you've lost the argument. That combination is genuinely rare. Most people can do one or the other. The managers who could do both were almost always the ones I'd want running a team.

Here's what I'd suggest you do with this. Think about the last significant initiative you led and answer these questions out loud, as if I'm interviewing you: What was the specific outcome in numbers? What decision did you personally make that most affected it? What did you get wrong? Who on your team grew as a direct result of working with you, and how do you know? If your answers feel specific and honest, you're operating like a real manager. If they feel thin, that's not a crisis. It's just a signal that tells you where to pay attention next.
