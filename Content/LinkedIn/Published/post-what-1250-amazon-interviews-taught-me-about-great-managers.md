After 1,250 Amazon Bar Raiser interviews, one pattern separated great managers from everyone else.

It wasn't intelligence. It wasn't polished answers. The thing that separated genuinely great managers from managers-in-title was depth of ownership. Not claimed ownership. Demonstrated ownership: they could tell me precisely what they did, precisely what they got wrong, and precisely what they'd change.

The tell I watched for most closely was how candidates used the phrase "my team." Some said "my team did X" for every answer. When I probed with "what was your specific contribution?" the answers dissolved into vague talk about vision and alignment. Great managers could tell me the exact conversation, the actual numbers, the precise moment where a decision they personally made shifted the outcome.

One Leadership Principle turned out to be the most predictive of management quality. The article explains which one and why.
