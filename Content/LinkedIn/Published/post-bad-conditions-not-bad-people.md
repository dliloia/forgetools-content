Most struggling teams aren't a talent problem. They're a conditions problem.

I've made the mistake of looking at the wrong thing. A team underperforms, and the instinct is to look at the people. But the harder, more useful question is what the environment was actually making possible for them. Slow output looks like disengagement until you find out someone is manually copying data across three systems that were never integrated.

The reason managers default to blaming people is partly speed, partly ego protection. Fixing a process takes longer and requires admitting some of the failure belongs to you. Managing someone out feels like decisive action. It's cleaner, even when it's wrong.

When you fix the actual constraints, something interesting happens. People who seemed checked out start performing. Not because they changed, but because the friction that was grinding them down is finally gone. You end up with the team you thought you didn't have.

The conditions audit doesn't have to be complicated. Sit with your people and ask what slows them down. The answers will surprise you.
