Most managers think psychological safety is built in team meetings and all-hands.

It isn't.

It's built in a single moment when someone brings you bad news and you decide how to respond.

Here's what actually happens:

Your engineer tells you the timeline you promised leadership is wrong. Your first instinct is defensiveness.

What you do in the next 60 seconds tells every person on your team whether speaking up is safe or not.

If you get defensive, dismiss the concern, or go quiet, people learn something. They learn that honest information gets punished.

If you say "you're right, I missed that, let's fix it," people learn something very different.

Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety was the #1 predictor of team performance. Not talent. Not resources. Not compensation.

Safety.

Teams without it hide problems, self-censor, and let small fires become disasters.

Teams with it surface issues early, take more risks, and keep their best people longer.

So here's what actually builds it:

React well to bad news. Every. Single. Time. Your reaction trains your team on what's safe to tell you.

Admit when you're wrong. Specifically. "I was wrong about that estimate and here's what I missed." Not vague. Not hedged.

Ask uncomfortable questions in your 1:1s. "What are you hesitant to bring up?" "What am I missing right now?" The questions that feel slightly awkward are the right ones.

And here's what destroys it just as fast:

Correcting people publicly. Punishing the messenger. Asking for input and then never acknowledging it.

You don't create safety with a policy or a poster or an offsite exercise.

You create it with a track record.

The manager who says "my door is always open" and the manager who actually creates safety are often two completely different people.

One is available. The other has earned it.
