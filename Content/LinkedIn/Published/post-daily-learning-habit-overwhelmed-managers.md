Most managers aren't too busy to learn. They're just using the wrong unit of time.

The idea that learning requires a dedicated block, quiet, uninterrupted, an hour minimum, is what keeps most managers stuck. Real learning doesn't need an hour. It needs about ten minutes, structured the right way. One question you're genuinely trying to answer, one source going deep on it, one sentence about what you actually think, and one person you tell about it.

That last part matters more than people expect. If you can't explain something simply to another person, you don't have it yet. Teaching is how you find out what's actually stuck.

The other piece is identity. "I'm trying to learn more" is a goal, and goals evaporate when things get hard. "I'm someone who reads every morning" is harder to abandon. The managers who keep growing have made learning part of how they operate, not a side project they're hoping to get to.

The calendar doesn't clear. The overwhelm doesn't magically lift. But the habit can live inside the noise you're already navigating, if you scale it down to something that actually fits.
