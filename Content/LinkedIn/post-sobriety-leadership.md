I've been sober since 2012, and it's the most important leadership development I've ever done.

Nobody talks about this in business. Recovery isn't on anyone's resume. It doesn't show up in 360 reviews or LinkedIn endorsements. You don't take a workshop on it. But recovery teaches you things about leading people that you can't learn anywhere else, and I've come to believe that the skills it builds are more valuable than anything I picked up in a corporate training program.

Radical honesty is the foundation. Recovery demands it. You can't stay sober by telling yourself stories about why the problem isn't yours. You have to look at yourself clearly, name what's true, and be honest with the people around you. That habit doesn't go away when you lead a team. It makes you the kind of manager who tells people the truth even when it's uncomfortable, because you've learned that dishonesty always costs more in the long run. Asking for help is another one. Every person in recovery has had to ask for help from other people who've been there. That's the whole model. I learned to stop pretending I had all the answers and start getting curious about what other people knew that I didn't.

The daily commitment changed how I think about consistency. You don't get sober once. You get sober today, and then tomorrow, and then the day after. Long-term outcomes are built from small, consistent daily choices. That's exactly how you build a team. And serving people you don't have to serve is where recovery and leadership overlap most clearly. The recovery community runs on people showing up for each other with no transaction involved. I watched people give their time, their stories, and their support to total strangers. That's what leadership without ego looks like.

I don't share this to be vulnerable for vulnerability's sake. I share it because the most important leadership training I've ever had wasn't in a boardroom. What's shaped your leadership in ways that don't show up on a resume?
