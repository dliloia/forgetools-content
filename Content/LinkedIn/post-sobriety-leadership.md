## Hook
I've been sober since 2012.

That's 13+ years of the most important leadership development I've ever done.

## Body
Nobody talks about this in business.

Recovery isn't on anyone's resume. It doesn't show up in 360 reviews or LinkedIn endorsements. You don't take a workshop on it.

But recovery teaches you things about leading people that you can't learn anywhere else.

Here's what sobriety gave me:

Radical honesty. Recovery demands it. You can't stay sober by telling yourself stories about why the problem isn't yours. You have to look at yourself clearly, name what's true, and be honest with the people around you. That habit doesn't go away when you lead a team.

Asking for help is strength. Every person in recovery has had to ask for help from other people who've been there. That's the whole model. I learned to stop pretending I had all the answers and start getting curious about what other people knew that I didn't.

The daily commitment. You don't get sober once. You get sober today, and then tomorrow, and then the day after. Long-term outcomes are built from small, consistent daily choices. Same with building a team.

Serving people you don't have to serve. The recovery community runs on people showing up for each other with no transaction involved. I watched people give their time, their stories, their support to total strangers. That's what leadership without ego looks like.

## CTA / Closing
I don't share this to be vulnerable for vulnerability's sake.

I share it because the most important leadership training I've ever had wasn't in a boardroom.

What's shaped your leadership in ways that don't show up on a resume?
