The system doesn't develop you. Your manager does.

Five years inside Amazon taught me that lesson over and over. The company is famous for its intensity, and that reputation is earned. The bar is genuinely high, the expectations are real, and most people don't make it two years. I was there five, and what I noticed watching people thrive and watching people flame out had very little to do with intelligence or work ethic. It had almost everything to do with their manager.

The people who thrived had managers who gave them real feedback, not just performance ratings. Their managers advocated for them in rooms they weren't in. They invested in their people's growth even when it was inconvenient, and they were honest about what "good" looked like at Amazon specifically. These weren't superhuman leaders. They were people who understood that the job was about developing the humans on their team, not just delivering results through them.

The people who burned out had a different experience entirely. Their managers led by fear, gave feedback only in reviews, took credit for their team's work, and were often too busy surviving themselves to develop anyone else. Same company. Same system. Same leadership principles on the wall. Completely different outcomes, because the variable was always the manager.

I became a Bar Raiser partly because I wanted to make sure we were hiring managers who actually knew how to develop people, not just execute projects. That conviction has only gotten stronger since I left. What's your experience been?
