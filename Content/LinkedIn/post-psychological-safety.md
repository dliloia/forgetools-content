## Hook
Psychological safety isn't about making people feel good.

It's about making your team effective.

## Body
Google studied 180 teams for two years trying to understand what made the best ones work.

The single biggest predictor of team performance wasn't individual talent, compensation, or manager quality.

It was psychological safety: the belief that you can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and offer ideas without being punished or humiliated.

That's not a soft outcome. That's a performance driver.

Here's what psychological safety actually looks like in practice:

People disagree in meetings. Not performatively. Actually. Because they know the idea gets challenged, not the person.

Someone admits they don't know something and asks for help, and nothing bad happens.

A mistake gets surfaced early so the team can fix it, instead of buried until it becomes a crisis.

Someone on the team has an idea that challenges how things have always been done, and they say it out loud, and people engage with it.

And here's what kills it: A manager who reacts badly to bad news. Who criticizes in front of others. Who says they want honesty but punishes it when it arrives. Who rewards the people who agree with them and sidelines the ones who push back.

You don't get psychological safety by announcing you value it.

You build it through thousands of small moments where you respond well when things are hard.

## CTA / Closing
Your team is watching how you handle the moments that don't go your way.

Those moments are where trust gets built or broken.
