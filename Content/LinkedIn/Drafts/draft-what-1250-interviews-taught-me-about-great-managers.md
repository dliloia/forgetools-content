I've interviewed over 1,250 people as an Amazon Bar Raiser.

One pattern showed up in every great manager I evaluated. Every single one.

They talked about their people first.

Not the project. Not the metrics. Not the strategy. Their people. Who they developed, who they coached through something hard, who they helped get promoted. The work was always framed through the lens of the humans doing it.

The mediocre managers? They talked about themselves. What they built. What they decided. What they delivered. Their team was a footnote.

**Here's what the Bar Raiser role really teaches you:**

Companies say they want great people leaders. What they actually hire for is execution. The candidate who says "I drove a $10M initiative" beats the one who says "I developed three people into senior engineers" almost every time.

That's a systemic problem. But it doesn't change what actually works.

**The managers whose teams performed at the highest level, stayed the longest, and produced the most future leaders had one thing in common:** they saw their job as developing people, not delivering projects. The projects got delivered because the people were developed.

I also noticed something about self-awareness. The best managers could tell you exactly what they'd do differently. They owned their mistakes without being prompted. The ones who said "I wouldn't change anything" were almost always the weakest.

After 1,250 interviews, I can tell you this with certainty: the bar for management in most companies is shockingly low. If you genuinely invest in your people, you're already in the top 10%.

That's not inspiring. That's a call to action.

What's the one quality you think separates good managers from great ones?
