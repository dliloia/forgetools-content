A Boundary Is for You. An Ultimatum Is for Them.

A young coworker asked me recently how to set boundaries at work without it blowing up. The honest answer is that most people are not actually setting boundaries when they think they are, they are issuing ultimatums, and ultimatums almost always blow up.

A boundary describes what you will do. If someone interrupts your meeting, you say "I am in a meeting right now, can I come find you in 15 minutes?" That holds whether the other person likes it or not, because the action is yours. An ultimatum, by contrast, describes what the other person must do, with a threat behind your back if they don't. The room reads it as aggression, the other person gets defensive, and the original problem rarely gets solved.

The reason this matters: when you state a boundary calmly, most people adjust, not because they are obeying a demand, but because they now have new information about what working with you looks like. And when they don't adjust, the boundary still works, because you said what you would do and then you did it. The situation resolves on your terms.

Sometimes people get annoyed anyway. That is their problem, not yours.
