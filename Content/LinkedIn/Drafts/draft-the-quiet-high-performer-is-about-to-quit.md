Your best employee hasn't complained in months, and that's not a good sign.

Every team has one. The person who delivers consistently, never causes drama, never asks for anything. They make your job easier, so you leave them alone. You spend your energy on the underperformer, the squeaky wheel, the person threatening to quit. Meanwhile, your quiet high performer is already interviewing. I've seen this play out dozens of times. By the time they tell you they're leaving, the decision was made weeks ago. There's no counteroffer that fixes it because the relationship gap is too wide.

Here's what quiet high performers actually need. They need to know you see them, not in an all-hands shoutout but in a 1:1 where you say "I notice the work you're doing on X, and it matters." They need to be asked what they want, not assumed. "Where do you want to be in two years? What kind of work energizes you? What would make you leave?" Those questions feel direct, and they should be. They also need stretch, not just stability. High performers who only get reliable work eventually get bored, and boredom is the quiet killer of retention.

The uncomfortable truth is that managers are conditioned to manage problems. We run toward the fire. But the person who never starts a fire still needs investment. They just won't demand it. One genuine conversation every two weeks can prevent losing someone irreplaceable. It doesn't take a grand gesture or a new title. It takes attention, curiosity, and the willingness to invest in someone who isn't making noise.

If you have a high performer you haven't really checked in with lately, today is the day. Not next week. Today. Who's the quiet high performer on your team right now?
