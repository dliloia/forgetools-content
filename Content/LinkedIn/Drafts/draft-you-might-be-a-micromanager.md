No one thinks they're a micromanager.

They think they're thorough, detail-oriented, high standards. "I just care about quality." I've managed through high-pressure product launches, tight deadlines, and million-dollar stakes, and I'll be honest: I've felt the pull toward micromanagement in every single one of them. The instinct to control more when the stakes go up is real and it's human. But here's what I've learned: the urge to micromanage is almost always a trust problem in disguise.

You don't hover because the work requires it. You hover because you don't have enough visibility into what's happening. And you don't have visibility because you haven't built the communication systems that give it to you. This is where consistent 1:1s change everything. When you talk to each person on your team every week, really talk, you know what they're working on, where they're stuck, what's at risk, and how they're feeling. You don't need to check in on every task because the relationship already gives you the information.

Here are the signs you might be sliding into micromanagement. You review work before it's done, not after. You give instructions on HOW to do things, not just WHAT needs to happen. Your team asks permission for things they should own. People stop bringing you ideas because they know you'll rewrite them. If any of these sound familiar, the antidote isn't "just trust your team," because that's empty advice. The antidote is building real visibility through real conversations. Trust calibrated to capability. Give autonomy where it's earned. Coach where it's needed. And check your own stress level, because pressure is the number one trigger that turns good managers into controlling ones.

The best managers I know have high standards AND high trust. Those two things aren't in conflict. They reinforce each other. Have you ever caught yourself micromanaging, and what triggered it?
