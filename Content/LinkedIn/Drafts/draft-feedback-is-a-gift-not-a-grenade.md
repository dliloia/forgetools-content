If your employees only hear feedback during performance reviews, you've already failed them.

That's not feedback, that's an ambush. I've given thousands of pieces of feedback across my career, and the ones that actually changed behavior had three things in common: they were specific, they were timely, and they came from someone the employee trusted. Miss any one of those three, and the feedback either bounces off or does more harm than good.

Specific means you don't say "you need to communicate better." You say "In yesterday's meeting, when you cut off Sarah mid-sentence, it shut down the conversation. The team stopped contributing after that." Timely means this week, not six months from now. Feedback has a half-life, and the longer you wait, the less it means. If you're saving feedback for the annual review, you're not giving feedback. You're building a case. Trusted means you've invested in the relationship before you needed to spend from it. You can't skip straight to hard feedback with someone who doesn't believe you're in their corner. Trust first, then truth.

Here's what most managers get wrong. They think feedback is about courage, that they need to be brave enough to say the hard thing. It's not about courage. Feedback is about caring enough to say it. The absence of feedback sends its own message. It tells your employee that you either don't notice their work, don't care about their growth, or don't trust them enough to be honest. None of those are messages you want to send.

The best managers I've ever worked with gave feedback like breathing. It was constant, specific, and never a surprise. Their people grew faster because they always knew where they stood. Performance reviews should never contain new information, ever. What's the best piece of feedback you've ever received, and what made it land?
