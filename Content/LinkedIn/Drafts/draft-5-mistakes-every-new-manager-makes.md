Nobody teaches you how to be a manager.

They just promote you and hope for the best.

I've been there. I made every mistake on this list. The difference between a good new manager and a struggling one isn't talent. It's whether someone told them what the job actually requires before they learned it the hard way.

**Mistake 1: You keep doing the individual contributor work.**

You got promoted because you were great at your old job. Now you spend 60% of your time still doing it, and your team gets the leftovers of your attention. Your job changed. Your calendar didn't.

**Mistake 2: You try to be everyone's friend.**

You want to be liked. That's human. But the first time you need to give hard feedback or make an unpopular decision, the friendship dynamic makes it ten times harder. Friendly, yes. Friends, no. The distinction is everything.

**Mistake 3: You avoid hard conversations until they become crises.**

That small performance issue you noticed three months ago? It's now a big performance issue that the whole team can see. Every week you waited made the conversation harder and the damage worse.

**Mistake 4: You don't listen enough in the first 30 days.**

You want to prove you belong. So you come in with ideas and opinions and changes. But your team doesn't want your vision yet. They want to know you see them. Listen first. Change second.

**Mistake 5: You manage everyone the same way.**

Your senior engineer and your junior hire need completely different things from you. Different levels of autonomy, different types of feedback, different frequencies of check-in. One size fits none.

**The common thread in all five mistakes?** They happen because no one explained that management is a different skill set, not a promotion of the old one. The sooner a new manager understands that their job is now about their people, not their output, the faster everything changes.

Which of these did you learn the hard way?
