Nobody teaches you how to be a manager.

I've been there. I made every mistake on this list. The difference between a good new manager and a struggling one isn't talent. It's whether someone told them what the job actually requires before they learned it the hard way. They just promote you and hope for the best, and then you spend months figuring out what changed.

The first mistake is keeping the individual contributor work. You got promoted because you were great at your old job, and now you spend 60% of your time still doing it while your team gets the leftovers of your attention. Your job changed but your calendar didn't. The second mistake is trying to be everyone's friend. You want to be liked, and that's human. But the first time you need to give hard feedback or make an unpopular decision, the friendship dynamic makes it ten times harder. Friendly, yes. Friends, no. The distinction is everything.

The third mistake is avoiding hard conversations until they become crises. That small performance issue you noticed three months ago is now a big performance issue that the whole team can see. Every week you waited made the conversation harder and the damage worse. The fourth is not listening enough in the first 30 days. You want to prove you belong, so you come in with ideas and opinions and changes. But your team doesn't want your vision yet. They want to know you see them. Listen first, change second.

The fifth mistake is managing everyone the same way. Your senior engineer and your junior hire need completely different things from you. Different levels of autonomy, different types of feedback, different frequencies of check-in. One size fits none. The common thread in all five mistakes is that they happen because no one explained that management is a different skill set, not a promotion of the old one. The sooner a new manager understands that their job is now about their people, not their output, the faster everything changes.

Which of these did you learn the hard way?
