If anything in a performance review is news to the employee, the manager failed.

Not the process. The manager. I'll say it plainly: performance reviews should never contain surprises. Not a single one. Here's why most managers dread review season. They've been managing by impression for six months with no notes, no documentation, and no consistent conversations. Now they're staring at a blank form trying to remember what happened in March. So they write something vague like "strong performer, meets expectations, could improve communication." The employee reads it, feels unseen, and starts updating their resume.

The fix is embarrassingly simple. Run a real 1:1 every week. Take notes. Document what you discussed, what went well, what needs work, and what you both committed to. That's it. Do that for 26 weeks and here's what happens at review time: you open your notes and the review writes itself. Every piece of feedback has already been delivered. Every development area has already been discussed. The review becomes a summary of a year-long conversation, not a performance verdict dropped from above.

The math is clear. 26 documented 1:1s with real notes versus zero notes and a blank form. Which manager writes a better review? Which employee feels more respected? I call consistent 1:1s an insurance policy against performance review panic, but they're more than that. They're an insurance policy against losing your best people. Employees who know where they stand don't leave out of frustration. They leave when they're ready, on their terms, with a manager they still respect. That's the difference.

How do you keep track of what happens in your 1:1s throughout the year?
