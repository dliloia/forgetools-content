I conducted 1,250+ interviews at Amazon as a Bar Raiser.

As a certified Bar Raiser, my job was to evaluate candidates across the entire organization, not just my own team. Engineers, PMs, marketers, ops leads, VPs. I spent thousands of hours over five years sitting across the table from people and asking them to tell me about their work. After all those conversations, one thing predicted management quality more reliably than anything else: how candidates talked about their people.

Great managers told stories where other people were the heroes. "My team built this." "She figured out the solution." "He pushed back on my idea and he was right." They framed every accomplishment through the lens of the humans who made it happen. You could hear the pride in their voice when they described what someone else achieved. The work got done because the people were invested, and these managers understood that.

Weak managers told a completely different kind of story. "I saved the project." "I made the call." "I pushed them to do it." The team was a footnote, a supporting cast in a narrative that always centered on the manager. These candidates weren't necessarily bad people. They just hadn't made the mental shift from personal achievement to collective success, and it showed in every answer they gave.

The best managers I've ever met saw their job as creating the conditions for other people to succeed. The rest saw their job as succeeding through people. The difference sounds small, but the outcomes aren't. Teams led by people-first managers retained better, performed better, and produced future leaders at a rate that the self-centered managers never matched.

If you want to know what kind of manager you are, listen to how you describe your team's wins. Who's the hero of your stories?
