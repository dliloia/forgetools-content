## The Data You Already Know But Haven't Acted On

People don't leave companies. They leave managers. You've heard this. Everyone has heard this. Gallup has been publishing variations of this finding since the late 1990s, and it keeps landing in conference keynotes and LinkedIn posts and HR decks, and then mostly nothing happens.

Here's what the numbers actually say. Gallup's 2023 State of the Global Workplace report found that managers account for at least 70% of the variance in employee engagement scores. Seventy percent. Not compensation. Not PTO policy. Not the snack wall. The manager is the variable, and in most organizations, the manager is also the least-developed person in the building.

Meanwhile, companies spend real money on employer branding. Glassdoor profiles, culture videos, referral bonuses, "Best Places to Work" submissions. That's all a bet that you can attract your way out of a retention problem. You can't. If the manager experience is broken, the pipeline just fills faster with people who are going to leave anyway.

The turnover cost math is brutal. Conservative estimates put the cost of replacing a single employee at 50% to 200% of their annual salary, depending on role complexity. For a $75,000 manager-level employee, that's $37,500 on the low end. For a senior engineer or a sales director, you're often well over six figures when you account for recruiting fees, ramp time, lost productivity, and the drag on the team that stays. Multiply that by two or three departures a year from a single mediocre manager and you have a serious operational problem disguised as a HR metric.

## What Actually Drives People Out

The tricky part is that employees rarely tell you the real reason they're leaving. Exit interviews are mostly theater. People say they're leaving for "a new opportunity" or "growth" or "a better fit," and the HR team records it and moves on. What they're usually not saying is: my manager gave me no feedback for six months and I stopped believing anyone noticed what I was doing. Or: I watched my manager advocate for someone else's project in every meeting and assume I was fine. Or: I asked for one conversation about where my career was going and it never happened.

Those are the real answers. The exit interview is the most expensive feedback you'll ever collect, because by the time someone is sitting across from you on their last day, the decision is done. The work is over. Whatever you learn in that conversation costs you a person.

Here are the five manager behaviors I see most consistently predict departure, based on what I've observed managing hundreds of people and watching organizations lose good ones.

**Absence of feedback.** Not hostile feedback. The absence of any feedback. People will tolerate criticism. What they cannot tolerate, especially high performers, is invisibility. When someone stops receiving meaningful input on their work, they start to assume their manager either doesn't care or doesn't know what they're doing. Both interpretations lead to the same place.

**No advocacy.** Employees watch very carefully to see whether their manager goes to bat for them. Budget conversations, promotion cycles, project assignments: these are the moments that matter. A manager who consistently fails to represent their people in rooms they're not in sends a clear message about relative priority. People read that message correctly.

**Unpredictability.** Managers who are inconsistent in their moods, their priorities, their standards, or their availability force their teams into a permanent state of low-grade anxiety. You can't do your best work when you're spending energy trying to read the room. High performers especially will exit this environment, because they have options.

**No investment in development.** People want to grow. Not everyone wants to be a VP, but everyone wants to feel like they're getting better at something. A manager who never asks "where do you want to go from here?" or "what do you want to learn?" is signaling that they see their team as a means to an end. That signal is received.

**Broken trust.** This one is the hardest to recover from. Trust breaks in small ways before it breaks completely: the manager who takes credit, the manager who says one thing in the one-on-one and another thing in the team meeting, the manager who punishes someone for raising a problem. Once trust is gone, the relationship is functionally over even if the person stays for another eight months.

## The Honest Question

Every manager who loses a high performer has to ask: was it me? The honest answer, more often than most managers want to admit, is yes. Not because they were cruel or incompetent. Because they were underprepared. Managing people is a skill, and most organizations promote people into it based on individual performance and then provide essentially no instruction.

You get a new title, a new org chart line, maybe a two-day leadership training with a workbook you'll never open again, and then you're expected to conduct performance reviews, navigate conflict, develop careers, maintain team morale, and do all of this while also delivering your own results. It's a lot to figure out alone.

The managers who retain good people aren't doing anything magical. They're doing a small number of things consistently.

## What Consistent Looks Like

One-on-ones are the most underused tool in a manager's kit. Not status updates, not project check-ins, but actual conversations about the person: how they're doing, what's getting in their way, what they're learning, where they want to go. Done weekly and done well, a one-on-one builds the relationship before the crisis. By the time someone is ready to resign, a good manager has usually already caught the signal and done something about it.

Feedback has to be specific and it has to be regular. Not the annual review. Not the quarterly check-in. Regular, direct, specific input on the work. "That presentation landed well because you led with the data" is more useful than "good job this week." "I noticed you seemed disengaged in that meeting and I want to understand why" is more useful than silence.

Advocacy has to be visible. Tell your people when you go to bat for them. Not as a transaction, but because it closes the loop. "I made the case for you in the budget conversation and I want you to know that" is a sentence that builds loyalty.

And development conversations have to be intentional. Not "we'll circle back to the career stuff when things calm down." Things don't calm down. Block the time, ask the question, and actually listen to the answer.

## The Variable You Control

Companies will keep investing in employer branding. That's not going away. But the managers reading this have a different problem to solve, which is what happens after someone accepts the offer and shows up on day one.

The exit interview is too late. The resignation letter is too late. The decision to leave is usually made months before anyone says anything out loud. By the time someone has mentally quit, they're just waiting for the right moment to make it official.

You have time before that moment. Use it. Have the one-on-one. Give the feedback. Ask about the career. Go to bat for someone this week. The return on that investment is compounding, and the cost of skipping it shows up in ways that are very hard to explain on a budget spreadsheet.

The manager is the variable. That's a burden and it's an opportunity. Which one it is depends entirely on what you do with it.
