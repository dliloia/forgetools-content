The first 60 seconds of a 1:1 set the tone for the entire meeting. If you open with "So, where are we on the project?", you've just told your direct report this conversation is about your needs, not theirs.

Start with something human instead. "How are you doing this week?" or "What's been on your mind?" These are simple questions, but they send a clear signal: I see you as a person, not just a resource.

Here's what happens when you do this consistently. People start telling you things earlier. They flag burnout before it becomes a resignation letter. They mention a conflict with a teammate before it becomes a full-blown problem. You get better information because you built a space where they feel comfortable sharing it.

This doesn't mean you spend 30 minutes on small talk. Two minutes is enough. Ask how they're doing, listen to the answer, and then transition naturally. "Glad to hear that. Anything work-related I should know about before we dig in?"

If someone says "I'm fine" every single week with zero elaboration, that's data too. Either they don't trust the space yet, or something is off. Pay attention to patterns.

The managers who get the most out of their 1:1s are the ones who treat the first few minutes as a temperature check on the whole person. Not just the employee. The person.

Try it in your next 1:1. Open with a genuine question about them. See what changes.
