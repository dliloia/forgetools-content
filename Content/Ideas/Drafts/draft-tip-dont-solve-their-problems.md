Your direct report comes to you with a problem. Your instinct is to fix it. You've seen this before, you know the answer, and it would take you five minutes. So you jump in with a solution.

Stop doing that.

When you solve problems for your team, you get a short-term win and a long-term loss. The problem goes away today, but your direct report didn't learn anything. Next time they hit the same kind of issue, they'll come right back to you. You've trained them to depend on you instead of developing their own judgment.

Instead, ask questions. "What have you tried so far?" "What options are you considering?" "What would you do if I wasn't here?" These questions force them to think through the problem themselves. Most of the time, they already have a decent answer. They just needed someone to push them toward it.

This is hard. Especially if you were promoted from an individual contributor role where your entire value was solving problems. But your job changed. You're not the solver anymore. You're the coach.

There are exceptions. If the building is on fire, just tell them where the exit is. Urgent situations with real consequences need direct answers. But most of the problems that come up in 1:1s aren't emergencies. They're growth opportunities disguised as questions.

A good test: after your next 1:1, count how many times you gave an answer versus asked a question. If the ratio is heavily tilted toward answers, you're doing too much of the thinking.

Develop problem-solvers, not problem-bringers.
