evaluation_rubric: |
  Strong response includes: being direct that now is not the right time while clearly communicating it's a "not yet" rather than a "no"; providing specific, observable gaps between current performance and the next level (not vague platitudes); co-creating a development plan with concrete milestones and a realistic timeline; committing to providing stretch opportunities that let them demonstrate readiness; acknowledging their ambition as a positive thing; taking responsibility for their development rather than making it entirely their problem; setting a follow-up date to revisit the conversation.
  Weak response misses: avoids saying no directly and gives a wishy-washy "we'll see" answer; says no without explaining what would need to change; provides only vague feedback ("you need more experience"); makes promises about future promotion without authority to deliver; frames the conversation around what they lack instead of what they need to build; doesn't create any path forward; dismisses their ambition or makes them feel foolish for asking.

Taylor, a senior developer on your team, asks to schedule extra time in your 1:1 this week. When you sit down, they get right to the point: "I'd like to talk about promotion to Staff Engineer. I think I'm ready and I want to know what the path looks like."

You respect Taylor's ambition. They're a solid senior engineer who delivers consistently. But they're not ready for Staff yet. The gap is primarily in technical leadership and influence. Taylor does great individual work but hasn't demonstrated the ability to drive architecture decisions across teams, mentor other seniors, or operate at the strategic level the Staff role requires.

Taylor is clearly nervous and has rehearsed this conversation. They've prepared a list of their accomplishments over the past year. Some of them are genuinely impressive. But they're all individual contributions, which is exactly the pattern you need to address.

**Consider:**
- How do you open your response after Taylor makes their case?
- What specific gaps do you name, and how do you make them feel achievable rather than discouraging?
- What concrete plan do you propose for the next quarter?
- How do you handle it if Taylor pushes back or gets frustrated?
