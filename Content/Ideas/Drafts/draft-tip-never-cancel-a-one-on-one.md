Every time you cancel a 1:1, you send a message: something else matters more than you do.

You might not mean it that way. You had a fire to put out, a meeting with your boss, a deadline that couldn't move. But your direct report doesn't see the context. They see that the one meeting on their calendar dedicated to them just disappeared.

Do this enough times and people stop preparing for 1:1s. They stop saving things to tell you. They stop trusting that you'll show up. And eventually they stop telling you the hard stuff altogether, because why bother? The meeting might not even happen.

If you absolutely cannot make the time, reschedule. Don't cancel. There's a massive difference. "I need to move our 1:1 to Thursday, same time" tells someone you still value the meeting. "Let's skip this week" tells them you don't.

Here's a practical rule: treat your 1:1s like you treat meetings with your boss. You wouldn't cancel on your VP because something came up. Give your team the same respect.

If you're consistently struggling to protect 1:1 time, that's a scheduling problem, not a priority problem. Block them early in the week when your calendar is cleaner. Make them recurring and non-negotiable.

Your 1:1 is the single most important meeting you have as a manager. It's where trust is built, problems surface early, and people feel seen. Protect it.
