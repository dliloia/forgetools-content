The fastest way to stunt a direct report's growth is to answer your own questions for them. They ask what to do. You tell them. It feels efficient and it teaches them nothing.

The move I use instead is small and slightly uncomfortable. When I ask someone "what do you think we should do here?" and they say "I don't know," I do not move on. I say, "think about it. Just one idea." Then I wait.

Usually within ten or fifteen seconds, they offer something, often the actual answer, because they did know, they just did not want to commit to it without permission. The "I don't know" was a stall, not a sentence.

If they push back a second time and genuinely have nothing, then I offer a possibility and ask them to evaluate it. "What if we tried X. Would that work?" Now they are still doing the thinking, just with a starting point. The thinking is what matters.

Here is what makes this work: silence is fine. Your job is not to make the conversation comfortable. Your job is to develop the person. Filling the silence with your own answer trains them to wait you out, and they will get smaller every week.

The opposite is also true. Over a year of being asked "what do you think?" with real patience, people start to think. They develop a point of view. They walk into your office with options instead of questions. That is what good management looks like.

Try this in your next 1:1. The next time someone says "I don't know," count to fifteen.
