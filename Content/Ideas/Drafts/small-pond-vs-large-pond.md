## The Idea
Someone who grows up in the town they end up living in as an adult, takes a job out of high school or college, and stays in that same job has a very small pond. Every problem that lands in that pond is, by definition, a big problem to them, because their reference set is small.

Someone who has traveled, worked across industries, lived in different places, and worked different kinds of jobs has a much larger pond. The same problem that paralyzes the small-pond person is a known quantity to them. They've seen something shaped like it before.

This is why life experience is not optional for resilience. It is the mechanism that produces resilience.

## Why it matters
Most career advice treats "experience" as years on a resume. That is the wrong metric. What actually matters is the variety of problems your brain has already metabolized. Variety builds the pattern library you draw from when something hard happens. Tenure in a single context builds a deep pattern library for one pond and an empty library for every other pond.

- How managers should weigh "weird" resumes vs. straight-line ones in hiring
- Why long-tenured employees often struggle when the business model changes underneath them
- Practical: how to add pond-size when you cannot change jobs (rotations, side projects, customer visits, cross-functional ownership)
- The link to the "adapt or die" idea: pond size is what makes adaptation cheap
