"How's it going?" is a throwaway question. People answer it on autopilot. "Good." "Fine." "Busy." You learn nothing.

"What's blocking you?" is a different question entirely. It's specific. It's forward-looking. And it gives your direct report permission to be honest about problems instead of performing confidence.

When you ask what's blocking someone, you're telling them two things. First, you assume they're trying to make progress. Second, you want to help clear the path. That framing matters. It puts you on the same side of the table.

Compare these two conversations:

"How's the migration going?" "It's going well, making progress."

versus

"What's blocking you on the migration?" "Honestly, I'm waiting on the API team and I've pinged them three times. I could use your help escalating."

Same project. Completely different information. The second version gives you something you can actually act on.

You don't have to use those exact words every time. Variations work just as well. "What would make this week easier?" or "Where are you stuck?" or "What's slowing you down?" The point is to ask a question that invites a real answer.

One warning: if you ask this and then don't follow through on removing the blocker, you'll lose credibility fast. Only ask if you're prepared to act. That's what separates a good manager from one who's just going through the motions.
