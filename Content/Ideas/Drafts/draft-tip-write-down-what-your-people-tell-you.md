Your direct report tells you their kid just started kindergarten. Their partner is dealing with a health issue. They're interested in moving toward a technical lead role. They mentioned feeling burned out two weeks ago.

Do you remember all of that? Probably not. Not across six or eight direct reports, week after week.

Write it down.

This isn't about surveillance or building a file on someone. It's about showing people you actually listen. When you follow up three weeks later with "How's your partner doing?", that lands differently than "Remind me what you mentioned last time?"

The first version says: I care enough to remember. The second says: you're one of many and I lost track.

Keep it simple. After each 1:1, spend two minutes jotting down the key things they shared. What they're working on, what's stressing them out, any personal updates they volunteered, action items you committed to. A bullet list is fine. You don't need paragraphs.

This habit pays off in ways you won't expect. Over time, you'll start seeing patterns. Someone who mentions feeling overwhelmed three meetings in a row is telling you something bigger than any single conversation reveals. Someone who keeps bringing up a teammate is flagging a relationship issue.

Good management is pattern recognition. But you can't recognize patterns if you don't capture the data points. Your memory isn't as good as you think it is. Write things down.
