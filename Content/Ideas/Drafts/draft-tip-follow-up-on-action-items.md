Nothing kills trust faster than a manager who says "I'll take care of that" and then doesn't.

Every 1:1 generates action items. Sometimes they're on your direct report: finish the spec, set up the meeting, send the update. Sometimes they're on you: talk to another team lead, escalate the blocker, share that resource.

If you don't track these and follow up, two things happen. First, important things fall through the cracks. Second, your team learns that commitments made in 1:1s don't actually mean anything.

Start every 1:1 by reviewing what was agreed to last time. "Last week you said you'd reach out to the design team. How did that go?" and "I committed to talking to Sarah about the timeline. I did that, here's what she said."

This takes two minutes and it does something powerful. It creates accountability in both directions. Your direct reports see that you take their commitments seriously. And they see that you take your own commitments seriously too.

Here's the practical system. At the end of every 1:1, write down the action items. Who owns what. Then review that list at the start of the next meeting. If you use a notes tool, keep a running section at the top called "Open Items" and check them off as they get done.

When you consistently follow up, people start showing up to 1:1s having already done the things they committed to. Because they know you'll ask. That's not pressure. That's structure. And good people thrive in structure.
