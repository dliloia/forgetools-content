Most feedback fails on choreography, not content. People know what they want to say. They do not know how to open the door so the other person actually listens.

The single highest-leverage move I have ever learned is the opening line. Before you say anything else, ask: "Can I give you some feedback?"

Then wait. Let them say yes.

This sounds almost too simple to matter. It matters. When someone consents to receive feedback, a cognitive shift happens in the next two seconds. They stop defending themselves against a perceived attack and start orienting toward the information you are about to share. The same exact words, dropped on someone without permission, land as an ambush. With permission, they land as input.

The second reason it matters: you find out very quickly when the timing is wrong. If they say "actually can we do this later, I am in the middle of something stressful," that is useful. You have just avoided a conversation that was going to go badly and gained a chance to do it well later. The permission question is also a diagnostic.

A small caveat. If you never give positive feedback, the permission question feels like a warning every time. Use the same opener for praise. "Can I give you some feedback?" then "I noticed how you handled that customer call yesterday, the way you slowed down when they were frustrated really worked." Over time, the question stops carrying a charge and just becomes the way you communicate.

Try it once this week, on something small. Notice the shift in the room.
