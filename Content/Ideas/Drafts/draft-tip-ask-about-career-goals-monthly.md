Most managers only talk about career growth during annual reviews. That's eleven months too late.

People think about their careers constantly. Where they're headed, whether they're learning, if this role is a stepping stone or a dead end. If you're not having that conversation with them, they're having it with themselves. Or worse, with a recruiter.

Once a month, ask a career question in your 1:1. It doesn't need to be a deep existential conversation every time. Simple questions work.

"Are you still feeling challenged in your current role?"
"Is there a skill you want to develop that you're not getting to use?"
"Where do you want to be in a year? Has that changed?"

The answers will surprise you. Someone you assumed was content might be quietly frustrated. Someone you thought was a flight risk might just need a new project to sink their teeth into.

Here's why monthly matters. People's goals shift. The person who wanted to become a tech lead six months ago might now be interested in product management. If you only check in annually, you miss these turns entirely and end up developing someone toward a goal they've already moved past.

Keep notes on what they tell you. Over time, you'll build a clear picture of each person's trajectory. That lets you spot opportunities for them: a project that aligns with their goals, a stretch assignment, an introduction to someone in the role they want.

The managers people remember are the ones who invested in their future. This is how you do it. One question a month.
