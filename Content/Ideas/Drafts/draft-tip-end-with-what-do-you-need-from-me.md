Six words. Ask them at the end of every 1:1. "What do you need from me?"

This question does something most closing questions don't. It flips the power dynamic. For 25 minutes, your direct report has been updating you, answering your questions, responding to your agenda. This final question hands control back to them.

And it surfaces things that wouldn't come up otherwise.

"Actually, could you introduce me to the PM on the platform team? I've been trying to get time with them for weeks."

"I could use your help pushing back on the scope change. I don't have the political capital to do it alone."

"Honestly, I just need you to check in on me next week. I'm juggling a lot."

These are small asks that make a big difference. And people won't volunteer them unless you create the opening.

The key is asking consistently. If you do it once, people give you a polite "I'm good." If you do it every single week, they start coming prepared with an actual answer. They'll save things for that moment because they know it's coming.

Two rules to make this work. First, actually do the thing they ask for. If someone asks you to escalate something and you don't, they'll never ask again. Second, don't rush through it. Ask the question and then wait. Give them a few seconds to think. The best answers often come after a pause.

Your job as a manager is to clear the path. This question tells you exactly where the obstacles are.
