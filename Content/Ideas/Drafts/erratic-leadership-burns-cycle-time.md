## The Idea
There is a leadership pattern that looks engaged from the outside and is actually destructive: the chicken-little leader. Every third day the sky is falling. Every week there is a new emergency. The behavior is high-energy and feels like attention to the business, but it produces a workforce that cannot complete anything because every plan is interrupted by the next freakout.

A question of the form "can you take a look at this, something seems off" would have produced a real diagnosis and a fix. The same person packaged as "DROP EVERYTHING, THE SKY IS FALLING" produces forty minutes of context switching, a wrong fix under pressure, and a missed promise date downstream.

## Why it matters
Erratic leadership is a hidden tax on team output, and the tax does not show up on any dashboard. It shows up as missed deadlines, low morale, and good people leaving. The leader doing it usually thinks they are demonstrating urgency. The team experiences it as noise.

## Possible angles
- The cycle-time math: how each false emergency costs an hour of recovery for every person it touches
- Why erratic leaders attract one another and create whole erratic orgs
- The connection to escalation culture: when "urgent" loses meaning because everything is urgent
- Practical: how to ratchet down your own urgency-signaling without dropping standards
- How a calm middle-manager can absorb erratic upward pressure to protect their team (and the cost they pay for that)
