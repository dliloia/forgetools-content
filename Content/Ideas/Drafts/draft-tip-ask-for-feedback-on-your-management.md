You ask your team to be open to feedback. You give them feedback regularly. But when was the last time you asked them to evaluate you?

Most managers never do this. It feels vulnerable. What if they say something you don't want to hear? What if it's awkward?

Good. That discomfort is exactly why you should ask.

Try this in your next 1:1: "What's one thing I could do differently to be a better manager for you?" One thing. Specific, contained, answerable. Not "give me all your feedback on my management style," which is too big and too scary for most people to answer honestly.

The first time you ask, you'll probably get "No, you're great." That's not honesty. That's self-preservation. They're testing whether it's safe. Thank them, and ask again next month. Consistency is what unlocks the real answers.

When someone does give you honest feedback, your reaction in that moment determines whether they'll ever do it again. If you get defensive, explain yourself, or subtly punish them by being cold for a week, you've just taught your entire team that the question is a trap.

Instead: listen, thank them, and then actually change something. When you make a visible adjustment based on their feedback, you prove that the question was genuine. That's when trust deepens.

Some variations that work well: "Is there anything I'm doing that makes your job harder?" or "What's one thing you wish I'd do more of?" or "How could our 1:1s be more useful for you?"

You can't improve as a manager if you don't know what your team actually needs. Ask them. Then do something about it.
