Feedback has a half-life. The longer you wait to give it, the less useful it becomes.

If someone gave a rough presentation on Monday and you mention it in your Friday 1:1, they've already moved on mentally. They can't remember the specific moment. They can't connect your feedback to what they actually did. It just feels like a vague critique.

But if you pull them aside Monday afternoon and say, "Hey, I noticed you lost the room during the technical section. Want to talk about what happened?", now they can recall exactly what went wrong. They can learn from it while the memory is fresh.

This applies to positive feedback too. If someone handled a tough client call well, tell them that day. "I liked how you redirected the conversation when they got heated. That was a smart move." Specific, timely, actionable.

The 24-hour rule is simple. If something is worth giving feedback on, do it within a day. Not next week. Not at the next review cycle. Now.

Here's how to make this practical. Keep a running note during the day. When you see something worth commenting on, jot it down. Then carve out five minutes before end of day to deliver it. A quick Slack message, a two-minute hallway conversation, or a short video call. It doesn't need to be formal.

Managers who give fast feedback build teams that improve fast. The ones who save everything for quarterly reviews build teams that repeat the same mistakes for months.
