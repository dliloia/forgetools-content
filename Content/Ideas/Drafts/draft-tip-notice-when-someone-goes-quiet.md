The person most likely to leave your team isn't the one complaining loudly. It's the one who stopped talking.

When a normally engaged team member goes quiet in meetings, stops contributing ideas, gives shorter answers in 1:1s, or becomes invisible in Slack, that's a signal. Something has changed. Maybe they're disengaged. Maybe they're dealing with something personal. Maybe they've mentally checked out and started interviewing.

Whatever the cause, silence is information. And most managers miss it because they're focused on the noisy problems.

Here's what to do. When you notice the shift, name it directly but gently. "Hey, I've noticed you've been quieter than usual the last couple of weeks. Everything okay?" That's it. No assumptions, no pressure, just an observation and an open door.

Sometimes the answer is simple. They're tired, they had a bad week, they're heads-down on a hard problem. Fine. You checked in, they felt seen, you move on.

But sometimes the answer is bigger. They're frustrated with a decision that was made. They feel overlooked for a project. They're burning out. These are the conversations that save people from leaving, and they only happen if you notice the silence and ask about it.

A practical habit: at the end of each week, mentally scan your team. Who did you hear from a lot? Who did you barely hear from at all? If someone has been quiet for two weeks running, make a point to check in during your next 1:1.

Don't wait for people to come to you with problems. Watch for the ones who stopped coming at all.
