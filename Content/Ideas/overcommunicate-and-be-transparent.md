## The Idea

Teams perform better when they understand the WHY behind decisions, not just the WHAT. Overcommunication and radical transparency cost almost nothing but pay back in trust, buy-in, and reduced churn frustration.

## Source Material

A college friend sent Dave a reel from @bizwithbecs on Instagram ("Vroom vroom, leadership edition") about leadership communication. Dave's response captures the core lesson from real experience:

> "The thing that I do with the teams that I work with (either those that are directly reporting to me or the ones that are my peers) is to overcommunicate and be transparent as possible at all times. A good example would be my engineering teams. They often get asked to do a lot of work but don't always know why or what led to that decision. When things change they can get upset about the change and not know that it actually was a decision to save them future churn, even though they feel like they are losing because the work completed already is discarded. Communicating to them about what the conversations were, why it was important and to take their feedback lets them know that they are important and that they can see the path forward. It's a small step, and doesn't take much time, but is totally worth it."

Reference: https://www.instagram.com/reel/DVtZNC4j1YB/

## Why It Matters

- Engineers (and most ICs) hate doing work that gets thrown away. What makes it tolerable is understanding WHY the pivot happened.
- Transparency turns a frustrating change into a team-building moment. People feel respected when they see the reasoning.
- This costs almost zero time. A 5-minute conversation or a short Slack message explaining the context behind a decision prevents weeks of resentment.
- Connects to the Connect Framework: the Check-In and Employee's Agenda segments are where this transparency naturally lives.
