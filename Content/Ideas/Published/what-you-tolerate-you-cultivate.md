## The Idea
One of the most useful phrases I have for managers is this: what you tolerate, you cultivate. Whatever behavior you let slide, whatever standard you decline to enforce, whatever pattern you watch without intervening, you are quietly teaching your team that this is what acceptable looks like. The lesson lands whether you mean it to or not.

The manager who lets one person miss every deadline and then acts surprised when the rest of the team stops sweating their own. The leader who lets a senior IC be rude in meetings and then wonders why nobody pushes back on bad ideas anymore. The director who watches one team's metrics slip for three quarters and then books an offsite to figure out why the whole org's standards have eroded. In every case the answer is upstream of the visible problem. You cultivated this.

## Why it matters
This phrase is uncomfortable on purpose. Most "culture problems" trace back to a specific moment when a manager noticed something and decided not to act. The decision feels small in the moment. The cumulative effect is the culture you ended up with.

## Possible angles
- How a behavior tolerated for one quarter becomes the new baseline, and how fast cultural drift compounds
- The asymmetric cost of intervening late vs. early (the conversation only gets harder the longer you wait)
- How to spot what you are currently tolerating without realizing it (the things your team has stopped raising because nothing happens when they do)
- The flip side: what you actively reinforce, you also cultivate. Same mechanism, used deliberately.
- Tolerance vs. patience. The line between giving someone room to grow and quietly endorsing the wrong behavior.
- The manager's own habits as the loudest signal of what is tolerated. You can't speech your way past what you model.
