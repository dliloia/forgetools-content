## The Idea
Consistency is what allows other people to learn how to work with you. The leader I learned this from was a data and analytics director who was cold, overly direct, and difficult on first contact. I butted heads with him constantly. But he was consistent. Every interaction was the same shape, with the same standards, the same priorities. Over time I learned how to read him and how to be useful to him, and the relationship became one of the most productive working relationships I had.

The problem was never him. The problem was my ability to adapt to a style that was uncomfortable.

The opposite of this is the leader whose behavior is erratic, where the standards shift week to week and the priorities flip overnight. With that person you cannot ever settle in. You spend your energy reading the room instead of doing the work.

## Why it matters
Most leadership writing treats "be consistent" as a soft virtue and moves on. It is actually the hardest part of management to do well, because consistency under pressure requires that you have already done the work of figuring out what you actually believe. Erratic leaders are usually erratic because they haven't.

## Possible angles
- The Data Leader story: how consistency in a "difficult" style still produces a workable relationship
- Why the burden of adaptation often falls on the person reporting in, and what that means for the manager
- The compounding cost of inconsistency on team productivity (cycle time, context switching, planning quality)
- How to audit your own consistency as a manager (the things your team can predict about you vs. cannot)
- The link to trust: consistency is what trust is actually built on, not big gestures
