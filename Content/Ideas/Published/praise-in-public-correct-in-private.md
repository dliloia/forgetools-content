This is one of the oldest rules in management and it still gets broken constantly.

When someone does great work, say it where others can hear. In a team meeting, in a Slack channel, in a company all-hands. Public praise does double duty. It makes the person feel valued and it shows the rest of the team what good looks like.

When someone makes a mistake, handle it one-on-one. Always. No exceptions.

Correcting someone in front of their peers doesn't teach them anything useful. It humiliates them. And everyone else in the room makes a mental note: this manager will embarrass me if I mess up. That kills psychological safety overnight.

Here's what this looks like in practice. Say your designer presents a mockup in a team review and it's clearly not up to standard. Don't critique it in the meeting. Say something neutral like, "Thanks for sharing this. Let me review it and follow up with you." Then have the real conversation privately.

In that private conversation, be direct. "The mockup missed the mark on X and Y. Here's what I need to see in the next version." You can be honest without an audience.

A few things to watch out for. Make sure your public praise is specific, not generic. "Great job" means nothing. "The way you structured that proposal saved the client relationship" means everything. And make sure you're distributing praise across the team, not just spotlighting the same person every time.

Simple rule. Spotlight in public, coaching in private.
