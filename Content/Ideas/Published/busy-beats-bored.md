## The Idea
The conventional wisdom is that busy is the problem and rest is the cure. Most of the time it is the other way around. Busyness is what keeps you engaged with life. People who slow down to "just rest" for too long tend to drift, get flat, and even slide into a low-grade depression that they then blame on something else.

I noticed this in my own week recently. I had cut back on the after-work things I usually do, like building businesses on the side, learning new tools, taking a class, and replaced them with phone games and TV at the end of the night. We need that low-stim rest sometimes. I let it run too long. After a few days I felt slightly depressed. It took me until late one night to figure out the cause was not the work that piled up, it was the absence of the things I had stopped doing.

## Why it matters
Managers and high performers often misread their own boredom signals as stress signals. They cut activities, expect to feel better, and feel worse. Then they blame their job, their team, or their relationship for a flatness that came from the wrong intervention.

This also matters for early-career employees. Student life has long open stretches and few obligations; adult work-life does not. The instinct is to grieve the open stretches, but the engagement that replaces them, hobbies, side projects, classes, friendships, hard conversations, is the actual material of an adult life. Even arguments with people count. Even the frustrating things keep the system on. You want to feel something.

## Possible angles
- Why "just rest more" is often the wrong advice for the wrong problem. The two-minute self-diagnostic: am I tired or am I bored.
- The dignity of having too much going on. Why life-richness comes from voluntary complication.
- How to engineer engagement back into your life when nothing is forcing you to.
- Managers as the steady-state model for their team: when your engagement drops, your team's does too.
- The trap of treating after-work hours as recovery only. Recovery and engagement are different inputs.
- Personal essay angle: a 48-hour cycle of low-grade depression and the embarrassing root cause.
