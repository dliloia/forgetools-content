## The Idea
If somebody never makes mistakes, one of three things is happening. Either they have never been under enough pressure for you to know how they would react, or they are not pushing themselves hard enough to encounter the edge of their competence, or they are hiding their mistakes from you. None of those are good. The third one is the most dangerous.

I would rather hire and work with people who make occasional, honest, well-handled mistakes than with people who present an unblemished record. The unblemished record is the warning sign.

What I look for in the response to a mistake: ownership before deflection, root-cause analysis before excuse, and the question "what can we do to make sure this never happens again." If those three show up, that's somebody I can trust in the long run.

## Why it matters
The reflex in most organizations is to penalize mistakes, which teaches people to hide them. The hidden mistake is always more expensive than the disclosed one, because it festers and compounds. A culture that rewards disclosure and ownership is a culture that compounds in the right direction.

## Possible angles
- The three-categories framework (untested / coasting / hiding) for interpreting a too-clean record in interviews and performance reviews
- The errant-variable story (today's incident): "I don't remember doing it, but it must have been me" + "what can we do to make sure this never happens again" as a working template
- How to actually reward disclosure as a manager without creating a culture where mistakes feel celebrated for their own sake
