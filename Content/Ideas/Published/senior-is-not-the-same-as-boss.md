## The Idea
Junior employees routinely treat senior coworkers as if those coworkers had authority over them. Most of the time, they do not. Senior and boss are different relationships and the confusion causes a lot of unnecessary anxiety.

When a tenured colleague interrupts your meeting, talks over you, drops work on your plate, or behaves in a way you find rude, it is reasonable to say something. The worst thing they can do is be annoyed. They cannot fire you. They do not write your review. They are coworkers, with more tenure and more institutional credibility, but not more authority over your day.

Once you internalize this, a lot of workplace decisions get easier. You can decline a request that is not yours to take. You can ask someone to wait until you are done with the conversation you are already in. You can give feedback upward without the meeting feeling like a career risk.

## Why it matters
Most early-career employees have only experienced authority structures where the older person was the authority: parent, teacher, coach. They walk into workplaces and apply the same template. Anyone older or more tenured gets treated as a boss-equivalent. That instinct keeps them from setting basic professional norms and trains the rest of the org to keep doing the thing.

This is not a license to be combative with senior people. It is a license to be ordinary with them, the same way you would be with a peer.

## Possible angles
- The clean version: who actually has authority over you, and how to look it up if you are not sure
- The gendered and culturally-coded version: which junior employees get this wrong most, and why
- A practical script set: "I am in the middle of something, can I come find you in 10 minutes?" / "I appreciate the input, I will think about it" / "That is not on my list, who is owning that?"
- Why senior people often appreciate being pushed back on, when the push-back is calm and professional. It signals you have judgment.
- Where this fails: actual cultures where seniority does equal authority, even if the org chart says otherwise. How to read those rooms and decide whether to stay.
