## The Idea
Six months into my Amazon tenure, I owned a $20k vendor project for an automated data processing system. The vendor's engineer knew partway through that what I had asked for would not actually work, and built it anyway. When it failed in testing, his response was essentially: "I built what you asked for, not what would actually work." It was going to cost $40k more to fix.

I stayed at the office overnight (which is much less interesting than it sounds; there is nothing going on in there) and wrote a six-page paper. What had happened. The root cause, which was that I had not asked enough of the right questions at the start. What it would cost to fix. What I would do differently the next time.

The next morning I asked my boss, his boss, and a peer to read it in a glass cube. Twenty silent minutes of reading is a long time when you think you might get fired. At the end my boss's boss looked at me and said, "This is fantastic. You took responsibility, you correctly identified the root cause, and you have a plan. You just got through your first big screw-up at Amazon, and you handled it well."

That moment shaped what I think accountability looks like.

## Why it matters
Most people think accountability means accepting blame. It does not. Accountability is the practice of converting a failure into a diagnosis that the organization can learn from. The reason Amazon's six-pager culture works as well as it does is that it forces you to do that conversion in writing, in advance, in front of people who can pressure-test it. The six-pager is the mechanism (see also the mechanisms idea); accountability is the practice it enforces.

## Possible angles
- The six-pager as a template even non-Amazon teams can borrow (structure, what goes in it, what gets cut)
- Why writing it in advance, before the meeting, changes the conversation entirely
- The asymmetric outcome of accountability: the worst case is you get fired anyway; the best case is the failure becomes the moment your reputation as a leader is built
- What happens to engineers who knowingly build what doesn't work, and why that vendor relationship ended
- The link to "I don't trust people who don't make mistakes" — this is what handling a mistake well actually looks like in practice
