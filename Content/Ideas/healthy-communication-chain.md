## The Idea
The single biggest predictor of whether an organization keeps repeating its old mistakes is the health of the communication chain from top to bottom. When the chain is cared for, problems surface early, lessons travel, and the institution gets smarter over time. When it is neglected, leadership loses sight of what is actually happening at the line level, and the same issues keep showing up six to twelve months later wearing slightly different clothes.

This is almost always a leadership problem, not an information problem. If the manager is not asking specific questions, if the manager's manager is not asking specific questions, if the skip-levels are sporadic or theatrical, the signal never reaches the people who could act on it. So somebody two layers up rediscovers the same problem next quarter and thinks they are the first to see it.

## Why it matters
Most "we keep solving the same problem" patterns are not a learning problem, they are a transmission problem. The org has already learned the lesson somewhere, it just isn't being communicated up and across reliably. Fixing the transmission is usually cheaper than running another postmortem.

## Possible angles
- The skip-level meeting as the most underused mechanism for keeping the chain healthy
- How status meetings degrade into theater, and what to do about it (specific questions vs. open mic)
- The CEO's reading habit: what reaches their desk and what doesn't, and how that selection bias produces strategic blind spots
- Postmortems that produce action items nobody owns. The cycle that lets the same problem return.
- How to build a feedback loop that surfaces line-level signal without forcing everyone to perform "we're doing great" upward
- The manager's job as a translator: pulling signal up, pushing context down. Both directions matter, and most managers do one of them well and the other poorly.
