## The Idea
Most feedback fails on choreography, not content. People know what they want to say. They do not know how to set up the conversation so the other person actually receives it. The fix is a five-move sequence I have used thousands of times.

1. **Ask permission.** "Can I give you some feedback?" Wait for yes. Granting permission shifts the listener from defending themselves to receiving information.
2. **Describe behavior, not the person.** Not "you are dismissive," but "when you walked into my meeting and started talking before checking in..."
3. **Name the impact and outcome.** What changed because of the behavior. "It cut our conversation short and we did not finish the work we needed to do."
4. **Invite them to solve it.** "What do you think we could do differently next time?" If they say "I don't know," push past it. "Think about it. Just one idea." If they still pass, then you offer something. The point is to get them engaged in the fix before you hand it to them.
5. **Confirm and thank.** "Awesome, let's try that next time. I appreciate you being open to this."

The whole exchange takes 60 to 90 seconds. The shape is what does the work.

## Why it matters
The SBI model (Situation, Behavior, Impact) tells you what to put inside the feedback. It does not tell you how to open the door. Permission-First is the door-opener. Without it, even well-constructed SBI feedback lands as an ambush, because the listener is on guard before the first word.

The "invite them to solve" move is the part people skip. If you hand someone the answer, they nod and forget. If you make them generate at least part of the answer, they own it. The "I don't know" push is essential. Most people throw out the first vague guess to make the conversation end. Holding the silence one more beat usually produces the actual answer.

This works on peers, on senior people, on direct reports, and on bosses. It is hardest to do the first time. It gets easier with reps.
