## The Idea
Companies hire someone, hand them a job description, and expect them to figure it out by watching whoever is already there. That is not onboarding. That is shifting a managerial duty onto the new hire and calling it tradition.

The pattern usually rationalizes itself with "I had to learn it myself." Sometimes followed by "and I turned out fine." Both are true and both miss the point. Whatever you learned by trial and error is now your institutional knowledge. Refusing to write it down forces every new person to re-learn the same lessons at the same cost, which is a tax the company pays on every hire.

The fix is not glamorous. Write things down. Record short walkthroughs. Keep a notes file open during the work, not after it. You do not need every instruction ready at a moment's notice. You need to be the kind of person who, when asked, can produce one within an hour.

## Why it matters
A manager who does not document is a single point of failure. They cannot take vacation cleanly. They cannot grow their team. They cannot promote anyone. They are the bottleneck, and the moment they leave or burn out, the system collapses.

The "I figured it out myself" stance is also a status move. It signals that learning the job is a hazing ritual rather than a normal expectation of management. Anyone who comes up under that model will repeat it when it is their turn to onboard the next person, and the dysfunction propagates.

The opposite stance is simple. Documentation, recordings, written instructions are part of the job. Not optional polish. Not "I'll do it when I have time." Part of the job.

## Possible angles
- The vacation test: can your team run cleanly for two weeks without you? If not, what is missing in writing?
- The promotion test: can the person below you take your job tomorrow with no scramble? Same question.
- What "documented" actually means: not a 60-page playbook, but answers within an hour to predictable questions
- The cultural piece: when a manager refuses to document, it is a tell about how they view their own value
- AI as the new documentation partner: voice notes, transcripts, summarization. The bar for documenting has dropped by 90%; the people still not doing it are now visible.
- Personal anecdote: every time I have onboarded into a place with no documentation, I have built it as I went. Then left it for the next person. That is the move.
