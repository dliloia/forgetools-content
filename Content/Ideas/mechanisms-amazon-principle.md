## The Idea
At Amazon, and especially in Bezos's writing, a mechanism is a specific thing: a practice that produces measurable signal, ideally automated, manual only when it has to be. The reason mechanisms matter is brutally simple. You cannot improve what you cannot see. If you don't have a way to measure an outcome, you cannot tell whether your last change made it better or worse, which means you cannot make real progress, you can only make changes that feel productive.

The content engine I run on ManagerForge is a working example. The cron generates an article daily. The voice audit measures whether the draft passes a set of explicit rules. The Telegram bot tells me which articles shipped, which failed, and which rules they failed on. Now I can see the error rate by rule and decide where the highest-leverage fix is. Without those three pieces, the system would feel like it was working as long as articles kept appearing, and I would have no idea what was actually being published.

## Why it matters
Most teams operate on vibes. The leader has a feeling about how things are going, and the feeling becomes the report. Mechanisms replace vibes with signal. The discomfort of introducing them is usually proportional to how badly the team needs them.

## Possible angles
- The mechanism vs. metric distinction (a metric is a number; a mechanism is the practice that produces and acts on the number)
- Examples of cheap mechanisms a small team can install today (weekly written status, error-rate dashboard, post-mortem template)
- Why "good judgment" alone scales poorly past about ten people, and how mechanisms cover that gap
