## The Idea
The fastest way to shut down a direct report's growth is to answer your own questions for them. They ask what to do. You tell them. Done. Efficient, and almost completely useless for their development.

The move I use instead is small and annoying at first. When I ask someone "what do you think we should do here?" and they say "I don't know," I do not move on. I say, "think about it. Just give me one idea." If they push back again, I sit in the silence. Usually within ten or fifteen seconds they offer something, often the actual answer, because they did know, they just did not want to commit to it.

If they genuinely have nothing, then I offer a possibility and ask them to evaluate it. "What if we tried X? Would that work?" Now they are still doing the thinking, just with a starting point.

## Why it matters
"I don't know" is rarely true. It is usually a stall, sometimes a deference, sometimes a fear of being wrong. Filling that silence with your own answer teaches the person that they do not have to develop a point of view, because you will always supply one. They get smaller. You get more meetings.

The other reason this matters: when they generate part of the answer, they own it. They are bought in by the time you agree on the next step, because the step has their fingerprints on it. When you hand them the answer, they nod and forget by lunch.

This is also the move that distinguishes coaching from telling. Telling is a one-way transfer. Coaching is a structured refusal to do the work for them, paired with the patience to wait while they do it.

## Possible angles
- The mechanics of holding silence: how long, when to break it, how to keep your face neutral
- Why "what do you think?" is the most under-used phrase in management
- The trap of being the smartest person in the room: it feels productive and it stunts everyone
- When to break the rule: emergencies, safety issues, situations where someone genuinely has not been exposed to the question before
- How to layer this with feedback delivery: the feedback move ends with "what could we do differently?" The principle is the same.
- The compounding effect over a year: people who get asked to think actually start thinking, people who get told stop trying
