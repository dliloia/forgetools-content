## The Idea
People conflate boundaries and ultimatums. They are not the same thing, and the confusion makes them avoid both.

A **boundary** is a decision you make about your own behavior. It describes what you will do. "If someone interrupts my meeting, I will tell them I am in a meeting and ask them to come back after." That is yours. It holds whether the other person likes it or not, because the action belongs to you.

An **ultimatum** is a demand on someone else's behavior. It describes what they must do or else. "If you ever interrupt me again, I will go to HR." That puts the other person's actions at the center, with you holding a threat behind your back. Ultimatums create defensiveness, escalate quickly, and rarely produce the behavior change you wanted.

The reason this matters: many people, especially early-career, do not set boundaries because they have only seen the ultimatum version and it looks aggressive. So they say nothing, the behavior continues, and they cultivate the thing they wish would stop.

## Why it matters
The boundary frame puts you back in the driver's seat. You stop trying to control the other person and start describing what you will do. That is the part you actually have authority over. It is the only part.

There is a second-order effect too. When you state a boundary calmly, the other person usually does adjust, not because they are obeying a demand, but because they now have new information about what working with you looks like. Most people adjust to clear information faster than they adjust to demands.

And when they don't adjust, the boundary still works. Because you said what you would do, you do that thing, and the situation resolves on your terms.
