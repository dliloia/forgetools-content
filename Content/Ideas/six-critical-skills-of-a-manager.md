## The Idea
Strip management down to the irreducible skills and you get six:

1. **Running 1:1s that actually surface what's happening** (relationship, not status)
2. **Hiring well** (interviewing, evaluating fit, calibrating signal)
3. **Setting goals that matter** (specific enough to fail at, ambitious enough to grow into)
4. **Managing through projects** (without micromanaging, without disappearing)
5. **Giving feedback continuously** (not via the annual review)
6. **Coaching yourself** (most managers never look at their own patterns)

Now look at the management software market. Every tool picks one or two and builds depth there. Lattice does feedback and reviews well, hiring is bolted on. Greenhouse owns hiring, the rest is generic. 15Five is feedback-heavy. Asana is project management with a team layer. None of them treat the manager as a single person with six interconnected skills, because they're building for HR buyers who buy by skill silo.

## Why it matters
This is foundational positioning for the eventual ManagerForge / HireForge / GoalForge / ProjectForge product line. It names the gap that every existing tool leaves: the manager experience is fragmented across six dashboards, six logins, six paywalls. The six-skill framing argues for an integrated portal — eventually. Today, ManagerForge starts at the keystone (1:1s) and the others follow.

## Tone notes
Surveying the market without trash-talking specific tools. Each has its strengths. The point isn't "they're bad," it's "the manager is being asked to integrate them in their head, and that's expensive." Use Dave's experience hiring/managing across his career, not abstract claims.
