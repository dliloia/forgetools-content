## The Director Nobody Wanted to Work With

There was a data and analytics director in one of my earlier roles who had a reputation that preceded him. People warned me before my first meeting with him. "He's cold." "He doesn't do small talk." "Don't take it personally." I nodded and figured I'd be fine.

I was not fine, at least not at first.

He was all of those things, blunt in a way that felt like dismissal until you understood it wasn't personal, it was just his mode. No preamble, no relationship maintenance, no checking in on how your weekend was. You came to him with a problem, he told you what he thought, you left. I came out of our early interactions frustrated in a way I struggled to articulate. It wasn't that he was wrong. If anything, that was part of what bothered me. He was usually right, and he delivered it with zero acknowledgment that being right might also require some care for the person receiving the information.

So I pushed back. Probably more than was useful. I mistook his style for disrespect, and I responded in kind, which helped neither of us.

And then, slowly, I started to notice something.

Every interaction had the same shape. Same standards for what constituted finished work. Same priorities, week after week, with no mysterious reshuffling on Monday morning. Same threshold for what deserved his attention versus what he expected me to handle without involving him. The feedback was consistent, the expectations were consistent, the way he ran a meeting was consistent. Nothing about working with him was warm, but everything about working with him was legible.

Once I understood that, everything changed. I stopped spending energy trying to read his mood. I stopped bracing for a new set of rules I'd have to reverse-engineer. I learned the pattern, and I built around it. I learned how to bring him information in the format he actually wanted, how to anticipate the three questions he'd ask before I walked in, how to frame a recommendation in a way that would land rather than get deflected. Within a year, working with him was not just manageable, it was genuinely good. We disagreed often and productively. He pushed my thinking in directions I wouldn't have found on my own.

Looking back, that relationship was one of the most professionally useful of my career, and it would never have gotten there without his consistency.

The lesson I took from it wasn't about communication styles or adapting to difficult personalities, though both of those things are real. The lesson was structural. Consistency is what gives the people around you the raw material they need to learn how to work with you. It doesn't matter how uncomfortable your style is. If you're consistent, people can eventually map you. They can build their habits and their planning and their communication around what they know to be true about you. If you're not consistent, they can't, no matter how personable or warm or well-intentioned you are.

The manager whose behavior is erratic, whose priorities flip overnight and whose standards shift week to week without explanation, is asking their team to do something impossible. To plan against a moving target. To adapt to something that has no stable shape. The cognitive overhead of that is enormous, and it produces nothing. People spend their energy reading the room instead of doing the work, and reading the room is expensive in a way that rarely shows up on any metric but absolutely shows up in results over time.

I've thought a lot about why some leaders are erratic, because in my experience it's almost never malicious. Most of the inconsistent managers I've known weren't trying to create chaos. They were operating without a clear internal compass, without a settled sense of what they actually stood for, what they were genuinely optimizing for, what trade-offs they'd make and which ones they wouldn't. When pressure arrived, there was no durable principle underneath the behavior to hold it steady. So the behavior moved with the pressure.

Consistency under pressure is what separates the managers people can count on from the managers people quietly route around. And doing it well requires something that most management development doesn't actually help with: figuring out what you believe before the pressure arrives.

That's the upstream work. Know your real standards, not the ones you'd write in a performance review, but the ones that show up in how you react when something goes wrong at 4pm on a Friday. Know what you're actually optimizing for when you have to choose. Know your non-negotiables and be able to name them without thinking. Most managers, if they're honest, have done very little of this work explicitly. They operate on instinct and assume their instincts are consistent. Often they aren't.

Trust, at the mechanical level of how it actually accumulates between a manager and a team, is built through repetition. Not grand gestures, not vulnerable moments at an offsite, not the time you went to bat for someone in a difficult review cycle. Those things matter, but they're not the foundation. The foundation is your team watching you make the same call in the same situation, over and over, until the pattern is undeniable. By the thirtieth time, they don't have to think about what you'll do. They know. And knowing frees them to do real work instead of spending their energy on you.

The analytics director I worked with all those years ago never softened. He was consistent until the end, in exactly the way he was consistent at the beginning. But I think about him often when I'm thinking about what it means to be a manager people can actually count on. He gave me something more useful than warmth. He gave me solid ground. And that turned out to be worth more.
