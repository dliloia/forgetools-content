## Erratic Leadership Burns Cycle Time

I worked alongside a senior leader once who I'll describe as a high-energy presence. Smart, genuinely invested in the business, always the first person to notice when something looked wrong. On paper, you'd want that. In practice, the team had developed a quiet, practiced ritual: when the urgent message arrived, they would pause, look at each other, and wait.

They weren't being lazy. They were being rational. They had learned, through repetition, that roughly half of the alarms would resolve themselves before noon. The other half were real, but had been packaged with so much urgency that no one could think clearly enough to actually fix them. So they waited for the signal to clarify before they committed.

That's what erratic leadership produces. Not panic. Eventually, not even alarm. Just a team that has learned to be strategic about when to take the alarm seriously.

The thing is, from the leader's perspective, the behavior looked like engagement. He was paying attention. He cared. He was catching things early and raising them immediately. What he couldn't see, because it didn't show up anywhere he was looking, was the cycle time burning off every time he fired a signal.

Here's what I mean by cycle time. When you're focused on a piece of work, really in it, you're not just executing steps. You're holding a mental model of the whole thing: the context, the dependencies, the next five moves. That model takes time to build. It also collapses immediately when you get interrupted.

Every "DROP EVERYTHING" message collapses the model for every person it reaches. Rebuilding it, once the emergency is sorted, takes another 20 to 30 minutes before someone is fully productive again. If you have five people on a team and you fire two of those messages a week, you are burning somewhere between five and eight hours of team output weekly, conservatively. That's before you count the actual meeting, the wrong fix applied under pressure, or the downstream miss when something that was supposed to ship that week didn't.

None of that shows up as a leadership cost. It shows up as team performance.

I've managed a lot of people across a long career. One of the things I've noticed consistently is that the teams with the most missed deadlines are rarely the teams with the worst individual performers. They're often teams whose leaders have inadvertently made it impossible to finish things. Every plan has a different emergency grafted onto it before it ships. Every sprint ends with half the work deferred because something blew up mid-week. The team is working hard and producing less because their time is being chopped into pieces too small to build anything with.

There is also a subtler cost, and it takes longer to name.

When urgent loses meaning, you lose the ability to mobilize. I've watched this happen to organizations. In the early days of an erratic leader's tenure, the team responds. They take the urgency seriously. They show up. But after a few months of false alarms, they develop a filter. They've gotten good at quiet triage: is this real, or is this another one? By the time the actual emergency arrives, the one that genuinely needs everyone's full attention right now, they apply the same filter. And the response is slower than it needs to be.

The leader has spent their mobilization capital on things that didn't need it. That's an expensive thing to run out of.

I want to be careful here, because I don't think most erratic leaders are indifferent to this. Most of them are anxious, and anxiety, when you're responsible for outcomes, can easily disguise itself as vigilance. There's also a real pattern where signaling urgency once actually worked. There was a genuine crisis, the leader pulled the alarm, the team rallied, and something got saved. That's a legitimate success. The problem is that the brain remembers it as a tool that works, and starts reaching for it more often.

Over time, you get a leader who has conflated caring about outcomes with signaling urgency about everything, and those are not the same behavior.

The leaders I've seen build genuinely high-output teams operate differently. They move fast when it's real. They stay out of the way when it isn't. They do the work of investigating before they broadcast, so when they do broadcast, the signal carries meaning. Their teams are activated, not desensitized.

That kind of trust is slow to build and fast to destroy. The thing worth protecting is the reliability of your signal. If you say something matters, it should matter every time you say it.

That's the actual job.
