## The Meeting You're Getting Wrong

There's a pattern I've watched play out hundreds of times, and it took me longer than I'd like to admit to see it clearly in my own behavior.

A manager, a good manager, puts real thought into their 1:1s with direct reports. They prep. They think about what each person needs. They show up with an agenda and they ask real questions. Then they walk into the weekly meeting with their own boss and just wait. They answer questions. They give a status update. They react to whatever the conversation surfaces. The meeting ends and nothing much changed.

That's not a management problem. That's an identity problem. Somewhere in the mental model of most managers, the 1:1 going upward is a different kind of meeting. Something more passive, more obligatory. Something you survive rather than lead.

I don't think this is laziness. I think it's a narrow definition of the job. Most of us learn to manage by learning to manage downward. We think about our direct reports, their development, their performance, their morale. We study what makes a good 1:1 with someone who reports to us. But the managing-up version of that same meeting, the one with your own boss, rarely gets the same intentional treatment.

And your relationship with your manager determines almost everything that matters in your work: your ability to protect your team from bad decisions above, your access to the resources your team actually needs, how much trust you can spend when things go sideways, and your own career. If you're leaving that relationship to chance, you're leaving all of it on the table.

The first shift is understanding what your manager actually wants from the meeting.

It's not a status update. Your manager, if they're any good, has a sense of where things stand. What they don't have, and what they actually need from you, is signal. They need to know you're thinking at the right altitude. They need to know you see the things that could become problems before they do. They need to believe, based on repeated evidence, that they can trust your piece of the business to run without them hovering over it.

When you walk in with a list of what's done and what's in progress, you're telling them what happened. When you walk in with a sense of what you're worried about, what's at risk, where you need their help, and what your team needs that only they can provide, you're being the kind of leader they can actually rely on. Those are not the same meeting.

I learned this the hard way in a role where I had a manager who was brilliant but deeply stretched. She was managing up herself constantly, pulling in multiple directions, rarely in the building. Our 1:1s were 30 minutes on the calendar and often got cut to 15. For the first few months, I treated those meetings as a chance to update her, to show my work, to reassure her things were fine. And they were fine. But I wasn't making use of the time.

The shift came when I started treating those 15 minutes the way I treated my best 1:1s downward. I came in knowing one thing I needed from her. One thing that was at risk. One thing my team needed that she could help move. And I kept it tight because I respected her time.

Within a few months, she was going to bat for my team in rooms I wasn't in. She was surfacing my work to people above her. Not because I'd impressed her in some big dramatic moment, but because I'd made it easy for her to trust me, week after week, in 15-minute increments.

That's the mechanism. Consistency over time, in small regular doses. The same thing that builds trust with a direct report builds trust going upward, it just requires you to be the one doing the work instead of waiting for the other person to draw it out of you.

There's also the advocacy piece, which most managers underperform badly. The 1:1 with your manager is your best opportunity to fight for your team. When you need headcount, when someone deserves recognition, when a decision has been stuck above you for weeks, when your team needs something your manager can either provide or unlock, this is the forum. If you don't bring it, nobody will. Your team is depending on you to have those conversations, and they happen in rooms your team never sees.

The trap is thinking that managing up is about managing perceptions. It's not. The managers I've respected most over the years weren't the ones who showed up polished and strategic and impressive. They were the ones who showed up honest, prepared, and consistent. Who surfaced real problems before they became fires. Who asked for what they needed directly instead of dropping hints and hoping. Who followed through on what they said they'd do.

That last part matters more than anything. Your manager is building a model of you in their head, whether you're aware of it or not. Every week that you say you're going to do something and then do it adds to that model. Every week you say something and it doesn't happen, you're making a small withdrawal from the account. Over time, those deposits and withdrawals determine whether your manager trusts your judgment when something important is on the line.

And something important will always eventually be on the line. The meeting you're having this week isn't just about this week.

Prepare for it like it matters. Because it does.
