## The Manager Who Grows People

There's a particular kind of manager that top performers seek out. Not the ones with the best titles or the biggest teams or the most impressive track records as individual contributors. The ones who have a reputation for growing people. The ones whose former direct reports are now running departments, launching companies, leading functions they weren't supposed to be ready for yet. Those managers are rare, and what separates them from everyone else isn't talent. It's intentionality.

I learned this the hard way, mostly by watching managers who didn't do it well and paying attention to what the failure cost them.

Early in my career I worked for a manager who was brilliant at execution. He could run a project, hit a number, solve a technical problem faster than anyone I'd seen. But he didn't develop people. He used them. When a senior role opened up on the team, he hired externally because, in his words, nobody internally was "quite ready." Three of his best people, including someone I considered genuinely exceptional, left within a year. The new external hire took 14 months to get up to speed and never fully earned the team's trust. That manager kept getting promoted himself for years because his short-term numbers looked good. But the teams he left behind were always weaker than when he found them.

I decided early that I didn't want to be that manager.

The first thing I had to learn was how to read the signals. Not the obvious ones, not the person who's already performing at the next level and just waiting for the title to catch up. The earlier signals, the ones that tell you someone has the potential to lead before they've proven it yet.

I started paying attention in 1:1s. Not to what people were reporting, but to how they were thinking. The ones who brought me a problem with context, a theory about why it happened, and at least a preliminary point of view on what to do about it. Those were different from the ones who just surfaced the issue and waited. Both might be solid performers. Only one of them was already thinking like a leader. I also watched how people talked about teammates when things went sideways. The ones who said "we didn't execute that well" even when it was clearly someone else's failure. Those people had the character to lead. Character is harder to develop than skill. When I found it, I paid attention.

The stretch assignment is where real development happens, and also where a lot of well-meaning managers blow it. The instinct is right: give people challenges beyond their current scope. But a stretch assignment that lacks real stakes is just extra work with a motivational label on it. The risk has to mean something. Success has to mean something.

I had a senior engineer on my team, technically one of the best I'd managed. Her work was consistently excellent and she had genuine leadership instincts. But she had a specific weakness: she couldn't hold a position under pressure. She'd give a well-reasoned recommendation, someone senior would push back, and she'd fold. Not because she was wrong. Because she hadn't yet developed the confidence to stay grounded when authority pushed against her. That's a very specific gap, and it wasn't going to close through feedback alone. She needed reps.

So I built her a real one. I gave her ownership of a significant build-vs-buy decision that had real budget implications and real organizational opinions on multiple sides. She had to analyze it, form a position, and then present and defend it in a working session with a VP who was known for asking hard questions and not letting go until he was satisfied. We spent two weeks preparing. I played the VP. I asked every hard question I could think of. I pushed on her assumptions and watched where she wobbled. We worked on those spots specifically. And then I stepped back and let her run it.

She held her position. She pushed back on the VP twice, clearly and respectfully, with data. He changed his view on one point and told her afterward it was a good meeting. She was promoted four months later. And more importantly, something shifted in how she carried herself after that. She'd proven something to herself that no amount of positive feedback from me could have given her.

The other piece of this that most managers miss is visibility. You can develop someone brilliantly in the context of your own team and still watch them get passed over for advancement because the senior leaders who make those decisions don't know who they are. Calibration, whether formal or informal, happens in rooms full of people making quick judgments. If your person has no presence in those rooms, no track record in the minds of the people in them, they are starting from behind.

I made it a practice to look for legitimate reasons to get my high-potential people in front of senior leadership. I'd have them present team results instead of presenting myself. I'd bring them into skip-level meetings with a real role, not just as an observer. I'd send them to represent the team in cross-functional work and then brief my own manager specifically on what they were doing and why it mattered. And in the conversations that happened without my team present, the planning meetings and the calibration sessions and the hallway conversations with my peers, I talked about them directly. "I want to tell you about Marcus. Here's what he did last quarter and here's what I think he's capable of." If you are not making that case in the rooms your people aren't in, you cannot count on anyone else doing it.

The last thing is the honest conversation. The one most managers avoid because they don't want to disappoint someone they respect. I've had to sit across from strong performers and tell them they weren't ready yet. Not because I didn't believe in them. Because the specifics weren't there yet and they deserved to know exactly what the specifics were. Vague encouragement is not kindness. It's avoidance. The people I've seen develop fastest were the ones who had a clear and honest picture of what ready actually looked like and what was standing between them and it.

I've also had to tell people who were genuinely ready that the opportunity wasn't there yet. That the business need didn't exist or the organizational will wasn't present. Those conversations are harder in some ways because there's nothing to fix. But even then, honesty is the right move. It lets people make informed decisions about their own careers. It treats them like adults. And it builds a kind of trust that survives disappointment.

The managers who build the strongest teams I've ever seen weren't necessarily the sharpest strategists or the most charismatic leaders in their organizations. But they were relentless about one thing: finding people with real potential and doing the patient, unglamorous work of turning that potential into something undeniable. That work is never urgent. It never shows up on a dashboard. But its absence, over time, is catastrophic. And its presence, over time, is what a legacy is made of.
