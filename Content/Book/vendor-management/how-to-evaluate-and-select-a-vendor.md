## How to Buy Without Getting Sold

I spent years watching smart people make bad vendor decisions. Not because they were careless, but because the process they ran was built to benefit the vendor, not the buyer.

The structure of a typical procurement cycle puts the vendor in the driver's seat from the start. Vendors come in with prepared materials, practiced answers, and a very clear picture of what their product does well. The buying team comes in with a general sense of what they need and a calendar full of demos. The vendor knows the terrain better than you do. That asymmetry is the whole problem.

The fix isn't more demos. It's building your requirements, your scoring criteria, and your evaluation process before any vendor gets a look at you.

I learned this the hard way. Early in my career I was part of a team that selected a contact center platform based largely on the quality of the vendor's demo and the confidence of their account executive. The product looked great. The implementation was a disaster. Not because the product was bad, but because we had never really interrogated whether it fit our actual environment. We had optimized for how good it felt in the room, and that's a completely different question from whether it would work in our operation.

By the time I was managing large operations at scale, my approach had changed completely. The RFP and scoring rubric went out before vendors ever got face time. And the rubric was locked before proposals came back, because I'd seen too many evaluation processes where the weights quietly shifted to favor whoever had made the strongest impression.

Here's what that discipline actually looks like in practice.

Before you write a single line of an RFP, your team needs to produce a requirements document. This isn't a feature wishlist. It's a clear-eyed answer to a harder question: what does this vendor have to do in order for your operation to function? What breaks if they can't do it? What does success look like in twelve months, and what does it look like in three years?

Split that list honestly into must-haves and nice-to-haves. Must-haves are disqualifiers. If a vendor can't meet one, they're out, regardless of how well they perform on everything else. Nice-to-haves are genuinely optional. The discipline of distinguishing between them is harder than it sounds, because vendors are skilled at making their differentiating features feel essential. Maintain the distinction.

From the requirements, you build the scoring rubric. Assign weights to categories based on what actually drives your decision. Total cost of ownership, integration capability, implementation timeline, support quality, whatever genuinely matters for your context. Then score each vendor one to five for each category, multiply by the weight, and total it. The rubric isn't a decision machine, but it produces a structured view that forces an honest conversation.

The RFP itself should be specific enough that a templated response won't satisfy it. Ask for the implementation timeline for a customer at your size. Ask what causes delays. Ask for references in your industry who went live in the past eighteen months, and then call those references on the phone. The difference between a written reference and a real conversation is the difference between what someone is willing to put in writing and what they'll tell you when they're actually talking. You want the second version.

The demos are yours to run. Send scenarios to vendors before the meeting. Your scenarios, based on your actual workflows. Tell them you want to see specific things, not their standard highlight reel. And bring the people who will actually use the system into the room, because they will see things the decision-makers never will.

The cost model deserves its own discipline. Proposals rarely include all the costs, and sometimes that's complexity rather than deception, but you need to build the full picture. Implementation, training, support, renewal terms, exit costs. Run a three-year model for every finalist. Year-one price comparisons are often misleading. The picture looks different at three years, and the vendor who appears most expensive upfront sometimes wins on total cost when you do the math.

After all of it, when the rubric scores are close and the decision comes down to judgment, I come back to a few questions. Which vendor's team do I trust to handle a problem I haven't seen yet? Which sales process felt like a partnership and which felt like a transaction? Which references were genuinely enthusiastic rather than politely positive?

The vendor who wins the demo wins the room for an afternoon. The vendor you select is your partner for years, sometimes under difficult circumstances, usually when something has gone wrong and you need a team on the other side of the phone who will actually help you fix it. That's the relationship you're evaluating.

Get your requirements written before any vendor sees you. Lock the rubric before you read a proposal. Run the demos on your terms. Model three years. Call the references.

The process isn't complicated. But it only protects you if you run it before you fall in love with a pitch.
