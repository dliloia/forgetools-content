## The Checklist That Didn't Work

There's a particular kind of organizational failure I've watched repeat itself across companies for most of my career. A problem surfaces, smart people get in a room, they work hard on a solution, and then three months later the problem is still there. Sometimes it's worse. The team is confused, leadership is frustrated, and nobody can explain what went wrong because the solution looked right on paper.

What went wrong, almost always, is that they solved the symptom.

The symptom is the thing you can see, the metric that dropped, the complaint that showed up in the survey, the process that started breaking down. It's real and it hurts and it demands attention. But treating it doesn't fix what's underneath it, and what's underneath it will keep producing symptoms until you find it and deal with it directly.

I've been guilty of this too. Early in my career I had a team where productivity numbers started slipping. My first instinct was that we needed clearer goals, so I spent time building out a goal-tracking system that we all agreed on and started reviewing weekly. The numbers improved slightly for about six weeks. Then they went back to where they were.

It took me longer than it should have to figure out that the problem wasn't goal clarity. One of my senior people had started actively disengaging, and they were influential enough that their energy was pulling the rest of the team down with them. The goal-tracking system was fine. It was solving for a world where motivation was the bottleneck. Motivation wasn't the bottleneck.

That was a painful lesson, and it was expensive in time and credibility. But it taught me something I've carried into every problem I've worked on since: the most visible explanation is almost never the real one.

## What Happens When You Stop Asking Too Early

The pressure to act on a problem, especially a visible one, is enormous. Sitting with uncertainty feels like inaction, and inaction feels like poor leadership. So we grab the most plausible explanation, we run with it, and we generate motion. Motion feels like progress.

Taiichi Ohno at Toyota figured out a better approach. The core idea is disarmingly simple: when a problem appears, ask why it happened. Then take that answer and ask why again. Keep going, five levels deep if you have to, until you've reached something you can actually change.

The genius of the approach isn't the number five. It's the discipline of not stopping when you hit an explanation that feels satisfying. Most people stop at the first explanation that fits. The 5 Whys forces you past it.

In practice the chain usually looks something like this: you have a problem, and the obvious explanation is just a description of the problem in different words. A process is slow because it's taking too long. A team is missing targets because results are below expectations. Those aren't causes, they're restatements. You have to push past them to get anywhere useful.

The question that does the most work, in my experience, is this one: "What changed recently?" Most acute problems have a triggering event. Something shifted. A policy was adjusted. A tool was swapped out. A person left. A priority was reorganized. Stable systems don't suddenly break without cause, and asking what changed puts you on the trail of that cause faster than almost anything else.

## Getting to the Real Thing

Once you've run the chain and landed on a root cause, there's a litmus test worth applying before you commit to anything. Ask yourself: if we fixed this today, would the problem go away?

It sounds obvious. But a lot of explanations that feel solid collapse under that question. If the answer is "probably, but we'd still have some of the issue," you haven't found the root cause yet, you've found a contributing factor. Keep going.

The other thing I've learned to do is go talk to the people who are closest to the broken thing before I decide I understand it. The person who can see the real cause most clearly is almost never in the room where the problem is being discussed. It's the frontline person who runs the process every day. It's the rep who gets the customer calls. It's the IC who's been working around the same obstacle for eight months without telling anyone because they assumed leadership already knew.

Going to those people feels like slowing down. It is slowing down, but it's the kind of slowing down that saves you from spending three weeks building the wrong solution.

## The Uncomfortable Answer

Sometimes root cause analysis leads you somewhere that's hard to say out loud. The real cause is a leadership decision that nobody wants to revisit. The real cause is a person in a senior role who shouldn't be there. The real cause is a structural problem that would require organizational change to fix.

Finding the real cause is still worth doing. Knowing what's actually broken, even when you can't immediately fix it, changes how you operate around it. It keeps you from investing resources in solutions that can't work. It keeps you honest with yourself about what you're managing.

The worst version of this pattern is finding the real cause, deciding it's too hard to address, and then quietly solving for something safer while telling yourself it's the same thing. That's how organizations end up with beautiful documentation and persistent dysfunction. Someone found the real problem, looked away, and built a checklist.

Before you finalize any diagnosis, try this: write the root cause in one plain sentence, then ask what would have to be true for that sentence to be wrong. If you can't think of anything, you haven't stress-tested it enough. If you can, go check those things before you build anything around your conclusion.

The extra hour of asking uncomfortable questions is almost always worth more than the first week of executing on the wrong answer.
