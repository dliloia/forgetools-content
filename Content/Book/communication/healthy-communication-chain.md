## The Same Problem, Again

Six months after a project failure that cost the company real money and real people, the VP stood up in a retrospective and said something I've heard in some version at least a dozen times across my career: "We need to make sure we never let this happen again."

I believed her. She meant it. The problem was that I had been in a room with her direct reports eight months before that meeting, and one of them had described the exact risk, almost word for word, that had eventually materialized into the failure they were now dissecting. The lesson existed. It had been learned. It just hadn't traveled anywhere.

That's the pattern. Not that organizations fail to learn. They learn constantly, at the line level, in the field, in the conversations that happen between people who are actually doing the work. The problem is that the learning doesn't move. It stays where it was generated, and so the people who could act on it never get it, and six or twelve months later someone at the top rediscovers the same problem and mistakes it for a new one.

I spent a long time thinking this was an information problem. Give people better tools. Better dashboards. Better reporting cadences. More data flowing into the room where decisions get made. That's what most organizations try first. It rarely works, because the issue was never a lack of information. It was a broken chain between the people who had the information and the people who needed it.

The chain I'm talking about is the communication channel from the front line to the executive layer, and back. It runs through every layer of management in between. And in most organizations I've seen, it is somewhere between partially clogged and almost completely nonfunctional, held together by a few specific people who have figured out on their own that transmitting signal is part of their job.

The ones who haven't figured that out are doing something different. They're curating. They're filtering. Not usually out of malice, but because the organizational culture they're operating in creates social pressure to report good news upward. To round up the forecast. To soften the problem before handing it to the boss. Every layer of management adds a little compression to the signal, and by the time the information reaches the person with the authority to act, it barely resembles the reality that originated it.

Status meetings are the most visible version of this problem. The format is so normalized that most people don't even recognize it as a dysfunction. Everyone goes around the table, everyone says some version of "on track," the meeting ends. Two months later something is very much not on track and everyone acts surprised. But if you had been watching carefully, you would have seen the signs at the line level weeks earlier. You just weren't asking the right questions.

That's the actual job of the manager in those rooms. Not to listen to updates. To ask specific, uncomfortable questions that give people permission to surface real information. Not "how are things going?" but "what's the one thing most likely to go wrong with this that we haven't talked about?" Those are different questions. They create different conditions. The first one invites performance. The second one invites honesty.

Most managers don't ask the second kind of question, because it requires tolerating tension. Sitting with bad news. Sometimes hearing things that reflect poorly on decisions you made. That discomfort is not incidental to the job. It is the job. The manager who can only hear good news is not actually managing, they're presiding over a performance.

Skip-level meetings are the other mechanism that almost nobody uses well. The idea is sound: if you're a director or above, you should be periodically talking directly to people two layers below you to get an unfiltered view of what's happening. In practice, most skip-levels are just another opportunity for people to perform wellness upward at a higher altitude. The person across the table knows they're talking to someone with real authority over their career, and they're going to manage that conversation accordingly.

The fix is to go in with specific questions. Not "tell me what's on your mind," but "what are you seeing on the ground that you think I'm probably not hearing?" Not "how's the team doing?" but "if you were in my seat, what would you change first?" Those questions are uncomfortable to ask because they imply you might be missing something. You almost certainly are. That's why you're in the room.

I've had skip-level conversations that completely rewired my understanding of what was happening on a team. Not because the person I was talking to was unusually forthcoming, but because I had created a condition where being forthcoming felt safe and worth the risk. When that happens, you often learn that the official narrative and the actual situation share very little overlap. And that gap, between what leadership believes is happening and what is actually happening, is exactly where repeat failures live.

There's a translation dimension to this that I don't think gets discussed enough. The manager's job runs in two directions. Pushing context down: here's the strategy, here's why we made this call, here's what the leadership team is trying to do. And pulling signal up: here's what my team is actually experiencing, here's the risk I'm watching, here's the lesson from last quarter that I don't want to lose. Most managers I've worked with are decent at the first direction and poor at the second. The second is harder, because it requires you to synthesize messy, sometimes uncomfortable information into something coherent enough to act on. But if you're doing only one direction, you're doing half the job.

The last piece is postmortems. After something goes wrong, most organizations hold one. People gather, they talk through what happened, they generate action items. And then, in the vast majority of cases, those action items disappear. Not because anyone decided to ignore them. Because "improve cross-team communication" was listed with no owner, no deadline, and no follow-up mechanism. That's not an action item. That's a sentence.

Lessons travel when someone is specifically responsible for carrying them. When there's a named owner for each insight, a clear deadline, and a check-in scheduled before the next quarter. Without that structure, the postmortem just documents a problem you're going to have again.

The organizations that get smarter over time aren't the ones with the best retrospective formats or the most sophisticated tracking tools. They're the ones where every manager, at every level, treats the upward flow of real information as a core part of the job. Where skip-levels surface signal instead of theater. Where status meetings are driven by specific questions instead of open mics. Where lessons have owners.

Build that chain deliberately. Maintain it actively. The alternative is rediscovery, and it will cost you more than the chain ever would.
