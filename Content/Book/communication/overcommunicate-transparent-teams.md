## The Gap Between the Decision and the People Who Live With It

Early in my management career, I made a mistake I've watched hundreds of managers make since.

I got good at making decisions. I got bad at explaining them.

I thought that was fine. Decisions are what managers are for. You weigh the information, you make the call, you move forward. That's the job. The team's job is to execute. My job was to point in the right direction.

What I didn't understand yet was how much it costs the people doing the work when they have no visibility into why the direction keeps changing.

I had an engineering team early on. Smart people, genuinely talented, working hard on something they'd been asked to build. Months into it, the business priorities shifted. We needed to change course. The original approach wasn't going to scale the way we needed it to. The right call, technically and strategically, was to stop what we were doing and start over with a different architecture.

I delivered that news in about four sentences. "We're changing direction. The new approach is X. Here's the new spec. Let me know if you have questions."

I thought I was being efficient. I was being careless.

What happened over the next two weeks was a slow leak. Energy dropped. People stopped volunteering ideas in meetings. One of my best engineers, someone who had been proactively sending me articles and flagging problems before they became problems, went quiet. When I finally checked in with him one-on-one, he said something I've never forgotten: "I just don't know what the point is anymore. We build things and then they disappear."

He wasn't wrong. I just hadn't given him the information he needed to understand why disappearing was sometimes the right outcome.

So I tried something. I called a short team meeting and I actually explained it. Not a summary. The real version. I told them what the scaling problem was going to look like. I told them when we would have hit it and what it would have cost us to fix it at that stage. I told them that the decision to pivot now was specifically made to protect them from a much worse problem later. I also told them that I should have explained this from the beginning, and that I was sorry I hadn't.

The room shifted. Not dramatically. Nobody started clapping. But you could feel something settle.

One of the engineers said: "Okay, that actually makes sense. I just thought nobody had thought it through."

That sentence stayed with me. I just thought nobody had thought it through.

That's what silence communicates. Not efficiency, not confidence. Carelessness. When you announce a decision without the reasoning, the message people receive is that the reasoning either doesn't exist or isn't worth their time.

Neither of those is the message you want to send.

I started changing how I operated after that. Not in a dramatic, overhauled-my-whole-system way. In a small, consistent, habitual way. Before I communicated any significant decision or change, I added one step: I asked myself whether the person receiving this had the context they needed to understand it. If the answer was no, I added the context before I sent it.

That's it. That was the whole change.

What I noticed over the following months was subtle at first and then unmistakable. People started engaging more in discussions about direction. They started pushing back constructively instead of going quiet and seething. When things changed, and things always change, the reactions were different. Less resignation. More problem-solving.

Because people were no longer guessing at the why. I was telling them.

There's something that happens when you explain your reasoning to the people doing the work. It signals several things at once. It signals that you respect their intelligence. It signals that you see them as participants in the mission, not just executors of tasks. It signals that when hard calls get made, someone was actually thinking carefully rather than just reacting.

And it gives people something they desperately need when their work gets changed or scrapped or deprioritized: a way to make sense of it that isn't "we don't matter."

Because that's the story people tell themselves in the absence of information. It's almost never accurate. But it's the story that costs you their best work, their engagement, and eventually their presence.

I've talked to a lot of people about why they left jobs they were otherwise good at. The reasons vary on the surface. But underneath most of them is some version of the same thing: I felt like I was just executing orders. I didn't understand what we were actually trying to do. I felt like the decisions being made around me were arbitrary, and nobody thought I deserved to know why.

You can't build real commitment on that foundation. People will do the work. They'll hit the deliverables. But they'll do it with a part of themselves held back, always scanning for somewhere they'll feel less like a resource and more like a person.

Overcommunication, as a habit, closes that gap. Not perfectly. Not in every situation. But consistently enough that the people working with you know that when something changes, there's a reason, and they're going to hear it.

That's not a small thing. That's the difference between a team that's present and a team that's already halfway out the door.
