## One Unit of You Is Not Enough

Early in my career, I thought being indispensable was the goal. If I was the one who knew how to do it best, I'd always have a seat at the table. Nobody could cut me. Nobody could route around me. I held onto tasks the way some people hold onto money: tightly, privately, a little anxiously.

It took me an embarrassingly long time to understand what that posture was actually costing.

The math is simple, and I wish someone had drawn it out for me when I was twenty-five. If you do a task yourself, your organization gets one unit of output. Everything runs through you. Your hours, your bandwidth, your focus on any given Tuesday. The ceiling on that work is you.

Now hand that same task to someone who can do it at half your level. Suddenly there are 1.5 units in play. Yours and half of theirs. You didn't work more hours. You didn't hire anyone new. You just stopped treating your skill as something to protect and started treating it as something to spread.

And here's where it gets interesting. That person doesn't stay at 50% forever. If you give them reps and real feedback and enough room to fail without getting rescued, they move. They go to 60, then 70, then one day they're doing the thing better than you ever did it. At that point, you've become a fraction of them. You built something that exceeds you.

Most managers hear that and feel threatened. The good ones hear it and feel like they finally got something right.

I want to tell you a story about pottery. I'll be upfront: I can't verify it's literally true. But I've seen the principle it illustrates play out so many times in real teams that the literal truth of the story matters less than what it shows.

Two groups of students. Same assignment, same time limit. Group One was told to pursue perfection. Take as long as you need on each bowl. Move on only when it meets your standard. Quality is everything. Group Two was told to iterate. Volume is the goal. Make as many bowls as you can. Go fast, go often, don't overthink it.

You'd bet on Group One, right? They were focused. Patient. Intentional.

They lost.

Group One produced a small number of technically beautiful bowls. Group Two produced a mountain of terrible ones. But buried in that mountain were more genuinely good bowls than Group One managed in total. Not slightly more. Considerably more.

The reason is reps. Group Two failed faster and therefore learned faster. Every bad bowl was data. Every iteration adjusted their hands a little. Perfectionism didn't elevate Group One. It protected them from the feedback loop that actually creates skill.

Replace bowls with projects. Replace students with your direct reports. Nothing changes.

The managers I watched struggle most were the ones who told themselves they'd delegate when their team was ready. It sounds responsible. It isn't. Readiness doesn't arrive on its own. It's built through experience, and experience requires being given something real to attempt and being allowed to fall short of it.

I managed a young analyst once who was sharp but slow. Everything she turned in was careful and considered and took twice as long as it needed to. I kept giving her work anyway, kept shortening her deadlines slightly, kept asking her to prioritize speed on the first draft and refine from there. She hated it for about three months. Then something clicked. She started seeing the first draft as a tool rather than a deliverable. Her work got faster and it stayed good. Eighteen months later she was running a process I used to own, doing it better than I had.

That didn't happen because I waited until she was ready. It happened because I gave her reps before she felt ready, and I didn't step in every time she stumbled.

This is the thing that managers who hold work too close never quite reckon with: every time you take a task back because someone did it at 70% of your standard, you've made two quiet decisions. You've decided the organization will only ever get your output on that work. And you've decided that person will plateau right where they are. You didn't mean to make those decisions. But behavior is a choice whether you're conscious of it or not.

The practical cost compounds fast. Your hours are finite. Every hour you spend doing work someone else could handle at 70% is an hour you didn't spend on the things only you can do. Strategy. Hiring. Building the systems that make everything else run better. You're trading high-leverage time for low-leverage work and telling yourself it's about standards. It's not about standards. It's about control, and control at that level is expensive.

The managers who built the best teams I've ever been around all had a specific quality in common. They measured their own success by what their people could do without them. Not what they personally produced, but what they enabled. They hired for potential, gave people harder work than felt comfortable, coached the gap without taking the task back, and tracked improvement over quarters, not days.

And they genuinely wanted their people to surpass them. Not as a performance of generosity. As an actual goal. Because a team where multiple people exceed the manager on specific tasks is a team that doesn't collapse when the manager is absent. It's a team with real depth. It's a team that scales.

One unit of you is the floor of what your team should produce. The ceiling is determined by how many people you're willing to trust with real work, how much failure you're willing to tolerate in the short term, and how seriously you take the job of making yourself unnecessary on the tasks that someone else can own.

That last part is the whole job, honestly. Making yourself unnecessary, one delegation at a time, so you can keep moving to the work that only you can do right now. And then making yourself unnecessary there too.
