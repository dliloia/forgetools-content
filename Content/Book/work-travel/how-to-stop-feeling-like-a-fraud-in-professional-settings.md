## The Rooms That Make You Smaller

The first time I sat in a meeting with people who were genuinely more senior, more polished, and more credentialed than I was, I did something that wasted about forty-five minutes of my life: I spent the entire meeting trying to figure out if anyone could tell.

Could they tell I was nervous? Could they tell I wasn't sure I should be there? Could they tell that some of the acronyms they were using were floating about two feet above my head?

They could not tell. I know this because I've now been on both sides of that dynamic enough times to understand something that took me an embarrassingly long time to learn. The room is not paying attention to you the way you think it is. It's too busy managing its own version of the same anxiety.

That insight sounds simple, maybe even obvious in hindsight. But when you're twenty-six and sitting across from someone who's been doing this since before you graduated high school, it doesn't feel obvious. It feels like a test you haven't studied for.

I grew up working. Started at thirteen, put myself through college, never had the luxury of a long runway between "doesn't really matter" and "this is real now." That meant I was in rooms that felt too big for me earlier than most people, and I stayed in them longer than was comfortable, because I didn't have the option to step back and wait until I felt ready. Feeling ready, I eventually figured out, is mostly a myth anyway.

What I noticed over years of being in those rooms is that the people who looked comfortable were often performing comfort. The VP who walked into a room like he owned it, the woman across the table who seemed to have an effortless answer for every question, the executive who laughed easily and took up space without apology. I assumed they felt what they projected. Sometimes they did. Often they didn't.

The projection itself is a skill, and it's a learnable one, but I think we've oversold it as the solution. Performing confidence without developing any real relationship to the discomfort just means you're running on borrowed energy, and it runs out.

What actually works is simpler and less glamorous.

You stop trying to prove you belong and you start trying to be useful. That shift, as small as it sounds, changes everything about how you show up. When you're trying to prove belonging, you're in your head, you're monitoring yourself, you're crafting rather than contributing. When you're trying to be useful, you're paying attention to what's actually happening in the room and looking for where you have something real to add.

Useful people are remembered. Impressive people are assessed.

There was a dinner early in my career, maybe a dozen people, mix of senior leaders and a few people at my level. I was underprepared socially, if that makes sense. I didn't know the players, I didn't know the history, and I had maybe fifteen minutes to figure out the dynamics before we sat down. I made a decision at some point during that dinner to stop trying to keep up with the conversation and start asking better questions. I asked someone at the table to walk me through how they thought about a problem I'd been wrestling with. I listened. I asked a follow-up. I listened again.

At the end of the night, that person sought me out to continue the conversation. He didn't know I'd been nervous. He just knew that I'd actually heard him.

That's the thing about a good question asked with genuine curiosity. It takes the attention off you and puts it somewhere more interesting, and it turns out people find that refreshing in a world full of people waiting to talk.

The other piece, and this one I think matters more as the stakes get higher, is the accumulation problem. The discomfort of intimidating rooms compounds if you avoid them. Every time you skip the dinner, decline the invitation, hang back at the edge of the conference, the next version of the same situation feels harder. You haven't built any evidence that you can handle it, so your nervous system treats it as a fresh threat every single time.

The evidence base has to be built through actual exposure. Not reckless exposure, not throwing yourself into situations you have no business being in yet, but enough consistent presence in rooms that stretch you that your brain eventually starts to file them as familiar rather than dangerous.

I've been in rooms that scared me more times than I can count. Hiring loops at companies with brutal standards, executive reviews where I was the youngest person in the building, client meetings where I knew less about the industry than everyone else at the table. What I know now is that the inventory I do after those situations matters as much as the situations themselves.

Did I contribute something real? Did anyone see through me as a fraud? What actually happened versus what I was afraid would happen?

The honest answers, taken together over years, build a different kind of confidence than the performed kind. Not "I'm great and everyone can tell," but "I've been in rooms like this before, and I was fine." That's enough. That's actually enough to keep moving.

The feeling doesn't disappear entirely. I don't think it's supposed to. What changes is how much space it takes up and how much it slows you down.

Eventually it becomes background noise. You know it's there. You've seen it be wrong before. And you walk into the room anyway.
