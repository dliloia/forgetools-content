## How You Show Up When Nobody's Grading You

There's a particular kind of clarity that only comes from watching someone operate outside a controlled environment. I learned this early, and it changed how I evaluate people.

When I was hiring and developing leaders, I could learn a lot from an interview. I could learn more from a performance review. But I could learn the most from watching someone on a work trip: at the dinner table with clients, in the hotel lobby waiting for an Uber, navigating a delayed flight with the rest of the team. Those unscripted moments told me things about judgment and character that no structured process ever could.

Most people don't realize this is happening. They're careful at the formal dinner and then they relax completely in the hallway, and that's where I'd see the person they actually were. How they talked about colleagues who weren't in the room. Whether they treated the hotel staff with basic courtesy. Whether they dominated every conversation or were genuinely curious about other people. Small things, high signal.

The drinking advice is the one that circulates most because it produces the most visible failures. And yes, it matters. Two drinks is a number that holds for almost any professional setting. Beyond that, judgment starts to erode in ways that are invisible to the person experiencing it and completely visible to everyone else. I've watched more than a few people say something at a work dinner that they'd never have said sober, something that seemed fine in the moment and cast a long shadow afterward.

But I want to focus on something less discussed, because the drinking part is obvious. The harder thing is what happens in the low-stakes moments.

I remember a trip I took years ago with a team I was building. We had a full day of client meetings followed by a group dinner. One of my newer managers had been excellent in the meetings, well-prepared, sharp, asked good questions. I was genuinely impressed.

Then at dinner, he spent most of the meal on his phone, gave one-word answers when addressed, and visibly checked out the moment the formal agenda wrapped up. He thought the day was over. What he didn't realize was that the dinner was where the client was actually forming their impression of our team, and of him specifically. That client mentioned it to me later, not with malice, just as an observation. "Your guy seemed distracted."

It cost that manager credibility it took months to rebuild.

The table dynamics on a work trip are not incidental to the trip. They are the trip. Who's being heard, who's getting talked over, how the senior person sets the tone, whether the energy of the table is collaborative or performative. Reading all of that, and adjusting to it in real time, is what separates someone who's just technically competent from someone who's ready for broader responsibility.

I used to tell people: your job at a work dinner is not to have a good time. It's to make the people around you feel like they're having a good time while you stay present and sharp. That sounds like a lot of work, and sometimes it is. But it becomes natural once you internalize the actual purpose of those evenings. The formal meeting is where you prove your competence. The dinner is where you prove your character.

Preparation is the other piece that separates the people who get remembered from the ones who blend in. If you're traveling to meet someone, know who they are before you walk in the door. Know what they care about. Have a perspective that's actually yours, not a recitation of talking points. The most striking thing in a client or partner meeting isn't a polished deck. It's a person with genuine curiosity and informed opinions who's clearly done the work.

And after the trip, follow through. This is where most people lose the gains they made in person. Send a specific note, not a generic "great to meet you" email. Reference something real from the conversation. If you said you'd send a resource or make an introduction, do it within 24 hours. The world is full of people who make a good impression in person and then disappear. You can distinguish yourself from that group with one thoughtful email.

The overall standard for professional travel isn't complicated, but it requires you to stay switched on longer than feels natural. You are representing yourself, your team, and your organization in a compressed, unstructured environment where judgment forms quickly and tends to stick. The floor is don't embarrass yourself. The ceiling is walking away from every trip with stronger relationships, a better reputation, and a clearer sense of what you're capable of when the environment is unpredictable.

That ceiling is reachable. Most people just don't aim for it.
