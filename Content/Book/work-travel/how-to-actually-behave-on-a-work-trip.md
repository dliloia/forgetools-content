## How to Actually Behave on a Work Trip

I've been on a lot of work trips. Long hauls to conferences where I knew maybe four people in a room of three hundred. Offsites where the whole point was two days of intensive conversation with people I'd otherwise only ever see in a Zoom grid. Quick one-nighters to close a deal or fix a relationship that had frayed over email.

And I can tell you with confidence that the version of "how to behave" that most people get before these trips is almost entirely focused on avoiding disaster. Don't drink too much. Don't say anything you'll regret. Don't be the story people tell on the plane home. That's not wrong advice, but it's the floor, and you can clear the floor without really trying.

The people I've seen come back from work trips with genuine gains, stronger relationships, more organizational credibility, actual momentum on things they'd been trying to move for months, they were doing something deliberate. They weren't just avoiding the obvious pitfalls. They were treating the trip as a real professional opportunity, and they prepared accordingly.

Here's what that looked like in practice.

The first thing that separates them is their understanding of when the clock is running. At home base, the boundaries are reasonably clear. You're at work, or you're not. On a trip, those boundaries collapse completely. You're having breakfast next to someone who signs your expense reports. You're in a shuttle with a peer you've been in conflict with over priorities for six months, and you've got twenty minutes and nowhere else to be. You're in an elevator with someone two levels above you, and that forty-five seconds is the entire relationship you get to build that week.

The people who understand this don't become exhausting. They don't perform enthusiasm at 7am. But they're present. They put the phone down. They ask a real question. They remember that every informal moment is where people decide whether they actually like working with you, and the meeting room is just where they confirm what they've already decided.

The second thing is preparation. I've been in rooms where it was obvious someone had flown a long distance to be there and hadn't done the basic homework. They were winging a conversation they could have owned with thirty minutes of prep the night before. And maybe they held their own, maybe it was fine, but everyone in that room knew they'd traveled to get there. The implied stakes are higher. Showing up flat signals that you either didn't care or didn't think it was worth the effort.

Before a significant trip, I want to know who's going to be in the room and what they're working on. I want to understand the problems the team I'm meeting is trying to solve. If there's a senior leader I don't know well, I want to know their background and where they've been focused. It takes ten minutes and it changes the quality of every conversation you have, because you're not starting from zero and asking obvious questions. You're starting from some actual context, and the people across from you can feel the difference.

The alcohol conversation is worth having more carefully than the standard advice allows. "Don't drink too much" is technically correct and practically useless, because the people who drink too much on work trips already know they shouldn't. The more useful thing is the pre-commitment: decide before you walk into the dinner what your ceiling is, and don't negotiate with yourself in the moment. In-the-moment negotiation is exactly how those decisions go wrong. You feel fine, someone offers another round, and the part of your brain tracking the 7am breakfast meeting with the VP goes quiet. That's not a failure of character. That's how it works. The defense is deciding beforehand, not relying on willpower after two drinks.

Sleep is almost completely absent from how people think about work trips, and it shouldn't be. A two-day offsite where you're going hard every night is a two-day offsite where you're degraded by the afternoon of day two. Your judgment softens. Your listening softens. You make the kind of conversational choices you'd never make rested: slightly too blunt, slightly too much disclosure, slightly too loose with something that deserved more care. And the compounding is real. One late night is recoverable. Two in a row on a trip that matters is a risk most people don't consciously take but take anyway because the social pull is strong and the energy of those trips is genuinely exciting.

Protecting one good night of sleep on any multi-day trip is a discipline move. "I'm going to call it a night" is a complete sentence. You don't owe an explanation.

The thing I think about most on work trips, though, is the relationship opportunity with people outside my immediate circle. These trips are one of the few times you're physically in the same space as people you'd never organically connect with day-to-day. The peer in a different function you've seen in all-hands but never actually talked to. The person from the regional office who's been a name on distribution lists for two years. The newer hire who's still figuring out where they fit and would genuinely benefit from someone investing five minutes in them.

Most people default to their existing network. They sit with their team at dinner, they grab coffee with their manager, they congregate with the people they already know because it's comfortable. And they leave almost all of the real opportunity sitting there.

Some of the most important professional relationships I've built started in exactly this kind of setting. Not at a networking event with a deliberate agenda, but over a long dinner or a slow morning, in the kind of unstructured time that doesn't exist in normal work life, where there was actual space for a real conversation. The key is genuine curiosity. Not a business-card exchange, not a positioning conversation. Just actually being interested in the person across from you and letting them feel that.

The last thing is intention. Before you travel, think about what you want to come back with. Not a vague sense of "I want it to go well." Something specific: a relationship you want to have deepened, a question you've been unable to get answered over email, a decision you want to drive to closure while you have people in the same room. And then follow through when you land. If you promised to send something, send it within 48 hours. If a conversation sparked something, write it down while it's fresh.

The trip is an investment. What you do after it matters as much as what you did during it.
