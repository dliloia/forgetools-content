## The Meeting That Belongs to Someone Else

I learned early in my career that I was terrible at 1:1s.

Not because I skipped them. I was disciplined about the calendar. Every week, thirty minutes, direct report across the desk. I showed up. I was present. I thought I was doing the thing.

What I was actually doing was running a weekly status meeting with an audience of one.

I had my list. Projects I needed updates on. Decisions I needed their input for. Things I'd forgotten to communicate in the team meeting. I would work through that list efficiently, ask "anything else?" and move on with my day. I thought I was a good manager. I was organized. I followed through. People generally liked working for me.

But I didn't know them. Not really.

I didn't know what worried them at night. I didn't know which parts of their job they found genuinely energizing and which parts quietly drained them. I didn't know where they wanted to be in five years or whether I was helping or accidentally hindering that path. I was managing deliverables, not people.

The shift happened when one of my best employees resigned.

She was talented, reliable, the kind of person you build a team around. When she sat down to tell me she was leaving, I did what most managers do. I asked if it was about the money. She shook her head. I asked if there was something about the team. She shook her head again. Then she said something I've never forgotten: "I don't think you ever really knew what I wanted. And I don't think you knew you didn't know."

That landed. Hard.

She was right. I had no idea what she wanted. Not from her role, not from her career, not from me as a manager. We had met every single week for fourteen months and I couldn't have told you her two-year goal. I couldn't have named the part of her job she found most draining. I didn't know whether she felt like she was growing.

I had been using our 1:1 time to manage my own needs, not hers.

After that conversation, I started building what eventually became the structure I use and teach today. The core insight is embarrassingly simple: a 1:1 is the employee's meeting. The manager is a guest. The moment you accept that, the format almost builds itself.

Here's what it looks like. Thirty minutes, three segments, ten minutes each.

The first ten minutes are personal. Not "how's the project going" personal. Actually personal. How are you doing? What's been heavy lately? What are you looking forward to? The goal is not to play therapist or to pry. The goal is to pay attention to the person sitting across from you as a full human being, not just as a function on your org chart.

This sounds obvious. It is not common.

I once managed a software engineer who was, by every external measure, performing well. Clean code, meeting deadlines, never a complaint. During one of our early personal segments he mentioned, almost in passing, that he was going through a divorce and hadn't been sleeping. Six weeks later his output started slipping. I had context. I knew what was happening and roughly why. So instead of a performance conversation, we had an honest one. I gave him some flexibility. I checked in more frequently. He got through a brutal few months and came out the other side. He told me later that the thing that kept him from quitting during that period was knowing his manager actually knew what was going on in his life.

That doesn't happen without the first ten minutes.

The second ten minutes belong to their work. Not your version of their work. Their experience of it. What's feeling energizing? What are they stuck on? What are they avoiding? What would make the next two weeks easier?

This is not a status update. You have a dozen other channels for that. This is about understanding how your employee is experiencing their work, not just whether the outputs are on track. A person can be technically performing and quietly checked out. The gap between those two things shows up here before it shows up anywhere else.

When I started running this segment consistently, I noticed that I was catching problems four to six weeks earlier than I had before. Not because people had been hiding things, but because they finally had a reliable, low-stakes space to surface them. The question "what are you most stuck on?" sounds simple. But asked consistently, by a manager who actually listens to the answer, it becomes a signal that you are safe to be honest with.

The third ten minutes are about growth. Where do they want to go? What do they want to learn? What opportunity have they been hoping to get access to? What's one thing you could do differently to help them develop faster?

This is the segment most managers never get to. Partly because they've used the time on their own agenda, and partly because growth conversations feel abstract or uncomfortable. But skipping this segment is how you lose your best people without ever seeing it coming.

The math is simple. Ten minutes a week on growth is roughly eight hours a year of intentional development conversation. That's eight hours more than most employees ever get from their managers. And that difference is felt. People know when a manager is invested in their future and when they're not. They make career decisions accordingly.

The rule that holds all three segments together is the hardest one to accept.

Your agenda is not in this meeting.

Not ever. Not even a little. The updates you need, the things you need to communicate, the decisions you need input on: those happen everywhere else. In the team meeting. In Slack. In a quick five-minute conversation before the day starts. The 1:1 is the one protected space in your entire working relationship that belongs completely to your employee.

The moment you slide your agenda into it, you change the nature of the meeting. Your employee feels it. They start calibrating their answers to what they think you want to hear. The candor that only exists in a genuinely safe space quietly disappears. You'll still have the meeting. It just won't do what you need it to do.

I've told managers this and watched their faces. The resistance is immediate. "But what about all the things I need to cover?" Cover them somewhere else. Protect this space. The discipline of keeping your own needs out of this thirty-minute window is, in my experience, one of the clearest signals of whether someone is a good manager or just a manager.

The first few weeks using this structure feel a little uncomfortable. Your employees aren't used to it either. They'll give short answers. They'll wait for you to fill the silence with your usual agenda items. Stay the course.

By month two, something shifts. They start arriving with things they've been thinking about. The conversation gets richer. You start learning things about them that change how you manage them and how you develop them.

By month four, you've built something that is genuinely hard to replicate: a manager-employee relationship grounded in real trust and real knowledge of who that person is and what they need.

That's what the meeting is for. Not your list. Theirs.
