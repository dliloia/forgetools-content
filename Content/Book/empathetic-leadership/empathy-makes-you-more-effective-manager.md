## The Most Misunderstood Tool in Management

Somewhere along the way, empathy became soft.

Not by definition, by association. It got bundled into management training programs alongside phrases like "vulnerability-based trust" and "radical candor," and after enough of that, every manager who considers themselves a serious operator started tuning it out. Too many feelings. Not enough numbers. Let the HR team handle it.

I understand that reaction. I had a version of it myself early in my career. I was young, I was trying to prove I could drive results, and anything that sounded like a therapy session felt like a distraction from the actual work. So I managed people the way I thought effective managers were supposed to. Clear expectations. Direct feedback. High accountability. Minimal hand-holding.

The results were mixed, and it took me longer than I'd like to admit to figure out why.

The managers I watched who consistently got the most out of their teams weren't the hardest ones. They weren't the most demanding or the ones with the tightest processes. They were the ones who seemed to know their people in a way that went past job title and performance metrics. They knew what motivated each person. They knew when someone was struggling before it showed up in output. They knew which feedback to deliver directly and which required a longer runway.

That's not warmth. That's modeling. And it's what I eventually realized I'd been missing.

Here's the simplest way I know to explain it. Every person on your team has a context you don't have full access to. Their relationship with authority, shaped long before they met you. Their career fears. The thing happening at home that they're trying not to let affect their work. Their mental model of what "doing a good job" actually looks like. Their theory of what you think of them.

All of that context shapes how they receive information, make decisions, and perform under pressure. If you don't have access to it, you're managing a version of them that isn't quite real. You're optimizing for signals and not for the actual system.

I had a person on my team who was technically very strong and consistently underperformed in cross-functional settings. Meetings with stakeholders, collaborative projects with adjacent teams, anything that required negotiating shared priorities. My initial read was that they were difficult, maybe a little arrogant. I started thinking about whether they were actually a fit for the role.

Then I had a real conversation with them. Not a status check. A conversation where I asked what it was like to work in those cross-functional environments, what felt hard about it. It turned out they were operating from a prior job where they'd been burned badly by a stakeholder who took credit for their work and left them exposed. They'd built up defensive behavior as a protection mechanism. They weren't being difficult, they were being careful in the only way they knew how.

Once I understood that, I could actually help them. We worked on it directly. I gave them more context on the stakeholders before they went into those settings. I debriefed with them after. Within a few months, the behavior had shifted substantially, and they went on to be one of the stronger cross-functional operators on the team.

If I had managed that situation based on my initial read, I would have moved them out of a role they were capable of excelling in, and I would have done it confidently, because the data supported my conclusion. The data just wasn't complete.

That's the problem empathy solves. Not the niceness problem. The accuracy problem.

I've thought a lot about why this doesn't get taught as a business skill more often, and I think it comes down to measurement. The ROI of empathy doesn't show up on a single dashboard. It shows up over time in retention numbers, in the quality of feedback conversations, in how early problems surface, in whether your best people choose to stay when they have options elsewhere.

Those outcomes are real and they compound. But they're hard to attribute to a single behavior change, so organizations don't prioritize it, and managers don't practice it, and then at some point someone great walks into an exit interview and says they left because they never felt like their manager actually saw them.

The other thing I want to say is that empathy doesn't require softening your standards. This is the misconception that causes the most damage. I've seen managers use "being empathetic" as a reason to avoid hard conversations, to let underperformance slide, to delay accountability indefinitely. That's not empathy, that's conflict avoidance with better branding.

The managers I've admired most in my career held genuinely high standards and also understood their people deeply. Those things reinforce each other. When your team trusts that you see them clearly and still believe in what you're asking of them, the accountability lands differently. They're not defending against your read of the situation. They trust the read is accurate, and they can put their energy into actually responding to the feedback.

That's the version of management worth building toward. Not warmer, better. Not nicer, more accurate.

The people on your team are trying to tell you things all the time. Most managers aren't receiving the signal because they haven't built the relationship that makes it safe to send.

Build that relationship, and the job gets easier in every direction that matters.
