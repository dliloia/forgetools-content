## The Room You're Never In

There's a meeting you're not in. Every organization has one, maybe several. Senior leaders, a conference room, budget on the table, headcount under discussion. Someone raises your team. And the question, spoken or unspoken, is: what exactly are they building over there?

If you haven't been doing the work of communicating your team's vision upward, the answer to that question gets assembled from whatever fragments happen to be available. A support ticket someone escalated three months ago. An offhand comment in a steering committee. A number on a dashboard that nobody fully understands. The picture that emerges may have almost nothing to do with what your team is actually doing, or where they're actually going.

I've been in that room. I've watched teams get cut, reorganized, or deprioritized not because they were failing, but because nobody in the room could articulate what they were building. The work existed. The progress was real. But the story was invisible, and in the absence of a story, people fill in their own.

This is the quiet problem with upward communication. Most managers know they need to manage downward: clarity with their team, context for the work, development conversations, honest feedback. That's well-trodden ground. But managing the narrative upward feels optional, even a little political, and a lot of managers I've worked with over the years were privately uncomfortable with it. They felt like they were doing something self-promotional when they should have been doing real work.

That instinct is understandable and it's wrong.

Communicating your team's vision to leadership is not about you. It is about your team. They deserve a manager who can tell their story clearly in the rooms they never get to enter. If you're not doing that, nobody else will.

The mechanics of it are less complicated than they feel. The first thing is knowing what you're trying to communicate. Vision is not a list of goals or a roadmap. Vision is directional. It answers the question: where are we going, and why does it matter that we get there? If you don't have a clear, honest answer to that question, start there. You can't communicate what you haven't figured out.

Once you have that answer, the translation problem starts. Senior leaders are operating at a level of abstraction that's different from yours. They're thinking about strategy, resource allocation, risk, competitive position. Your team is thinking about the work in front of them. Your job is to bridge those two levels, to show the connection between what your team is building and what leadership is responsible for. That connection is not always obvious, and leadership won't always draw it for themselves. You have to name it explicitly.

I had a conversation once with a senior leader who told me something I've thought about since. She said the managers she trusted most were the ones who could walk into her office and answer three questions without hesitation. Where is your team going? Why does that matter to the business? What do you need from me? She didn't care how long the answer was. She cared whether the manager had thought it through, whether the direction was real and not just aspirational language, and whether they had a clear enough picture to actually ask for something.

That last part is the one most managers miss. Upward communication should almost always end with something actionable. Either a request, a specific decision you need, a resource question, air cover for a difficult change, or a signal for leadership to watch. Tell them what to expect and how they'll know it's working. Give them a role in the story. That's not politics, it's alignment.

Consistency matters more than formality. You don't need a quarterly presentation, though those have their place. You need to find the regular moments where leadership hears the direction, not just the status. A sentence at the start of a one-on-one. A connection you name between your team's work and a priority leadership mentioned in an all-hands. A short written narrative you update every few months and send when new stakeholders get involved. These small, repeated touchpoints accumulate. Over time, they become a very clear picture.

The compounding effect is real. When leadership understands where your team is going and why it matters, they show up for your team in ways that are hard to manufacture any other way. They advocate in the rooms you're not in. They protect resources when things get tight. They flag opportunities that fit your direction. And because they trust the direction, they extend more autonomy, more latitude to figure out how to get there.

Your team feels all of that. They feel the difference between a manager who is running the work and a manager who is fighting for them. That feeling matters more than most managers realize. It's part of why people stay, and part of why they bring their full effort rather than a managed version of it.

The teams that get cut in reorgs are rarely the worst-performing teams. Often they're teams whose work was real but whose story was invisible. Leadership had no narrative to defend, so when the pressure came, there was nothing to hold onto.

You are responsible for that story. Not the work alone, the story of the work and where it's going. Tell it clearly, tell it often, and tell it in terms that connect to what leadership is trying to accomplish.

Your team has earned that much.
