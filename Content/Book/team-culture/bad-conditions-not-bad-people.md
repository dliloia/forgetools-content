There's a pattern I've watched play out more times than I can count, and it took me an embarrassing number of years to fully recognize it in myself.

A team isn't hitting their numbers. Morale is low. Output is inconsistent. The manager pulls the personnel files, looks at performance history, runs through the roster in their head, and arrives at a conclusion: wrong people. Too slow, not motivated, not talented enough. The solution, therefore, is to change the people.

Sometimes that's the right answer. But more often than not, when I've dug into a struggling team, what I've actually found is a capable group of people operating inside conditions that were quietly preventing them from succeeding. And nobody had stopped to ask whether the conditions were the problem.

I managed a team once that was consistently missing handle time targets on customer calls. The coaching had been going on for months. We were reviewing calls, running training sessions, giving feedback. Nothing moved. Then someone, I don't remember who first had the idea, actually walked through the workflow with a rep in real time, watching every step. Turns out the reps were spending several minutes per call on a manual lookup process that, with a single system change, could have been handled automatically. The people weren't inefficient. The process was broken. We had been coaching people to compensate for a problem that coaching could never fix.

That's the thing about broken conditions. They hide. They look like underperformance. They look like a skills gap, or a motivation problem, or a hiring mistake. The feedback loops are slow, and the instinct to attribute performance problems to the people is fast, so organizations keep doing it, keep cycling through talent, keep wondering why the numbers don't move.

The question I've learned to ask first is: what would better conditions have made possible here?

It's a genuinely uncomfortable question if you're the one who built or inherited the conditions. Because the honest answer is often: a lot. If the tools fit the workflow, you get time back in every person's day. If there's a clear escalation path, you get better decisions at the edges. If onboarding actually reflects the current state of the product, you get faster ramp times and fewer early exits. These aren't soft benefits. They're calculable. You can put numbers on them, and when you do, you often find that the team was producing less than they could have been, not because of who they were, but because of what you handed them to work with.

The default to blame is understandable, even if it's wrong. Changing a person is visible action. Auditing a process, rebuilding a workflow, redesigning how a new hire gets integrated into a team: all of that is slower and requires a degree of intellectual honesty about whose responsibility the conditions actually are. It's easier to write a performance improvement plan than to write a process improvement plan, partly because one puts the burden on someone else and the other puts it on you.

I've seen this in organizations at every level. Front-line teams with no clear escalation path, making judgment calls that explode because nobody ever defined what "handling it yourself" is supposed to mean. Sales teams using a CRM that was configured for a go-to-market motion that no longer exists, spending twenty minutes on data entry that should take two. Customer service reps onboarded on policies that changed three months ago, then held accountable for giving customers the wrong information. The people aren't the problem in any of those situations. The environment is the problem. But the people catch the consequences.

What changed for me, over time, was learning to do the conditions audit before reaching for any personnel conclusion. Sit with your people in working sessions, not performance conversations, but real working sessions about the actual mechanics of the job. Ask them where they get stuck. Ask them what they do manually that feels like it should be automatic. Ask them what they wish someone had told them in their first month. Ask them what they do when they hit a wall and aren't sure what the right call is.

The answers will tell you things that performance data alone never will. And once you understand what the conditions are actually costing you, in time, in quality, in ramp, the conversation about what to fix and in what order becomes a lot clearer.

It also reframes how you think about the people themselves. Because here's the thing I've found consistently over a long career of managing teams: most people came to the job wanting to do good work. That's the starting condition for almost everyone. When they don't produce, when they seem disengaged, when they take shortcuts, that behavior is almost always adaptive. They've learned what the environment actually rewards and they've adjusted. Fix the environment, and you often find the person who wanted to be there in the first place.

That doesn't mean every performance problem is a conditions problem. Sometimes the fit is genuinely wrong, and no amount of better tooling or clearer process fixes a fundamental mismatch between a person and a role. But that conclusion is much more credible, and much more honest, when you've actually built the conditions for someone to succeed and they still haven't. The difference between "this person failed in a broken environment" and "this person failed despite a good one" matters. To you, to them, and to everyone on the team watching how you handle it.

The team you're frustrated with might be more capable than you're giving them credit for. The talent you're about to replace might be the talent you need, just not in the conditions you've given them to work in.

That's worth figuring out before you start over.
