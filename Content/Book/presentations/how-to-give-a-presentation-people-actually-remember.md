## How to Give a Presentation People Actually Remember

Early in my career, I thought the goal of a presentation was to cover the material. Get through the slides, hit the key points, don't go over time. I treated it like a delivery problem. Package the information, hand it over, done.

I was wrong about this for a long time.

The moment it clicked for me was watching a VP present to a senior leadership team at a company where I was managing a large operations group. She had maybe fifteen slides. She used maybe eight of them. For the first three minutes, she didn't touch the deck at all. She just talked about a specific customer call she'd been on the week before, a frustrated customer, a real problem, a number that didn't add up. The room was completely still. And then she said, "That call is why we're here today."

She had one point to make. Everything in the presentation was evidence for that point. Forty-five minutes later, the senior team had made a decision they'd been avoiding for six months.

I drove home thinking about what she'd done differently. It took me a while to put the right words to it, but here's what I eventually landed on: she wasn't sharing information. She was trying to change how people thought. Those are different jobs, and most presenters only know how to do the first one.

The first thing I started doing differently after that was writing a single sentence before touching any slide. Not an outline, not a list of topics. One sentence: "When this is over, I want my audience to believe that ________." If I couldn't finish it, I wasn't ready to build anything. I might have been ready to write a document, which is a fine and useful thing, but a document should be emailed, not presented. The moment you're standing in front of people, you owe them something a document can't give them, which is the experience of being convinced.

That one change improved my presentations more than any design advice I ever got.

The architecture question matters too. Most presentations I've sat through follow the same structure: agenda, background, data, data, data, recommendation, thank you. The audience spends the first forty minutes waiting for the part that matters. By the time you get to the recommendation, half the room has mentally checked out.

What works better is starting with the problem. Not the agenda, the actual problem. What's broken, what's at stake, why we're here. The audience needs to know that what you're about to say matters to them before they'll commit to listening. Earn their attention in the first two minutes, and you'll have it for the rest of the time. Skip that step, and you're fighting the whole way through.

I started opening with a question at some point. Not a rhetorical one, a real one directed at the room. Something like, "How many of you have sat through a quarterly review and couldn't tell me the top priority three days later?" Watch what happens in a room when you do that. People look up. A few of them laugh a little. A few of them look slightly uncomfortable because they've been in exactly that meeting. Either way, they're with you now. You've made it about them instead of about you.

After the problem, each section of the presentation should depend on the one before it. The audience should feel like they're being walked somewhere, not handed a menu of topics. By the time you make your ask or your recommendation, it should feel like the only logical conclusion given everything that came before it. Not a surprise, an inevitability.

The close should be one sentence. Not three takeaways, not five action items. One sentence the audience can repeat to a colleague on the elevator. If they can't explain your point in a sentence, it wasn't clear enough. That's not their failure, it's yours.

On the question of memorability: people remember what surprised them, what made them laugh, and what made them feel something. They remember stories. They almost never remember the third bullet on slide eleven. If you have a point you really need to stick, you have to figure out how you're going to make it stick. Saying it out loud isn't enough.

One of the best presenters I ever watched did something simple. He opened by writing a single word on a whiteboard, turned around, and said nothing for about five seconds. Then he said, "Every problem your team is going to tell me about this quarter comes back to this word." He'd built a question in the room before he'd made a single claim. For the rest of the presentation, everything he said had weight because the audience was already engaged in answering the question he'd posed at the start.

You can do something similar in almost any context. The mechanism matters less than the intent. Know what you want the audience to feel at each major transition and design for that, not just for the information you need to convey.

The rehearsal piece is where most people shortchange themselves, including people who know better. Reading your slides out loud is not rehearsal. It's proofreading with sound. Real rehearsal is delivering the whole thing from memory, at full energy, out loud, as if the audience is already there. Multiple times. You time it. You notice where you fumble and you fix it before you're in front of real people.

The reason rehearsal matters so much isn't confidence, though it helps with that too. It's headroom. When you haven't rehearsed, most of your mental bandwidth is going toward remembering what comes next. There's nothing left for reading the room, adjusting when people look confused, cutting a section when you're running long, or recovering cleanly from an unexpected question. Rehearsal gets the material out of working memory so you have capacity to actually be present. That's the difference between someone who's presenting and someone who's performing.

Nerves are a separate thing. They don't disappear with experience. What changes is how you interpret them. The same physiological state that makes you feel anxious before you start is what makes you feel sharp and alive when it's going well. Speak a little slower than feels natural in the first minute, take a real breath before you begin, and let the energy be there, just channel it into your voice and your movement instead of letting it sit in your chest.

The last habit, and the one most people never develop, is reviewing what happened afterward. Not formally. Just ten minutes, a few notes. What got the reaction you expected. Where the room went quiet. What landed flat. Which story worked and which one you should cut. Over time, you build real self-knowledge as a presenter. You stop repeating the same mistakes because you've actually looked at the pattern instead of just accumulating years.

The best presenters I've known aren't the most naturally charismatic people in the room. They're the ones who walk in having done the work before they got there. They know what they want the audience to believe. They've rehearsed until it's automatic. They've built moments designed to stick. And they've done it enough times that they know what works for them specifically.

That's a learnable thing. Every piece of it.
