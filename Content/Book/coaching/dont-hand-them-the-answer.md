## Don't Hand Them the Answer

I used to be very good at solving other people's problems.

I don't mean that as a compliment. I mean that if you came to me with a situation you weren't sure how to handle, I would within about ninety seconds have a clear opinion and a suggested path forward. I thought this was a management skill. I had years of experience, I'd seen most flavors of the problem, and I could shortcut the analysis quickly. My team always left our one-on-ones with clear direction.

What I did not realize, for longer than I want to admit, is that I was training them to need me.

The signal I kept missing was this: the problems never got smaller. The same people were coming back to me with the same categories of problems, slightly re-dressed. Nobody was developing a point of view. Nobody was walking into my office with a decision already made and just wanting a gut-check. They were walking in with a problem and waiting for me to hand them an answer. And I kept handing it over.

It took a manager above me, someone I respected, to name what was happening. She said, "You're not developing anyone. You're just working very, very fast." That landed hard.

The shift I eventually made was small in execution and genuinely uncomfortable in practice. When someone came to me with a problem and I asked "what do you think?" and they said "I don't know," I stopped answering. I said, "Take a second. Give me one idea." And then I waited.

The first few times I tried this, I nearly broke. Silence in a one-on-one feels different than silence in a group. It feels charged, almost accusatory. I kept wanting to soften it, to offer something, to make the person feel less on the spot. But I'd made a decision that I was going to wait, so I waited.

What happened, consistently, was this: within ten or fifteen seconds, they would offer something. Sometimes it was tentative, framed as a question: "I mean, could we maybe try X?" Sometimes it came out with more confidence than they probably felt. And occasionally it was the right answer, said almost apologetically, as if they were surprised it had been in there the whole time.

Which it had been. That's the thing. "I don't know" is almost never a factual statement. It's a social move. It's a way of deflecting the risk of being wrong in front of someone who has authority over your career. And when you answer for them, you confirm that the deflection was the right strategy. They learn to lead with it. Over time, it becomes the default response to any question that requires commitment.

When you hold the silence, you're sending a different message: I know you have something, I'm going to wait, and I'm not going anywhere.

There's another version of this for situations where someone genuinely has no starting point. They're new to the problem type, they've never been in this situation before, and asking them to generate an idea from nothing is going to frustrate both of you. In those cases, I offer a possibility and immediately turn it back to them: "What if we approached it this way? Would that work?" Now they're evaluating, not generating, but they're still doing cognitive work. I'm not handing them the answer. I'm handing them a starting point and asking them to take it somewhere.

The distinction matters more than it sounds. When someone evaluates your suggestion, they either adopt it or push back. Either one is useful. Adoption means they thought it through and agreed. Pushback means they've found a flaw or a better path, which is exactly what you want, someone developing judgment. What you're trying to avoid is passive receipt, the nod, the "okay, sounds good," followed by the forgetting.

Something I noticed over the years is how differently a solution lands depending on who generated it. When I told someone what to do, they did it, usually. When they arrived at the answer themselves, even partially, they owned the implementation in a different way. They were already bought in before we agreed on the next step, because the answer had their fingerprints on it. They'd argued for it, at least internally, before it came out of their mouth.

That's not psychology theory. That's just what I saw in the room.

There's an ego trap buried in all of this that I want to name directly, because I fell into it and I've watched a lot of other good managers fall into it too. If you're competent and experienced, you probably do know the answer. You've seen this kind of problem. You could solve it in the time it takes them to stammer through a first attempt. And so it feels like leadership to just say it. It feels like you're adding value.

You are adding value, in the smallest possible frame. You solved this one problem. You also made it incrementally more likely that they'll bring you the next one, and the one after that. Because why would they struggle through the discomfort of thinking if you're going to hand it to them anyway?

The compounding effect runs in both directions. A person who gets asked to think starts thinking, starts doing it in the shower, starts coming in with hypotheses instead of questions. A person who gets told starts waiting. Over a year, those two people look like they work in different organizations.

One more thing, because I don't want to leave this as a blanket rule. There are moments where you just tell someone what to do. A genuine emergency. A safety issue. A situation with real consequences and no time for a coached discovery process. In those moments, be direct and be clear. This framework is not for a crisis. It's for the everyday work of building someone up, which is most of what management actually is.

The rest of the time, when someone says "I don't know," the most useful thing you can do is nothing. Wait. Let the silence do the work. You'll be surprised how often they already have the answer and just needed someone to stop talking long enough for them to say it.
