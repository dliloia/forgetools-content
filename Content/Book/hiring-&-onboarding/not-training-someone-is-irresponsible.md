## The Tax You Pay on Every Hire

Early in my career I walked into a job where nobody had written anything down. There was a shared drive, technically. It had folders with names like "OLD" and "OLD_2" and one called "FINAL_USE THIS" that contained a single spreadsheet last updated sometime during a previous administration. The person I was replacing had been there for six years and was leaving in two weeks. Those two weeks were supposed to cover everything she knew.

They did not cover everything she knew.

I spent the first three months of that job building the documentation she never had time to create, learning the same lessons her predecessor had learned before her, making the same recoverable mistakes, asking the same questions of people who were tired of answering them. Nobody was malicious about it. That was just how the place worked. You figured it out. You asked around. Eventually it stuck.

What I understood only later is that the company was paying for those three months of ramp-up every single time they brought someone new in. The institutional knowledge lived in people's heads, and when those people left, the knowledge went with them. The next person started from zero. This was not a knowledge management failure. It was a management failure, repeated, at scale, by people who had normalized it because they'd experienced the same thing when they started.

The rationalization is almost universal. "I had to figure it out myself." Sometimes extended with "and I turned out fine." Both are true statements that miss the actual problem. Whatever you learned through trial and error is now yours. It lives in you. Refusing to write it down does not toughen up the next person. It just makes them spend time and energy rediscovering things you already know, on the company's dime, and it makes their first months harder than they need to be.

That is a choice. It does not feel like one, but it is.

There is a version of this that goes beyond simple neglect into something more deliberate. Some people protect their institutional knowledge because it protects them. If you are the only one who knows how the system works, you are very hard to cut. You are indispensable in a way that feels secure. I understand that impulse; I have watched it operate in people who were otherwise thoughtful managers. But it is corrosive, because it makes your team dependent on you for the wrong reasons, and it limits what they can do and where they can grow. Real value in a leadership role does not come from being the only person who knows how to process an exception in the billing system. It comes from judgment, from context, from the ability to read a situation and make a good call. None of that evaporates when you write the billing exception process down.

The clearest test I have found is simple. If you take two weeks off, completely unreachable, does the predictable work still get done? Not the genuine crises that require your specific judgment. The recurring, process-driven work that happens every week. If the answer is no, the problem is not your team's capability. The process lives in your head instead of somewhere they can access.

That same test applies to the people below you. The person directly underneath you on the org chart: could they step into your role tomorrow? Not indefinitely, not perfectly. But competently, with access to what they need? If they cannot, you have work to do, and the work is not hiring someone better. The work is documentation.

I have seen managers resist this framing because it sounds like they are being asked to make themselves replaceable, which feels threatening. But making yourself replaceable in your current role is precisely how you move up. Every senior leader I have genuinely respected understood this. You cannot be promoted into a bigger job if the organization cannot function without you in your current one. The managers who grip their knowledge like a life raft are usually the ones who stall.

What documentation actually requires is less than most people assume. I am not describing a 60-page playbook that takes a quarter to write. The standard I use is this: for any predictable, recurring question your team might ask, you should be able to produce a written or recorded answer within an hour. That's the bar. Not perfect, not comprehensive. Findable, and ready within an hour.

The mechanics are genuinely simple. Keep a notes file open while you work. When you do something for the first time or explain something verbally to someone, write down the steps in the moment, not later. Later never comes. Record a short screen capture walkthrough and drop it somewhere with a name that means something. That is most of what is required.

The technology available now makes this easier than it has ever been. I dictate notes while I'm working and clean them up later. Short audio recordings become searchable text. What used to take an hour of careful writing takes fifteen minutes. The people still not doing it have no logistical excuse left. They are making a choice, even if they have not articulated it to themselves that way.

When a new person joins a team with no written processes, they absorb a lesson that goes beyond the practical confusion of not knowing how things work. They learn that this is what management looks like here. They learn that figuring it out is the expected path. And when it is their turn to bring someone new onto the team, they will repeat exactly what was done to them, because that is the model they were shown.

The dysfunction propagates. One manager at a time, one hire at a time, across years.

The interruption is straightforward. Write things down. Leave them somewhere findable. Do it as part of the work, not after it. The next person who sits where you sat will either spend their first months recovering from your absence or building on your foundation.

One of those options is a gift. The other is a bill you are sending forward.
