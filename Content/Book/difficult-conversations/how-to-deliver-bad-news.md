## Delivering Bad News

There's a particular kind of dread that settles in before a conversation you know is going to hurt someone. I've felt it dozens of times across my career. The night before a layoff notification. The morning of a PIP delivery. The hour leading up to telling someone their role no longer exists.

What I know now, and wish I'd known earlier, is that the dread is mostly about you. Not them. The discomfort you feel before the meeting is your discomfort, and if you don't get that under control before you walk in, you will manage your anxiety instead of managing the conversation, and the person who trusted you enough to work for you will pay for it.

I've watched good managers become completely unreliable in these moments. They start talking the second the meeting begins and don't stop. They frame and reframe and add context and explain the business climate and describe the difficult decisions leadership had to make, and by the time they get to the actual news, the person across the table has been in a state of suspended dread for six minutes, running through every possible scenario, trying to read their manager's face for clues.

That is not a kindness. It is the opposite.

The kindest thing you can do is say the real thing first. One sentence. Then stop.

I learned this by watching it done well, once, when I was early in my career. A leader I worked with walked into a difficult conversation, sat down, and within about fifteen seconds said exactly what was happening. No preamble. The person's face shifted, the emotion came up, and the leader just sat there and was present for it. Didn't try to explain it away, didn't apologize three times in a row, didn't start immediately listing the support resources available. Just let the news land. And then they talked.

I've thought about that scene a hundred times since.

The structure matters because the person across the table is not receiving information in a normal state. From the moment the meeting was scheduled, something in them has been on alert. They know. Maybe they don't know the specifics, but something in the body registers when something is wrong. So when you finally sit down together, every second of buildup is compounded dread, and you are the only person in that room who can end it.

Say the news. Then let there be silence. Then provide the context.

What comes after the news depends on what the news is, and those situations are genuinely different even though they feel similar.

A layoff is a business decision. The person's work may have been excellent, their relationships strong, their output consistent, and they are still losing their job because the company made a choice. The worst thing you can do in that conversation is allow any ambiguity about that. If someone leaves a layoff notification wondering whether their performance was secretly the issue, you have failed them. Say it directly: "This is not a performance issue. This is a business decision." And then tell them what they actually need to know: what they're receiving, what the timeline is, what happens with their benefits, who they should call with questions.

A performance conversation is different because the performance is the issue, and they need to hear that with enough clarity to do something about it. The failure mode here is vagueness. Managers soften the message so completely that the person walks out of the meeting not actually knowing what's wrong or what they need to do differently. That isn't mercy. It's a setup for the termination conversation two months later that feels completely unfair because nobody ever told them clearly what was expected.

Be specific. Not "we need better communication" but "we need a written update to the team every Friday by noon." Not "your attitude in meetings is a concern" but "in last Tuesday's product review you interrupted three people and at one point told a colleague their idea was stupid in front of the group." Specific, observable, unambiguous. That's the standard. If you can't describe the problem with that kind of specificity, you're not ready to have the conversation yet.

What happens when they react matters as much as how you opened. People don't respond to bad news the way people respond in meetings about normal things. They cry, they go quiet, they ask the same question multiple times because the brain keeps looping, they get angry, they deflect into logistics to avoid feeling the thing they're feeling. All of that is normal and none of it needs to be fixed.

If someone cries, let them cry. You don't need to fill that space with words. "Take the time you need" covers it. If someone gets angry, stay calm. Not performatively calm, actually calm, which requires you to have done the emotional preparation before you walked in. The anger is almost never actually about you. It's about the situation and you're the one who delivered it, so you become the focal point. That's part of the job.

The one thing I'd flag as genuinely harmful is telling someone how they're about to feel or should feel. "I know this is disappointing, but I really think this could be a great opportunity for you" is the kind of sentence that will live in that person's memory for years, and not in a good way. Let them own their reaction. Your job is to be honest and present, not to manage their emotional experience into something more comfortable for you.

Preparation cannot be optional here. You have to know your first two sentences before you walk into the room, and you have to know them well enough that the dread of the moment doesn't knock them out of your head. Write them out. Say them out loud in your car on the way in. Know the logistics cold, because questions will come and the inability to answer them in that moment communicates a carelessness that cuts deep when someone is already vulnerable.

After the conversation, stay in contact. People process bad news in stages, and the questions they have on day two are different from the ones they had in the room. Make yourself available. If you put someone on a PIP, show up for the follow-through you committed to. If you told someone in a layoff conversation that you'd write them a reference, write the reference. These things matter in a way that transcends formality. They tell people whether you're someone who means what they say.

The reality is that how you handle the worst conversations is inseparable from who you are as a leader. People don't forget these moments. Not the people they happened to, and not the people who watched from the edges and drew their own conclusions about what you're made of.

The news will be hard no matter what. How you deliver it is entirely up to you.
