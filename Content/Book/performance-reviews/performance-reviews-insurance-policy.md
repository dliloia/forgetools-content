## The Insurance You Buy in January

There is a particular kind of dread that hits managers every fall. You know the feeling. Review season opens, you pull up the form, you stare at the blank text box next to someone's name, and your brain produces nothing useful. You try to remember what they worked on in the first quarter. You can't. You default to vague praise and hope it sounds thoughtful.

I have been in that position exactly once in my career. I made sure I never put myself there again.

Early in my time as a people manager, I was asked to complete annual reviews for a team I had inherited mid-year. I had no documentation. No notes from 1:1s, because the previous manager had kept none and I hadn't yet started keeping my own. I spent an entire weekend trying to reconstruct the year from memory, email threads, and conversations I barely remembered having.

The reviews I wrote were embarrassing. Not because they were harsh. Because they were empty. I described people in the broadest possible terms. I used the word "reliable" four times across three reviews. I cited no specific examples because I had none. When I handed them out, I watched one of my best employees read her review with an expression I can only describe as polite disappointment. She thanked me. She folded the paper. She never mentioned it again.

That was the moment I understood what a performance review is actually for.

It is not a bureaucratic obligation. It is a formal, documented record of how a person spent a year of their professional life. It is the moment when a manager either demonstrates that they were paying attention or reveals that they were not. My employee knew the difference. I knew she knew.

The following January, I started keeping notes after every 1:1. Nothing elaborate. I used a simple document, one section per employee, with the date at the top and a few bullet points underneath. What they told me. What I observed. What feedback I gave. What they committed to working on. Most weeks, it took me three minutes after the meeting ended.

By the following November, each section was a running record of the year. Not every conversation was significant. Some entries were just status updates and logistics. But threaded through all of it were the moments that mattered. The project that nearly came apart and how they held it together. The feedback I gave in March that they actually acted on by June. The quarter where their output dropped and the conversation we had about it. The moment in August when they did something that genuinely surprised me.

When review season opened that year, I read back through my notes for each person and started writing. I finished all six reviews in one afternoon. Not because the writing was effortless, but because I wasn't inventing anything. I was synthesizing a record that already existed.

The reviews I wrote that year were specific. Not flowery, not generous beyond what was true, but specific. One employee's review included a paragraph about a moment in May when a client escalation landed on his desk with no warning and he resolved it without involving me, briefed me afterward, and followed up with a short process recommendation to prevent the same escalation from happening again. I knew that because I had written it down in my notes the day after he told me about it.

He read that paragraph during our review conversation and went quiet for a moment. Then he said: "I didn't think you knew about that." He meant it as a compliment. I took it as a reminder that specificity is how you show someone they were seen.

Most managers, if they're honest, manage by impression. They form a general sense of each person over the course of the year, and at review time they translate that impression into language. The employee who seems engaged becomes "a motivated contributor." The one who keeps things running becomes "reliable." The one who pushes back in meetings becomes either "a strong communicator" or "someone who can improve their collaborative approach," depending on how the manager feels about them.

Impressions are not nothing. They carry real information. But they are filtered through recency bias, through personal affinity, through whatever happened in the last few weeks before the review was due. The employee who had a rough October pays for it with a review that doesn't reflect their January through September. The employee who had a great October benefits from a review that papers over the rest.

Documentation doesn't eliminate judgment. It grounds it. When you have notes from all year, you can see the actual shape of someone's performance. You can see whether their strong Q3 was consistent with the rest of the year or an exception. You can see whether the development goal they committed to in February showed up anywhere in the months that followed. You can see patterns that memory would have smoothed away.

There is also something that notes do for the harder conversations. When performance is a problem and you need to write a review that reflects that honestly, documentation is what makes the difference between an assessment and an opinion. An opinion can be argued with. An assessment grounded in documented observations is much harder to dispute, and much more useful to the person receiving it.

I have had employees push back on difficult reviews. In every case where I had documentation, the conversation moved faster and landed better. Not because the notes "won" the argument, but because they shifted the conversation from feelings to facts. There was something concrete to look at together. That changes the dynamic entirely.

The notes are not the review. That is worth saying clearly. The notes are raw material. The review is the synthesis. Your job at review time is to read back through the year, find the patterns, decide what is true about this person's performance and growth, and then write that truth in specific language.

That is still an act of judgment. Documentation doesn't replace it. But it makes it possible to exercise that judgment on the basis of a full year, not just the last six weeks and whatever impressions have accumulated in your memory.

The managers who dread review season are, almost without exception, the ones who have no raw material to work from. They are not bad writers. They are underequipped. They are trying to build something with no materials.

The fix was available to them all year, three minutes at a time.
