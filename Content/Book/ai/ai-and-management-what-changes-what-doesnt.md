## The Overhead and the Work

There's a distinction I've been drawing in my head for years, and I never had a clean way to say it until recently.

There's the work of managing people. And there's the weight of managing people.

The work is the conversation with someone who's struggling and doesn't know how to say it. It's the decision about whether to push a strong performer harder or let her find her own pace. It's the moment in a team meeting when you read something in the room and know the whole agenda needs to change. It's sitting with someone who's about to quit over something that feels insurmountable and finding the thread that brings them back.

The weight is the documentation. The administrative overhead. The time spent writing up what you already know, assembling evidence you already have, preparing for conversations by reconstructing records from scattered notes. The hours you spend doing things that are necessary but that have nothing to do with the human being sitting across from you.

For most of my career, those two things came as a package deal. You wanted to do the work, you had to carry the weight. There was no separating them.

That's changing now. And I think it matters more than most people realize.

I spent a long stretch of my career watching smart, capable managers get consumed by the weight. Not because they were disorganized or lazy. Because the system demanded an enormous amount of administrative output, and there was no efficient way to produce it. Performance review season would arrive and people would disappear into their offices for days, building narratives from fragments, trying to do justice to a whole year of someone's work in a document that would take them ten times longer to write than it would take the person to read.

I watched managers who genuinely cared about their people produce reviews that felt generic, not because they didn't see their people clearly, but because they were exhausted by the time they got to the writing. The insight was there. The energy to express it wasn't.

This is the problem worth solving.

What I've seen AI actually do well is exactly this: take the information you already have and organize it in a way that makes your thinking faster and sharper. You've taken notes across twelve one-on-ones. You know this person. You've been watching them grow, or stall, or quietly disengage. AI doesn't add new knowledge. It helps you see the pattern you were already sitting in the middle of, and it gives you a starting point instead of a blank page.

That's not a small thing. When you're not starting from zero, you think better. You catch what you would have missed. You have the energy to say the true thing instead of the convenient thing.

But here's where it gets complicated, and where I think a lot of people will go wrong.

The risk isn't that AI makes managers lazy. The risk is more subtle than that. It's that over time, if you're not deliberate about it, you start outsourcing the thinking along with the writing. You accept a draft that sounds right without interrogating it. You skip the part where you sit with your notes and ask yourself what you actually observed, because the summary is already there and it's close enough.

I took notes by hand for years before I had anything to capture them digitally. The act of writing, of deciding what to write down, was how I processed what I was hearing. When you take a note, you're making a judgment: this matters. When AI transcribes everything and summarizes it for you, that judgment gets deferred. And if you never circle back to make it, you start to lose the texture of what's actually happening with your people.

The tool is useful precisely because it removes friction. That's also its risk. Friction is sometimes where the thinking lives.

The managers I most respect figured out how to use their time well long before any of these tools existed. They were ruthless about protecting the hours they spent on actual human interaction, and they moved through the administrative work as efficiently as they could. What they were doing, manually, was the same thing AI now makes structurally easier: separating the overhead from the work.

When I think about what changes and what doesn't, the answer is clean. The overhead changes. The work doesn't.

Reading the room doesn't change. Knowing when someone needs a direct challenge versus a longer runway doesn't change. Earning trust by being consistent, by following through, by being honest when it costs you something, none of that changes. The relationship is still the job. It always was.

What changes is how much time you have to show up for it.

That's the whole thing. Not a technology story. A time story. A question of what you do with the hours you get back, and whether you spend them on the part of the job that actually moves people.

The managers who get this right will look different from the ones who don't. Not because they're using better tools. Because they used the tools to make more room for the work, and then they actually did the work.
