Early in my career, I had a manager above me who seemed to get everything done faster than anyone else at his level. Same company, same processes, same constraints. But when he needed a decision from legal or a resource from finance or a sign-off from someone in ops, it happened. Not instantly, but noticeably faster than it happened for the people around him.

I watched him for a while trying to figure out the trick, and eventually I realized it wasn't a trick at all. He just knew everyone. Not in the glad-handing, company-event way, but in the genuine, I-actually-know-what-you-work-on-and-what-keeps-you-up-at-night way. When he walked into a conversation needing something, he wasn't walking in cold. He was continuing a relationship that already existed.

I started asking him about it, and his answer was so mundane it almost disappointed me. He kept a list of people in other departments whose work touched his, and he had a standing monthly conversation with each of them. Nothing formal. No agenda document. Just thirty minutes of genuine exchange: what are you working on, what's coming up, what do you need from my side that you're not getting. That was it.

The thing I didn't fully understand until I started doing it myself was how much of organizational friction is actually just unfamiliarity. Two teams stepping on each other, not because their goals conflict, but because neither team really knows what the other is trying to do. A decision sitting in someone's inbox for two weeks, not because they're obstructing, but because the person asking is a name they don't recognize attached to a request they don't have context for. A project that dies in the handoff between departments because the managers involved have never had a real conversation.

None of these problems require a new process. They require two people who actually know each other.

I've watched organizations spend enormous energy building escalation structures, cross-functional steering committees, integrated planning cycles, all of which are useful, but all of which are also, at some level, compensating for the absence of something simpler. When people trust each other, information moves. When they don't, it routes through layers and slows down, and everyone concludes the organization is dysfunctional when what's actually dysfunctional is the relationship layer underneath.

The peer 1:1, which is what I call a regular informal meeting with a key cross-functional counterpart, isn't a complicated concept. But managers skip it for a few reasons that are worth naming honestly.

The first is that it feels presumptuous or strange to schedule a meeting with someone you don't directly work with. There's no project forcing the conversation, no standing reason to be in each other's calendars, and managers worry about coming across as political or needy. So they wait for a reason, and the reason usually arrives in the form of a problem, which is the worst possible time to try to start a real relationship.

The second reason is that the value isn't immediately visible. You spend thirty minutes with the head of product operations and you walk out without a deliverable, without a decision, without anything you can point to. In a world where calendars are already overloaded and every meeting is scrutinized for ROI, that's a hard sell even to yourself.

But the value shows up the first time something goes sideways between your teams, and instead of an escalation or a tense email chain, you can just call the person and sort it out. It shows up when a reorg is brewing and your peer mentions it in passing because they trust you enough to share what they know. It shows up when you need something quickly and your message doesn't go to the bottom of a stranger's queue.

What I've learned about how to run these conversations is that they work best when they're genuinely reciprocal and mostly agenda-free, but anchored around a few consistent questions. What's keeping you busy right now? What's coming up that I might not know about? Is there anything my team does that creates unnecessary work for yours? The last one especially tends to surprise people, because asking a peer how you can make their life easier is not something most managers think to do. But it signals that you're showing up as a partner, not just a borrower of goodwill, and that distinction matters.

The managers I've seen build the strongest cross-functional reputations are almost always the ones who invested early in relationships that, at the time, had no obvious payoff. They had coffee with the finance partner before budget season. They got to know the legal team before there was a contract to review. They built the relationship when neither side needed anything, which is the only time you can build it honestly.

By the time the pressure arrived, and it always arrives, they weren't introducing themselves. They were continuing a conversation.
