## Matching Your Experience to What a Company Actually Values

I've done over a thousand interviews on the other side of the table. Not the side where you're hoping the answer lands. The side where you're deciding whether it does.

And one of the most reliable tells I developed over those years is whether a candidate has done the real preparation work, or whether they've done the surface version. The surface version is knowing the company's values and finding ways to mention them. The real version is understanding what the values actually demand behaviorally and then proving, with specific stories, that you've already lived that way.

Most candidates stop at the surface. Here's what the rest looks like.

The first thing you have to accept is that published values are not PR. They're a behavioral contract. When a company says "Ownership" or "Customer First" or "Act with Integrity," they're not describing what they hope their people are like. They're describing what they hire for, what they reward, and what they manage out. If you can read those values with that lens, you've already pulled ahead of most of the field.

So take one value and really interrogate it. What behavior does it reward? What behavior would it punish? And what does a real story look like where someone chose this principle when the easier path would have been to ignore it?

That third question is the one that does the work. Because the thing that separates a real story from a performed one is pressure. If you tell me you're customer-focused, I believe you in good conditions. Everybody's customer-focused when it's easy. What I'm listening for is the moment when being customer-focused cost you something internally. When you pushed back on a decision your own team made. When you delivered bad news to a client honestly instead of managing them toward a better headline. When you argued for the right outcome in a room where nobody wanted to hear it.

That's the story that proves the value.

I spent five years as an Amazon Bar Raiser, which means I sat in on hundreds of hiring decisions with the specific responsibility of evaluating candidates against leadership principles. The conversations after the interview were revealing. When a candidate had done the real preparation work, the debrief was easy. The stories they told mapped cleanly onto the behaviors we were looking for, and the team could point to specific moments that gave them confidence. When a candidate had done the surface version, the debrief was full of phrases like "they seemed aligned" and "I think they get it." Those candidates didn't usually get the offer. Seeming aligned isn't the same as demonstrating alignment.

Here's the honest version of what I'd coach anyone to do before a serious interview.

Pull up the company's values and go through them one at a time. For each one, write down what you think it actually means in practice, not the marketing version. Then write down two or three real situations from your own career where that value was relevant. Don't filter yet, just surface what's there. Then look at each situation and ask whether you actually chose the path the value demands, or whether you happened to be in a situation that looked like it. There's a difference. Choose the situations where you made a real choice, where another path existed and you took this one.

Those are your stories.

The other thing worth sitting with is what happens when you don't have a story. That's real information. Sometimes the gap exists because you haven't had the opportunity yet, which is honest and handleable. Sometimes it exists because your previous environment actively rewarded the opposite behavior, which is worth thinking hard about before you accept an offer. Values in a healthy company aren't decoration. They're operating principles. If a core value asks something of you that runs counter to how you've been trained for a decade, you should go in with your eyes open about what that friction feels like day to day.

And this process runs in both directions. You're evaluating fit just as much as they are.

The way I'd probe that in an interview is to ask about specific tradeoffs, not general philosophy. Ask how they've navigated situations where two values pulled in different directions. Ask what a hard decision looked like for a team that really lived one of the principles. The stories they tell you, or don't tell you, are more informative than anything the recruiter will say.

The candidates who do this well don't sound rehearsed. They sound grounded. They bring specific details, specific moments, specific outcomes. They don't reach for the right language because they don't need to. The story carries the alignment without anyone having to announce it.

That's what preparation actually looks like from the other side of the table. Not confidence. Not polish. Evidence.
