## Matching What You've Done to Where You're Going

There's a version of job searching that a lot of people do that I've always found a little sad to watch. They find a company they want, read the careers page, absorb the values language, and then go to work retrofitting their history to match. They'll take a thing they did in 2019 and slowly reshape the story until it sounds like it was designed to answer the exact question they're expecting.

They're not lying. But they're not being honest either. And the people on the other side of the table have usually done enough interviews to feel the difference.

I've been the person on the other side of that table more times than I can count. And what I can tell you is that the candidates who actually land, who get the offer and then actually succeed in the role, are almost never the ones who came in with the most polished presentation. They're the ones who showed up with real stories and told them straight.

Here's the thing about values-based interviews that most job seekers misunderstand. The interviewer is not listening for whether you know the company's vocabulary. They're listening for whether your experience contains the actual behavior the value describes. Those are completely different things, and only one of them requires you to have actually done something.

I developed a simple habit years ago when I was helping people prepare for senior roles, and I've seen it work consistently enough that I think it's worth laying out clearly.

Before you research the company at all, write down the stories from your career that you're most proud of. Not the outcomes that look best on paper. The situations where you made a real decision under real pressure, where you had to choose between two bad options or convince someone who didn't want to be convinced, or where you failed at something and then figured out why. Write them down plainly. What happened, what you did, what came of it.

Do this before you look at their values page. The whole point is that these stories come from you, not from them.

Then go do your research. Read everything. Their stated values, their executive interviews, the way their leaders talk about decisions they've made publicly. Try to understand not just what they say they care about, but what behavior they actually seem to reward. Read between the lines. Companies that genuinely live their values talk about them differently than companies that just posted them on a wall.

Now do the matching. For each value the company has named, look at your list and find the story that fits most naturally. Not the one you can most convincingly bend to fit. The one you actually recognize.

If you find a value and you genuinely don't have a story that maps to it, that's important. Sit with it. Maybe you haven't operated in that kind of environment before. Maybe you disagree with it. Maybe you just haven't thought about your own experience clearly enough yet. Any of those answers is more useful than skipping past the gap and hoping no one asks.

Because someone will ask. And they'll follow up. And they'll follow up again. I've watched candidates who had clearly reverse-engineered their stories hold up fine through the first question, then start losing resolution on the second or third follow-up. The interviewer isn't trying to trap them. They're just going deeper, the way you do when something interests you. But a retrofitted narrative can't go deep. It runs out of texture.

A real memory has texture. It has the name of the person who disagreed with you. It has the specific thing you said in that meeting that changed the direction. It has the part where you were wrong. That stuff is not something you can invent under pressure. You either have it or you don't.

The candidates who've impressed me most across all those conversations were rarely the ones with the most impressive titles or the cleanest accomplishments. They were the ones who could tell me exactly what happened, including where it got hard or didn't go the way they'd hoped, in enough detail that I had no choice but to believe them. That quality, the ability to be specific about your own history, is actually rare. Most people summarize their experience. The best candidates describe it.

The values matching exercise works because it forces you to do that work before you're in the room. You come in knowing what you have and where it applies. You're not searching your memory in real time while someone watches you. You've already done the inventory.

And if the inventory reveals that your honest stories don't map well to a company's values, that's the exercise working correctly. A lot of job searches end in misery not because someone failed to get an offer, but because they worked so hard to land at a company that wasn't actually right for them that they ignored every signal pointing that direction.

The matching process, done honestly, is a filter. It should tell you where to invest your energy and where to save it.

You've got real experience. The job is to know it clearly enough to place it accurately, not to dress it up in someone else's language and hope it passes inspection.
