## The Preparation Most People Never Do

I've sat across from a lot of candidates over the years, somewhere north of three thousand interviews across my career. And the pattern that separates the ones who get offers from the ones who don't isn't usually what you'd expect. It's not credentials. It's not even how sharp they are in the moment.

It's self-knowledge.

The candidates who struggle aren't struggling because they haven't researched the company. Most of them have done that work. They've read the website, maybe skimmed some press releases, know roughly what the company does and why it's been in the news. That's all fine. But when I ask them what they actually value in a workplace, or what kind of problems light them up versus drain them, or why this particular role is the right next step for them specifically, a lot of them go quiet in a way that has nothing to do with nerves. They just haven't thought about it.

The best interview prep I know starts there, not with the company, but with yourself.

Before any interview, sit down and write out your five core values as they relate to work. Not aspirational values, actual ones. What do you need from a work environment to do your best? Some people need high autonomy and get strangled by heavy process. Others need clear structure and flounder when nobody's telling them what the priorities are. Some people want to be in rooms where hard data drives every decision. Others want to be somewhere the culture is warm and collaborative and the work is grounded in relationships. None of these are wrong. But you need to know which one you are.

The reason this preparation matters for the interview itself is that it changes what you can say. When someone asks you why you want to work at a particular company, and you've done this work, your answer is specific and true rather than assembled from their own marketing. You can point to something real: a way they operate, a problem they're working on, a structural thing about the team that matches something you know about yourself. That kind of answer lands differently. It's memorable. It's also honest, and experienced interviewers can tell the difference.

There's a second payoff that takes longer to appreciate. When you know your values clearly enough to name them, you're not just performing better in the interview. You're actually evaluating the company more effectively while it's happening. You're listening for signals about whether this place matches what you need, instead of just trying to convince them to pick you. That shift in posture, from approval-seeking to mutual evaluation, changes the whole dynamic. You're more confident. You ask better questions. You leave with information you actually need to make a good decision, rather than an offer you accepted partly out of relief.

I've talked to people who ignored something that felt wrong during an interview and took the job anyway. Almost universally, the thing that felt wrong turned out to be the thing that made them miserable six months later. The values exercise doesn't prevent that entirely, but it gives you something to compare against while you're in the room.

The other piece of preparation that most people skip is mapping their own stories before they walk in. Behavioral questions are everywhere now, and the mistake people make is trying to retrieve their examples during the interview, while the interviewer is watching. You can see it happen. The eyes shift, there's a pause, the candidate starts a story, realizes it's not quite right, and has to redirect. The answer ends up vague and they lose confidence in real time.

The fix is simple. Before the interview, spend 45 minutes writing down 8 to 10 of your most significant professional experiences. Problems you solved. Failures you owned. Situations where you had to lead or influence or make a hard call. Write them plainly. What was the situation? What did you do specifically, not what the team did, what you did? What was the outcome? You don't need to memorize scripts. You just need to know the inventory exists. Once it's mapped, the retrieval during the interview is fast, and you can adapt the right story to whatever question comes your way.

One thing I pay close attention to in that kind of answer is pronoun choice. Candidates who answer every behavioral question with "we" are often hiding in the group. I don't mind hearing about the team, but I need to know what you did. When you've mapped your stories in advance and separated your contribution from the team's contribution in your own writing, you show up with that clarity already in place. You say "I" when you mean you, and "we" when you mean the team, and that distinction is obvious to the person across the table.

Research matters too, but the kind that actually differentiates you isn't the company overview everyone else has read. It's the specific stuff: the team's recent challenges, the trajectory of the function, the real problems this role exists to solve. If you can talk to someone at the company before you go in, even for 20 minutes over coffee, the texture of what you learn from that conversation is worth more than two hours on their website. And the questions you prepare matter as much as the answers you give. A sharp, specific question signals that you're already thinking like someone who does this job. A generic question signals that you showed up.

The night before, review your values, your story inventory, and your questions. Keep it to under an hour. You're not cramming, you're activating what you've already done. Then get some sleep, because showing up rested and clear is more valuable than anything you'd absorb at midnight.

The candidates who get offers aren't always the most credentialed or the smoothest talkers. They're usually the ones who know themselves well enough to tell a true story about why they're in that room. That clarity is the preparation most people never do. It's also the easiest to start.
