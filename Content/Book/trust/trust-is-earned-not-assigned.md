## The Trust You Don't Have Yet

The day I got my first management role, I thought the hard part was over.

I'd earned it. Put in the hours, delivered the results, made the case. When my director handed me a team of seven people, I walked into that first meeting feeling like I'd arrived somewhere.

They were polite. Attentive, even. But they didn't trust me. I just couldn't see it yet.

It took me about three months to understand what I was looking at. One of my best performers, a guy named Marcus, was consistently late on deliverables. Missed two deadlines in six weeks. In one-on-ones he told me everything was fine. Turned out he'd been sitting on a serious conflict with another team for weeks, one that was actively blocking his work. He hadn't told me because he didn't know yet if I was the kind of manager who would help or the kind who would make it worse.

He was waiting to see who I actually was. Not who I said I was. Who I proved to be.

That was the moment I started understanding trust not as a feeling, but as a ledger.

Every manager has a trust account with every person who reports to them. The starting balance is zero. Not negative, but not positive either. Just zero. The title gives you nothing in that account. The title is just the door. You walk through it and then you start earning, one transaction at a time.

Deposits look like this: you say you'll follow up on something by Thursday, and you do. You show up to a 1:1 on time, with your full attention on the person across from you, not on your phone. You give someone real feedback when what they actually need to hear is uncomfortable. You advocate for someone's idea in a meeting and then tell them you did it. Small transactions. Small deposits.

Withdrawals are bigger. Always bigger. That's the part most managers don't account for.

You cancel a 1:1 because something else came up. One withdrawal. You do it again two weeks later. Larger withdrawal, because now it's a pattern. You tell someone their presentation went well when it didn't, because giving real feedback felt risky in the moment. Withdrawal. You mention something in a leadership meeting that someone told you in confidence, and word gets back. That's not a withdrawal. That's emptying the account and lighting it on fire.

I've made every one of those mistakes. The one that stays with me is the confidence breach.

Early in my career, someone on my team told me during a 1:1 that they were struggling with a team member's behavior. Specific, personal stuff. They told me because they trusted me. Two weeks later, in a different conversation with that team member's manager, I said something I shouldn't have. It wasn't malicious. I wasn't thinking. It got back to my employee before the week was out.

She never quit. She never made a scene. But something closed. The openness she'd had in our 1:1s contracted. She became careful around me in a way she hadn't been before. I watched it happen and I knew exactly what I'd done.

I spent the next four months being completely reliable in every small thing I could control. Showing up, following through, keeping my mouth shut when I should. Slowly, partially, she opened up again. But there was always a faint line there. A place where the trust had been and been broken and was rebuilt but not quite the same.

You can repair trust. It's just slow and the scar tissue is real.

The most counterintuitive thing I've learned about trust is this: consistency matters more than intensity. A manager who is warm and charismatic but unpredictable will lose to a manager who is steady and reliable every single week. Every time.

I've worked with both types. The charismatic ones get early loyalty. People are drawn to them. But their teams never quite settle. They're always calibrating, never sure which version of the manager they're going to get. High intensity can create excitement. It cannot create safety. And without safety, people protect themselves. They filter what they tell you. They hedge. They wait to see how things land before they commit.

Steady and reliable does something different. Steady and reliable lets people stop watching and start working.

The 1:1 is where all of this plays out. It's a 30-minute recurring meeting on a calendar, but what it actually is: a weekly signal about whether you're someone worth trusting.

When you show up every week, on time, with your attention on the person in front of you, you're telling them something. You're telling them they matter enough to have a protected slot in your schedule. When you follow through on the one thing you said you'd look into, you're telling them your word means something. When you tell them a hard truth about their performance, respectfully and clearly, you're telling them you respect them enough not to manage them in the dark.

Those signals compound. Slowly. Over months. And then one day you realize Marcus is telling you about a problem before it's a crisis instead of after. He's not waiting to see what you'll do. He already knows.

That's the whole game. Build the account large enough that people stop hedging and start trusting. Stop filtering and start talking. Stop protecting themselves and start bringing you the real problems.

It doesn't require charisma. It doesn't require a gift for people. It requires showing up, keeping your word, and telling the truth. Week after week. In the small moments that feel routine but aren't.

The title is just the door. What you build after you walk through it is up to you.
