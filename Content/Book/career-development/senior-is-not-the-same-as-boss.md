## Who Actually Has Authority Over You

Most people carry a template into their first job that they built in school, and it causes them problems for years before they ever name it.

In school, the older person was the authority. Always. Your teacher held your grade. Your coach held your playing time. The senior student who ran the club held your standing in it. Seniority and authority were the same thing, reliably, for the better part of two decades. So when you walk into a workplace and someone has been there five years longer than you, your brain does what it was trained to do. It files them under boss-equivalent.

The problem is that almost never reflects how authority actually works in organizations, and the confusion extracts a real cost.

I've watched this play out more times than I can count. A junior employee is in the middle of a focused work block, a senior colleague walks over and starts pulling their attention toward a different project. The junior employee doesn't say anything. They pivot. They work on the new thing. Their actual priorities slip. At the end of the week they're behind, and they're not quite sure how it happened.

What they missed is that the senior colleague had no authority to redirect their work. None. They weren't this person's manager. They didn't write the performance review. They had institutional credibility and social comfort with asserting themselves, and the junior employee's brain read those signals as authority. They are not the same thing.

Authority at work is narrow and formal. It means someone has a legitimate role in decisions about your employment: your compensation, your advancement, whether you stay. For most people, that is exactly one person. Their direct manager. Maybe a skip-level relationship in certain organizations, but even then the actual formal power usually sits with the direct manager.

Everyone else is a peer. A senior peer maybe, with more context and more credibility, but a peer. And peers, by definition, do not have authority over your day.

Here's the practical version of that. If you're trying to figure out where someone actually sits relative to you, ask yourself one question: does this person have any formal role in my performance review or in decisions about my employment? If the answer is no, you already have what you need. Treat them accordingly.

Which means: like an intelligent, professional adult who deserves your respect and your consideration, but not your unconditional compliance.

The thing people underestimate is that senior colleagues often prefer it this way. I've seen this pattern so many times that I'm confident in saying it directly. When a junior employee quietly absorbs every dropped task, never asks a clarifying question, never pushes back on an informal priority redirect, they often look less capable, not more. The person watching thinks: does this person not understand how work gets prioritized here? Do they not have enough conviction about their own responsibilities to protect them?

The junior employees who stood out over the years were almost never the most deferential ones. They were the ones who, when a senior colleague dropped something on their plate, said something like: "I want to make sure I get this right. Let me check with my manager on where this fits against what I'm already working on." That response is not resistance. It is professionalism. It routes the authority question back to the person who actually has authority, which is exactly where it belongs.

There are environments where this framework gets complicated, and I'd be doing you a disservice if I didn't say so. Some organizations have real informal hierarchies that the org chart doesn't capture. Consulting firms often work this way. Some engineering cultures do too. In those places, ignoring the social authority of a senior person can carry genuine consequences even if they hold no formal power over you.

If you're early in a new job, spend a few weeks watching before you act. Who defers to whom, and in what contexts? When a senior person expresses a preference, does leadership seem to weight it? Are there patterns around which junior employees get access to the best work? Those observations tell you something important about the real power structure, which might look different from the official one.

But even in cultures with strong informal hierarchies, there is almost always a version of "let me confirm this with my manager" that lands as competent rather than defiant. You're not refusing. You're routing the request correctly. That matters.

The other thing worth naming: this problem doesn't land evenly. People who grew up in environments with high deference norms, first-generation professionals, people who came up in family or cultural contexts where questioning an elder had real social costs, often have a version of this template that runs deeper than average. The intellectual understanding that someone isn't your boss doesn't automatically translate into different behavior. The instinct was built over a long time.

If that's you, the path is practice in low-stakes situations. "I'm in the middle of something, can I come find you in a few minutes?" said to a senior colleague who clearly isn't your manager is a small rep. Do it a few times. Watch what happens. Notice that the world doesn't end, and that more often than not the person responds like the peer they actually are.

Your job is to do excellent work for the people who are genuinely accountable for your results. That's the whole thing. It is not to absorb informal redirects from people who carry no accountability for whether you succeed or fail.

Knowing who your boss actually is, and treating everyone else with the respect and the ordinary professional directness that peers deserve, is not a political move. It's just an accurate read of how the organization actually works. And operating from an accurate read is always the right foundation.
