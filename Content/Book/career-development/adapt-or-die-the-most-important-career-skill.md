## Adapt or Die

Dr. Jinx had three words above his classroom door.

I was sixteen, sitting in 11th grade honors human physiology, and on the first day somebody always read the sign and asked what it meant. It became a ritual. He'd stop, turn around, and explain it like nothing else he was about to teach that year mattered as much. He wasn't talking about organisms. He wasn't talking about Darwin in the narrow sense. He was talking about life, about the world not holding still, about the only real choice you ever have when things change around you.

I thought about it a lot over the following three decades. Not because I kept it in mind deliberately, but because life kept proving the point without asking my opinion.

Before I was in any kind of professional role, I had a long stretch of jobs that nobody frames as a coherent career path. Baker. Pharmacy delivery driver. Trench digger. Landscaper. Pizza maker. Telemarketer. Each one of those was a different world with different rules, different feedback loops, different definitions of what "doing well" meant. I wasn't building a resume. I was putting myself through college and trying to get through the week.

But here's what I didn't understand at the time: I was training something. Every time I walked into a new context where I didn't know how things worked, I had to build a map from scratch. What matters here? Who do I need to talk to? What does good look like? Where are the invisible rules? At first that process was slow and uncomfortable. After enough repetitions, it got faster. And eventually, the process of building the map became its own skill, separate from any particular domain.

That's what resourcefulness actually is. Not cleverness. Not hustle. The ability to orient quickly in a new environment and figure out what to do next. It's the only professional skill I've developed that has never once lost its value.

Most people don't think about this explicitly. They think about their skills in terms of what they know: the software, the methodology, the industry expertise. Those things matter, but they depreciate. The world shifts underneath them. What you know today is worth less five years from now, and you can either be the person who has rebuilt their capabilities by then or the person who's defending what they knew five years ago.

There's a pattern I've watched play out repeatedly in organizations that have a habit of keeping people for a long time. The company develops genuine loyalty to its long-tenured employees. That loyalty is real and in many cases well-earned. But the people who stay in one place for fifteen or twenty years, especially in a company that isn't asking hard things of them anymore, often quietly stop growing without noticing it happening. They become calibrated to one environment. One set of problems. One way of working. And when the environment shifts, as it always eventually does, they're navigating by a map that no longer matches the territory.

The hard thing about this is that it's invisible from the inside. You feel like you know what you're doing because you do, in the context that made you. It's only when something forces a change that the gap becomes apparent.

The other thing I've noticed is that no one is going to push you toward growth if keeping you comfortable is easier for the organization. And it usually is. Companies don't promote valuable people out the door. They keep them satisfied, give them incremental responsibility, and call it development. If you're waiting for someone else to engineer your adaptability, you'll be waiting a long time.

I moved a lot across my career. Companies, industries, roles, responsibilities. Some of those moves were planned. Some of them were forced on me by circumstances. Every single one of them, without exception, made me a better operator on the other side. Not immediately. The first few months in a new context are usually humbling in a way that's uncomfortable to sit with. You don't know the players. You don't know the history. You don't know the unspoken rules. You are visibly the least experienced person in a lot of rooms.

But that discomfort is the rep. That's what rebuilds the map-making muscle. And the more times you've done it, the less threatening the discomfort feels, because you have evidence that you've come out the other side before.

The practical version of this is not "blow up your career every three years." It's about actively seeking friction when your environment stops providing it naturally. Take the project that's a stretch. Embed yourself with a part of the organization you don't understand yet. Read outside your domain. Talk to people who solve problems that are nothing like yours, because the pattern that unlocks your problem often comes from somewhere unexpected.

And at some point, change the whole context. A new company, a new industry, a new set of problems that don't respond to your existing playbook. That one costs something real. But what it buys you is perspective that you cannot build in any other way. The clearest thinking I've ever done about any organization I've been part of came immediately after I left it, because I finally had enough distance to see what I'd been too close to understand.

Dr. Jinx put those three words above his door because he knew that most of what he was about to teach us would be obsolete by the time we needed it. The specific content of 11th grade physiology wasn't really the point. The point was the posture. The orientation toward change as something to move with, not something to defend against.

Thirty-three years into a working life, I haven't found a better piece of career advice. The world is going to keep changing faster than any of us can predict. The people who will be fine are not the ones with the right skills today. They're the ones who've built the habit of acquiring the next ones.

Adapt or die isn't a metaphor. It's a policy. Build it into how you manage your own career before life builds it in for you.
