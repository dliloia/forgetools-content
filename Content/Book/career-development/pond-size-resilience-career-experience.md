There's a question I've been turning over for a long time, and I keep coming back to it in different forms.

Why do some people fall apart when a company restructures, while someone sitting next to them barely misses a step? Why does a hard conversation that derails one manager roll right off another? Why does the same amount of adversity produce completely different responses in two people with similar credentials, similar roles, similar performance reviews?

For a long time I chalked it up to personality. Some people are resilient, some people aren't. Natural variation, hard to change.

I don't believe that anymore.

What I've come to believe, after a career that has taken me through enough organizational chaos to fill a reasonably depressing memoir, is that resilience is not a trait. It's a library. And the size of that library is determined almost entirely by the variety of situations you've had to navigate in your life.

Here's how I think about it. Imagine every hard problem you've ever solved, every unfamiliar situation you've had to figure out, every time you were the new person in the room without a map, as water in a pond. That pond is your reference set. When something difficult shows up, your brain scans the pond automatically. If it finds something shaped like the new problem, you feel a version of calm, or at least competence. You've seen something like this. You know roughly what it requires. The problem is familiar in its outline even if it's new in its details.

If the pond comes up empty, the problem fills your entire field of view. It feels enormous. Not because it is objectively enormous, but because you have nothing to measure it against.

I grew up working. My first real job was at 13, and from there I strung together enough different kinds of work across enough different contexts that by the time I was managing teams in a serious way, I had seen a genuinely strange variety of problems. Call centers, retail, technology companies, organizations in growth and organizations in freefall. I worked with people whose priorities were completely different from mine, in cultures that operated on assumptions I had to figure out from scratch.

I didn't know I was building anything at the time. I thought I was just working.

But when organizational disruption started happening around me in my thirties and forties, I noticed something. The situations that seemed to immobilize colleagues I respected, people who were smarter than me in specific domains, felt navigable to me. Not easy. But navigable. I had a shape in the pond for most of what I was seeing.

The colleagues who struggled most were often the ones with the cleanest resumes. Same industry their whole career, steady progression, increasing seniority, one or two companies. Deep expertise. Real accomplishment. But when the terrain shifted, they had no reference for operating in unfamiliar terrain, because they'd spent twenty years making sure they were never in it.

That's the trap of the clean, linear career. It optimizes for a single pond and leaves everything else empty.

I want to be careful here because I'm not saying tenure is worthless or that deep expertise in one domain is a mistake. It isn't. There are contexts where you need someone who has spent a decade inside one specific problem space, and breadth alone doesn't substitute for that. But the leaders I've watched adapt most successfully through disruption, whether it was a business model changing underneath them, a market collapsing, a merger scrambling the organizational chart, they all had one thing in common. They had worked in enough different contexts that they knew, at a visceral level, that they could figure out unfamiliar things. They had done it before. The confidence wasn't theoretical, it was built from evidence.

There was a person I managed early in my career who had the strangest resume I'd seen up to that point. A few years in hospitality, a few years running a small business that hadn't worked out, then a pivot into tech that didn't connect obviously to any of it. Every hiring rubric I'd been trained on said to pass. The experience wasn't relevant. The trajectory wasn't clean.

I hired them anyway, mostly on instinct, and they turned out to be one of the most adaptable people I've ever worked with. When we hit a crisis that required someone to step into a completely undefined role and figure it out in real time, they volunteered and then actually did it. When I asked them about it afterward, what they said stuck with me. "I've had to start from scratch a few times. I know I can figure things out."

That's the sentence. That's what a big pond produces. Not arrogance, not recklessness, just a grounded belief that unfamiliarity isn't the same thing as impossibility. They had a track record with themselves of navigating things they didn't know yet.

The question I think is worth sitting with, especially if you're at a point in your career where you're making choices about what's next, is how deliberately you're expanding your pond. Not just deepening what you already know, but adding genuine variety to your reference library.

Uncomfortable assignments. Roles that put you in rooms where you're not the expert. Time spent close to customers who will tell you things your internal world never would. Projects that cross into functions you've never owned. A season of work that looks sideways on paper but drops you into a completely different set of problems.

None of it feels strategic in the moment. It mostly feels like being out of your depth, which is uncomfortable. But being out of your depth is exactly the experience that makes the pond bigger.

Resilience is the return on that discomfort. And you collect it over a career, not all at once, just a little at a time, every time you chose the thing that was harder and less familiar and figured it out anyway.
