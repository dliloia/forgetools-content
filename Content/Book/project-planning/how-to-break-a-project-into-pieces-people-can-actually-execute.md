## The Translation Nobody Does

Early in my career I was handed a project and told it was mine to run. I had a goal, a rough timeline, and a team. What I didn't have was any real sense of how to take the goal and turn it into work people could actually do. So I did what most people in that situation do: I held a kickoff meeting, made a list of big things we needed to accomplish, put names next to each one, and called it a plan.

Three weeks in, we were behind. Not catastrophically, but visibly. I pulled each person in for a quick check-in and discovered the same thing each time. They knew what they were responsible for in a broad sense. They didn't have a clear picture of what they were supposed to do this week, or even this month. The goal was clear. The work wasn't.

That gap, between the goal and the actual work, is where most projects quietly fall apart.

I call it a translation problem. Somebody has to take the destination and convert it into moves. Specific, completable, assignable moves. And that translation almost never happens cleanly in a kickoff meeting, because everyone in the room is still thinking at the level of the goal. Nobody has their head down yet. Nobody has hit the first real obstacle. The granularity that makes a plan executable only becomes obvious once you start asking hard questions about the specifics.

The fix I eventually landed on was simple, though it took me a while to trust it. You start with the first major milestone, and you ask one question: what has to be completely, undeniably true for this to be done? Not "what do we need to work on." What has to be finished. Locked. Closed. The answers to that question give you categories of work. Then you go one level deeper and ask the same question again inside each category. Now you have tasks.

Real tasks. The kind with a single owner, a clear output, and a finish line you can verify.

The distinction between a milestone and a task sounds obvious once you hear it, but I've watched experienced project leads confuse the two consistently throughout their careers. A milestone is a checkpoint. It tells you where you're trying to be. A task is a move. It tells you what to do today. You need both, and they live at different levels of the plan. Mixing them up is how you end up with a team that's busy but not moving, and status meetings that produce updates nobody can actually act on.

The other piece that took me longer to figure out is ownership. Shared ownership feels right in theory. Collaborative, team-oriented, nobody left out. In practice it's an accountability gap wearing a team-player costume. When two people own a task, both of them assume the other has the lead, and the task drifts. I've seen work fall through the cracks not because anyone dropped the ball, but because two people were each holding half a ball and nobody had the whole thing.

One owner. That's the rule. The owner can involve whoever they need. They can ask for help, escalate blockers, pull in a specialist. But there is one person whose name is on it, who knows they are accountable for getting it to done. And done has to be defined. "Done" is not "I worked on it." Done means the output exists in a specific state, ready for whatever comes next. Write that down before the work starts. Thirty seconds of specificity up front prevents a lot of confusion downstream.

There's also a sizing problem that I see constantly. Tasks that are too large are really just smaller projects in disguise. "Build the analytics dashboard" is not a task; it's a cluster of decisions, dependencies, and subtasks that will take weeks to untangle if you treat it like a single unit of work. On the other end, tasks that are granular to the point of absurdity create overhead that costs more than the work itself. The right size is something one person can complete without needing to resolve major open questions first. If they can't start because a decision hasn't been made yet, the decision is the task. Make it explicit, assign it, close it out.

What I've come to believe is that the real work of project leadership isn't the kickoff, it's the breakdown. Anyone can put a goal on a slide and announce a deadline. The harder and more valuable thing is sitting down before the project starts and doing the translation. What are we actually going to do? Who is doing each piece? What does done look like? What has to happen before this other thing can start?

That last question matters more than most planning frameworks acknowledge. Dependencies don't disappear just because you didn't map them. They show up later, usually at the worst possible moment, as a blocked team member or a missed handoff. The projects I've seen run cleanly are the ones where someone took the time early to trace the sequence: this has to happen before that, which means this person needs to finish their piece before that person can start theirs. That map is the difference between parallel work that actually accelerates you and parallel work that creates four simultaneous near-finishes and no real progress.

None of this requires elaborate tooling or a formal methodology. The underlying skill is just clarity of thought applied to work. What are we doing, exactly? Who owns it? When is it done? What has to come first? Answer those questions for every milestone before the work starts, and you'll spend your project in execution rather than in discovery mode.

The teams I've seen struggle most aren't lacking effort. They're lacking the specificity that turns a goal into a sequence of moves. That specificity is something a leader has to create. It doesn't emerge on its own from a room full of motivated people. Someone has to do the translation, and that someone is you.
