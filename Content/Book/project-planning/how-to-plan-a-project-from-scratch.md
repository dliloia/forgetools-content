## The Project That Taught Me to Plan

Early in my career, I was handed a project with a short runway and a clear expectation that I'd figure it out. I was confident. I had energy. I dove straight into execution.

Six weeks later, I was behind, my team was stressed, and I was in a room trying to explain to a senior leader what went wrong. I remember sitting across from her and not being able to give a coherent answer. Not because I didn't understand the situation, but because I'd never actually defined what success looked like at the start. We'd been working hard. We just hadn't been working toward a shared, concrete target.

That meeting was uncomfortable enough that I never repeated the mistake.

What I learned, the hard way, is that the first job on any project is not to start the work. It's to define what done looks like, in language specific enough that you could use it to settle a disagreement.

"We want to improve the customer experience" is not a definition of done. "Customers can complete checkout in three steps instead of seven, and we'll verify that with a usability test of fifteen participants before we launch" is a definition of done. The difference between those two statements is the difference between a project that finishes and one that drifts.

The outcome definition is the filter for everything that follows. When someone suggests adding scope, you hold the definition up and ask whether the addition moves you toward the stated outcome or sideways from it. When a stakeholder wants to accelerate the timeline, you look at what the definition requires and tell them honestly what's achievable. When two team members disagree about priorities, the definition resolves it. Or it should, if you wrote it clearly enough.

Getting to that clarity takes a specific kind of conversation, one most leaders rush past because it feels like process overhead. It isn't. It is the work.

---

The second thing I learned was about dependencies, and this took longer to sink in.

Dependencies are the seams where projects come apart. Not because anyone dropped the ball, but because one team assumed another team would move faster, or because an external approval everyone expected in a week took three, or because a system integration turned out to require a database migration that nobody had scoped. I have been in all of these situations. The ones that hurt the most were the ones where I saw the dependency early and assumed it would resolve itself.

It never resolves itself. It surfaces, at the worst possible moment, as a blocker.

Before any timeline gets built, the dependency map has to come first. For every major piece of work, ask what has to be true before this can start. Write it down. For anything controlled by someone outside your team, reach out to that person before the project kicks off and get a realistic estimate from them directly. Don't assume. Don't estimate on their behalf. Ask.

This takes an extra few days at the start of the project. It saves weeks at the end.

---

The third thing, and maybe the most stubborn habit to break, is the instinct to build timelines optimistically.

When you plan forward, starting from today and working out to the deadline, everything in the distance looks manageable. Week one is clearly scoped. Week eight feels far away. So you make week eight's assumptions generous, because there's time to figure it out. Then week eight arrives and you're scrambling.

Planning backwards fixes this. Start from the deadline and pull the thread back. What has to be complete the week before the deadline? What has to be in place for that milestone to be reachable? Keep going, all the way back to today. When you do this, you see the real constraints early, while you can still act on them. If the plan requires six weeks of parallel work and you have four weeks total, you know that on day one. You can make a real decision: compress scope, extend the date, bring in more resources. Any of those is a reasonable choice. Making that choice in week five, under pressure, is not reasonable. It's crisis management with a PowerPoint attached.

---

There's a version of this chapter I could write that's just a planning template. Here are your columns, here are your rows, fill in the boxes. I'm not going to do that, because the template isn't the point.

The point is the thinking behind the template. Why you map dependencies before you build the timeline. Why you assign one person's name to each deliverable instead of a team. Why you build in a buffer even when everyone's pressuring you to show a tighter schedule. Why the kickoff meeting should be a validation of a plan that already exists, not a working session to figure out what the project is.

Every one of those choices is the result of a lesson learned on a real project, usually by getting it wrong first. The plan is where you apply those lessons before the project starts, so you're not in a room six weeks later trying to explain what happened.

Do the planning. All of it. Before anything else starts.

The thinking you do in that first week is the highest-leverage work on the project. Everything downstream is execution.
