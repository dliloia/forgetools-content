## You Can't Improve What You Can't See

Early in my career, I worked for a manager who had an answer for everything. Ask him how the team was performing and he'd tell you without hesitation. Ask him whether the new process was working and he'd give you a confident read. He had opinions, he was decisive, and he almost always sounded sure.

He was also wrong a lot. Not catastrophically wrong, just consistently a quarter-turn off from reality. Problems that should have surfaced in week two showed up in week eight. Changes that should have been reversed kept running. And when things finally broke, there was always genuine surprise in the room, including from him.

I didn't understand why until a few years later, when I started managing my own teams and ran into the same pattern in myself. The issue wasn't confidence or competence. The issue was that we were both operating on feel, and feel is a terrible measurement instrument.

Here's what feel actually is. It's a weighted average of the conversations you happened to have, filtered by who's willing to talk to you, shaped by whatever problem you were most focused on that week. It sounds like insight. It functions like noise.

The word I eventually landed on for what fixes this came from Amazon. Mechanism. It's a specific term there, used precisely. A mechanism is a practice that produces measurable signal on a repeatable schedule. It doesn't rely on someone remembering to check. It doesn't require the right person to surface the problem. It runs, it produces signal, and it creates a forcing function around what that signal means.

The distinction that mattered most to me was mechanism versus metric. A metric is a number. Revenue, headcount, error rate, ticket volume. Numbers are inert until someone picks them up and does something with them, and the person picking them up shapes what they mean. A mechanism is the system around the number that makes sure someone acts on it consistently, before the moment of crisis.

I'll make this concrete. Tracking customer complaint volume is a metric. A weekly review where you look at the top complaint categories, name an owner for each, and follow up the following week on whether the owner closed the issue is a mechanism. The metric tells you something is happening. The mechanism ensures something gets done about it.

One of the best mechanisms I ever saw in action was a simple weekly written status format. No meeting, no verbal update, just three sentences per person: what shipped, what's blocked, what's next. The discipline of writing it down changed everything. Patterns in blockers showed up inside two weeks. Problems that would have stayed invisible in a verbal standup became visible in the aggregate text. And the record was searchable, which meant six months later you could actually understand what happened and when.

The barrier to installing a mechanism like that is almost nothing. It's three sentences and five minutes. Most teams don't do it. And I've noticed over the years that the resistance to installing a mechanism is almost always proportional to how badly it's needed. Teams that are running well are usually happy to add a feedback loop. Teams that are struggling often push back, because a feedback loop will confirm what everyone already suspects and nobody wants to name.

That's the real cost of operating without mechanisms. It's not just that you miss problems. It's that you create an organizational culture where problems are allowed to stay invisible because there's no agreed-upon way to surface them. The leader's feel becomes the official read, and anyone who sees something different has to fight against the authority of the feel to get it heard.

I managed a large distributed team through a period of significant process change, and we built a simple error-rate tracker for our core output. One number, tracked weekly, visible to everyone. The number focused every conversation. When someone proposed a change, the question became concrete: will this move the number? When the number moved badly one week, we didn't debate whether there was a problem, we looked at what changed. The mechanism turned abstract arguments about quality into specific conversations about cause.

Good judgment matters. I'm not arguing otherwise. But good judgment has a scale limit. Once a team gets past a certain size, maybe ten people, maybe fifteen, no single person's judgment can cover the whole surface area. Information filters. Context gets lost. The leader is operating on what got escalated, which is always a subset of what actually happened.

Mechanisms are how you extend your perception past the limits of your own attention. They're not a replacement for judgment. They're the input that makes judgment reliable.

The simplest version of this principle: pick one thing your team does every week that you currently evaluate by feel. Design the most minimal possible practice that produces real signal on it, something that runs on a schedule and creates a record. Run it for a month. The feel and the signal will disagree at least once, probably more. That disagreement is the whole point. That's where the real learning is.

You can't fix what you can't measure. That's not a management aphorism. It's just true, and most teams are learning it the expensive way.
