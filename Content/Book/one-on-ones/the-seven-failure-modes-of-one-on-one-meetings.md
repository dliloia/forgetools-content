I once worked with a director who was proud of his management habits. "I never miss a 1:1," he told me. And he was right. The meetings were on his calendar like clockwork, every Tuesday, every direct report, 30 minutes each.

Then three of his six reports left within a four-month span.

When I sat down with two of them during their exit interviews, I heard the same thing in different words: "He didn't actually listen." One of them told me, "Every week I'd walk in and he'd ask about project timelines for 25 minutes, then say 'anything else?' with two minutes left. I stopped trying."

He was holding the meetings. He just wasn't holding 1:1s.

The distinction between a real 1:1 and a meeting that borrows the name is one of the most consequential differences in management. Over years of managing, observing, and coaching, I've identified seven specific failure modes that turn this critical practice into empty routine. Most managers are guilty of at least two or three at any given time.

**The first and most pervasive failure is converting the 1:1 into a status update.** This feels productive because information is exchanged. The manager asks where things stand, the employee walks through their task list, and both leave feeling like the meeting had a purpose. But the purpose it served, gathering project updates, is something that Slack, email, dashboards, and standups already handle. The 1:1 is the only meeting on a manager's calendar that exists for the person, not the work. When you fill it with status, you've eliminated the one space where your direct report can talk about what actually matters to them.

**The second failure mode is the cancellation pattern.** I need to be blunt about this one because I've seen its effects up close dozens of times. When a manager cancels a 1:1, the employee hears one thing: you are less important than whatever else just came up. Do it once, you're forgiven. Do it twice in a row, and something shifts. The employee starts protecting themselves emotionally. They invest less in the relationship. They stop bringing up problems because they've internalized that this time isn't really theirs.

I managed a team once where I inherited a direct report who never brought anything to our 1:1s. She'd shrug and say everything was fine. It took me three months to learn that her previous manager had canceled their 1:1s so frequently that she'd stopped treating the meeting as real. She had trained herself not to need it, and in the process, she'd stopped trusting that any manager would actually show up consistently.

**The third failure mode is the manager monologue.** The 1:1 belongs to the employee. Full stop. Yet many managers walk in with a list of topics, talk through all of them, ask "any questions?" and consider the meeting complete. This isn't a 1:1. It's a broadcast. The employee's job in this version is to sit, nod, and absorb. Nothing about that builds trust or surfaces the issues that a manager actually needs to hear.

**Fourth is the follow-through gap.** An employee raises a concern. The manager says "I'll look into it." The next week, nothing has changed. The employee mentions it again. "Right, I'll follow up." Another week passes. By the third cycle, the employee has learned a powerful lesson: raising things in this meeting is pointless. They go quiet, and the manager mistakes that silence for satisfaction.

This one is insidious because the manager often doesn't even realize the promise was made. It felt like a small thing in the moment. But to the employee who gathered the courage to raise it, it was anything but small. Every unkept commitment erodes trust in a way that's almost impossible to measure until the relationship has already degraded past the point of repair.

**Fifth is the vague questioning trap.** "How's everything going?" is not a real question. It's a social pleasantry disguised as management. The answer is almost always "good" or "fine," and both parties accept this as if actual information was exchanged. It wasn't. Generic questions get generic answers because they don't create enough psychological safety for honest responses. Nobody tells their manager they're frustrated, overwhelmed, or job hunting in response to "how's it going?" That kind of honesty requires specific, thoughtful questions that signal genuine interest.

**Sixth is structural absence.** Some managers are allergic to any kind of format, believing that structure kills authenticity. So they wing it. Sometimes the conversation is useful. Sometimes it drifts into 30 minutes of small talk. Sometimes it becomes crisis management for whatever fire is burning that day. Without a lightweight framework, 1:1s become random, and random meetings eventually feel pointless to both parties.

**The seventh and most fundamental failure is treating the 1:1 as optional.** This is the meta-failure that enables every other one. When a manager views 1:1s as nice-to-have rather than essential practice, all the other failure modes become inevitable. Of course you'll cancel when busy, because it's optional. Of course you'll let it become a status update, because you haven't committed to it being anything more.

I hear the same justifications repeatedly. "My door is always open." "We talk constantly in Slack." "My team knows they can come to me anytime." None of these replace a dedicated, recurring, protected space for real conversation. The hallway chat is not where someone tells you they feel undervalued. The Slack message is not where they reveal they're burning out. Those conversations need a container that signals: this time is yours, I'm here to listen, and what you say matters.

The cost of these failures compounds in ways that are easy to miss because the damage is invisible until it's catastrophic. A manager running broken 1:1s with a team of six for a full year hasn't just wasted 300 hours of combined meeting time. They've missed hundreds of small signals. The frustration that could have been addressed in week two but festered until month six. The career aspiration that was never discussed. The interpersonal conflict that grew toxic in silence.

And then the resignation arrives, and the manager says, "I had no idea."

Of course you didn't. You canceled the meeting where you would have found out.

The 1:1 is not a calendar obligation. It is the primary mechanism through which a manager builds trust, gathers signal, develops people, and prevents the slow, invisible erosion of a team. Every other management practice depends on the foundation that good 1:1s create.

The question isn't whether you have 1:1s on your calendar. It's whether what happens inside them actually deserves the name.
