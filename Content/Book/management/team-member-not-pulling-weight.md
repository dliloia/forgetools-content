There was a manager I coached a few years back who had a situation like this. She managed a small operations team, and one of her people, someone who'd been a dependable contributor for over a year, started slipping. Missed deadlines, work that came back with more errors than usual, a couple of no-shows on team calls. She liked this person. That made it harder. She kept waiting for things to turn around, kept telling herself it was probably temporary, kept adjusting coverage quietly rather than having the conversation.

By the time she came to me, two of her other team members had already brought it up to her directly, and one of them was actively looking for another role because they were exhausted from picking up the slack.

She'd waited two months to say something. The problem was now three problems.

I've seen this play out more times than I can count. The instinct to delay a hard conversation is almost universal. It comes from a real place, usually care for the person, sometimes conflict aversion, sometimes genuine uncertainty about what's causing the problem. But the delay always costs more than the conversation would have.

Here's how I think about what you actually do when someone on your team stops performing.

The first thing is separating what you know from what you've been told. Your team will notice before you do, and they will tell you. That information matters, but it cannot be the foundation of your intervention. What you need is your own documented record of specific gaps: which deadlines, which deliverables, which meetings. Not because you're building a legal case, but because you need to be able to say "here's what I'm seeing" in a way that's grounded in fact and can't be dismissed as gossip or perception.

Doing this work also forces you to ask a question that's uncomfortable but necessary: were the expectations actually clear? Sometimes what looks like someone not pulling their weight is at least partly a failure of alignment. You want to go into this conversation having already checked your own record. If the expectations were murky, you own part of that, and acknowledging it in the conversation will go a long way.

The conversation itself should open with your observations, not with what the team said or what you've been hearing. "I want to talk about a pattern I've been seeing" is the right frame. Then name it specifically. What slipped, when, and by how much. Don't soften it until the point is gone. The person needs to understand that this is serious and that you're not there for a casual check-in.

Then ask a real question and listen. "What's going on?" sounds almost too simple, but most managers either skip it or ask it in a way that makes clear they've already decided the answer. If someone was a strong performer for a year and has shifted, something changed. It might be personal. It might be the work itself, a skill gap, a project that's a bad fit, expectations that were never fully clear. Or it might be disengagement, and that's information too. You won't know until you ask and actually hear the answer.

What you avoid saying is "the team has noticed." Not as an opener. Leading with what other people have said puts the person on defense immediately and signals that you've been collecting testimony rather than managing. Hold what the team told you in reserve. Your position is yours; it's based on what you've observed.

The conversation ends with specific, measurable expectations and a timeline. Not "let's get things back on track." What does back on track actually mean? Which deadlines, which deliverables, what quality standard? By when will you reassess? And then say plainly that the current pattern can't continue. That's not a threat. It's respect, delivered as honesty.

The follow-up matters as much as the conversation. Most managers have the hard talk and then wait to see if things improve. That's not managing, that's hoping. Set a check-in at two weeks. Come back to the specific things you named. If you're seeing improvement, say so explicitly. People need to know when they're getting back on track. If you're still seeing the same pattern, the conversation shifts. You've coached. Now you're in performance-management territory, and the person deserves to know that too.

There's also the rest of the team to think about, and this is the part that gets neglected. The person covering the gap will burn out. The pointed comment in the standup will keep coming. If you address the underperformer privately and say nothing to the people absorbing the impact, the resentment grows. You don't need to disclose what's happening in your conversations with the underperformer. But you do need to let your senior contributor know they were heard and that the situation is being managed. "I hear you, I take it seriously, I'm working on it" is enough. They need to know they're not invisible.

The cost of avoiding this conversation is almost always higher than the conversation itself. Your best people will not wait indefinitely. They will stop covering the gap, or they will start looking for somewhere else to work, and either way you've traded a solvable problem for a much harder one. The person who was good at this job once deserves the direct conversation before you've made any decisions. And your team deserves a manager who enforces the bar rather than quietly adjusting around the people who aren't meeting it.

That's the job. Fair and firm, in that order, at the same time.
