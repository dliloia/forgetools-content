## When One Person Stops Delivering

I've had this situation more times than I can count, and it never gets easier, but it does get clearer the more you go through it. Someone who was solid starts slipping. Deadlines move. Quality dips. The team feels it before you've done anything about it, and now you're behind the curve.

The Morgan scenario is one of the most instructive management situations I know, because it's loaded with the exact traps that turn a fixable performance problem into a team morale crisis, and sometimes into a legal one.

Let me walk you through how I think about it.

The first trap is waiting too long because you like the person. I've done this. You see the old Morgan, the strong first year, the good intentions, and you give it another week, then another. Meanwhile your best people are absorbing the slack and getting frustrated. I had a team member come to me once after carrying a colleague's work for two months and tell me, quietly, that she was thinking about leaving. Not because of the work itself, but because she felt like the arrangement was invisible to me. That one hit hard. She stayed, but the conversation made it clear I had waited too long on a different problem.

The second trap is building your case on hearsay. I've seen managers walk into these conversations carrying everything the team said, framing it as "I've been hearing from multiple people," and watching the whole conversation derail into damage control. The person you're trying to coach is now focused entirely on figuring out who said what. You've lost the thread. Base the conversation on what you personally observed, your own documentation, your own meetings, your own review of the work. The team's frustration is context for you. It is not evidence to present.

Before I have this kind of conversation, I sit down and write out what I actually know. Specific dates. Specific missed commitments. Specific output quality issues I reviewed myself. This is not to build a prosecution. It's so I can stay grounded when the conversation gets uncomfortable, and it will get uncomfortable. When someone minimizes or deflects, I can come back to the specific example rather than getting pulled into a debate about general impressions.

The conversation itself has to open with curiosity before it lands on consequences. I've learned this the hard way. The first time I managed someone through a real performance issue, I went in with the data and I presented it like a case. The person shut down completely. I got nothing useful out of the meeting, and the relationship took weeks to repair.

What I do now is name the pattern I've seen and then ask a genuinely open question. "I want to understand what's going on" is not a rhetorical setup. I mean it. Because sometimes what looks like disengagement is someone going through something real that nobody told me about. I had a team member once whose parent was in the hospital for six weeks. He never said a word. He was trying to hold it together and failing, and I almost had a very different conversation with him than the one we ended up having, which was about how we could adjust his workload temporarily and what support looked like.

I'm not saying every underperformer has a sympathetic explanation. Some people have simply checked out, and you'll know the difference pretty quickly from the conversation. But you have to be willing to hear the real answer before you decide what it is.

After the conversation, wherever it ends up, you need to leave with specific and measurable expectations in writing. Not a vibe, not "let's see how the next few weeks go." Concrete commitments and a concrete timeline, shared in a follow-up message so there's no ambiguity about what was agreed. Then you follow up on exactly those things in subsequent one-on-ones. Not obliquely. Directly. "We said four weeks ago that you'd hit your committed dates and flag slippage before it happened. Let's look at how that went."

The formal performance process, if it comes to that, is not a weapon. It's a structure that protects everyone involved and forces the kind of clarity that should have been there from the beginning. Some people respond to that level of seriousness and turn it around. Others don't, but they exit through a fair process, and that matters both for them and for you.

The hardest part of this whole situation isn't the conversation with the underperformer. It's what you owe the rest of the team. They can't know the details, but they need to see that you're managing the situation. A senior team member who comes to you with feedback like "I've been picking up three of this person's tasks" took a risk to tell you that. Acknowledge it. Tell them you're on it and that you can't share more than that. Then actually be on it.

Your standards are visible to your team whether you intend to show them or not. What you tolerate becomes the floor. The best people on your team will calibrate to that floor, and if it's low enough, the best ones leave first.

Address it clearly, address it early, and follow through. That's the whole framework.
