## When They Ask Before They're Ready

I've been on both sides of this conversation.

Early in my career, I asked for a promotion I wasn't ready for. I knew I was doing good work. I thought that was enough. My manager at the time gave me one of those non-answers that felt diplomatic in the moment and left me completely in the dark for months afterward. I didn't know if I was being managed out, overlooked, or if the answer was actually yes and I just needed to be patient. I spent the next four months anxious and confused when I could have been developing.

That experience stayed with me. It's a big part of why I became as direct as I am now when someone on my team asks me the hard question.

The scenario plays out like this more often than you'd expect. A solid performer, someone you genuinely like and want to keep, asks for a promotion they haven't earned yet. They've prepared for the conversation. They have their list. Some of what they've accomplished is real and worth acknowledging. But the gap between where they are and where the next role requires them to be is also real, and you can see it clearly even if they can't.

What you do next matters enormously.

The temptation is to soften it. To say "let's keep this conversation going" or "I think you're on the right path" without actually landing the honest answer. Managers do this because they care about the person sitting across from them and they don't want to discourage them. I understand that instinct. I've felt it. But it's the wrong call.

Vague answers to direct questions are not kindness. They're a way of avoiding discomfort at someone else's expense. The person asking you walks out of the room not knowing where they stand. They interpret the ambiguity however their anxiety leads them, which is usually in the wrong direction. And they stop trusting you to tell them the truth, which means every future conversation you have with them is operating with a little less signal.

The honest answer, delivered with care, is the respectful one.

For the Taylor scenario, the honest answer is something like: "You're not ready yet, and I want to explain what I mean by that because I think the path is real." That's not discouraging. That's a manager taking someone's career seriously.

The specificity matters as much as the directness. If you tell someone they're not ready without telling them what "ready" actually looks like, you haven't given them feedback. You've given them a verdict without a standard to appeal to. The gap for Taylor isn't effort or work ethic, those are genuine. The gap is scope. Senior-level work is about your own output. Staff-level work is about your impact on others' output. Driving architecture decisions that other engineers have to align to. Mentoring someone through a hard technical problem. Owning a piece of a system that lives beyond your own tickets. That's a different skill set, and it doesn't just appear because someone works harder at what they're already doing well.

Once you've named the gap specifically, the next question is what you're going to do together to close it. And I want to be clear about that word: together. The development plan isn't something you hand to the employee and tell them to go execute. You own part of it. If Taylor needs cross-team architecture experience to demonstrate Staff-level work, you need to find or create an opportunity for that. A project with real scope. A decision that needs an owner. That's your job as the manager.

Set a specific milestone 90 days out. Not "show more leadership." Something observable, something you can both evaluate at the end of the window. Then put the follow-up on the calendar the same day. Not "we'll revisit this," a specific date. And then show up to that conversation with your half of the work done.

The version of this story that bothers me most is the manager who gives the employee just enough hope to keep them around without giving them any actual guidance, and then watches them get passed over again while someone else gets the promotion. By the time the employee starts to understand what happened, they've lost a year of development time and they're quietly resentful. The manager usually doesn't even realize they caused it because the damage accumulated slowly.

I've also watched the other version play out. A manager who sits down with a strong employee, tells them the truth about where the gap is, makes it clear the answer is "not yet" and not "no," and then actually builds the path with them. That employee goes and does the work. Ninety days later, the conversation looks different. And when the promotion eventually comes through, it sticks because the person was genuinely ready for it.

That's the goal. Not just to make someone feel good about a hard conversation, but to actually move them forward.

The conversation Taylor deserves is one where they leave knowing exactly where they stand, exactly what needs to change, and exactly what you're going to do to help them get there. Most managers don't give that conversation. The ones who do build the kind of loyalty and trust that holds teams together through hard stretches.

Say the true thing. Name the specific gap. Build the path together. Then do your part of the work.

That's it.
