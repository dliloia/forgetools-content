## What Most Presenters Get Wrong

I have sat through more presentations than I can count across a career that started long before slide decks existed. I watched people present from overhead projectors and whiteboards and printed handouts. I watched the transition to PowerPoint, then to Keynote, then to whatever this week's preferred tool is. The technology changed completely. The core problem didn't move an inch.

Most presentations are built for the presenter, not the audience. The structure follows the order in which the speaker thought about the problem. The slides exist to remind the speaker what comes next. The examples are the ones the speaker finds interesting. And when it's over, the audience walks out with a vague sense of what happened and almost no memory of the specifics.

I want to tell you about two presentations I watched within about six months of each other, because the contrast between them explains everything worth knowing about this skill.

The first one was a regional business review. Solid company, talented team, real results to share. The presenter had been working on the deck for two weeks. It showed. There were forty-three slides. The first eight were background and context. Then came the methodology section. Then the results, broken up across twelve slides with charts that required explanation every time because they weren't labeled clearly. The recommendation showed up on slide thirty-nine. By then, the senior leaders in the room had already formed their own opinions, started side conversations, and checked their phones at least twice. The presenter was smart, prepared, and completely ignored.

The second presentation was a budget ask. The leader walked in, put up a single slide with two numbers on it, and said, "We have a $400,000 problem and a $150,000 solution. Give me fifteen minutes." She had the room's complete attention for the next fifteen minutes. Questions were sharp and engaged because everyone understood what they were evaluating. She got the budget.

The difference wasn't confidence or charisma. It was clarity about what the audience needed to know and in what order.

## The Question You Have to Answer First

Before I ever start building a presentation, I force myself to write one sentence: what do I want to be true inside this room when I walk out of it that wasn't true when I walked in?

That sentence is the whole job. Everything else, the structure, the slides, the examples, the level of detail, is just the mechanism for producing that outcome. If I can't write that sentence, I'm not ready to build anything yet.

The reason most presentations fail is that the presenter skips this step. They go straight to the slides and start organizing content. What they produce is an organized collection of information with no clear purpose. The audience receives information but doesn't know what to do with it, and without knowing what to do with it, they forget it almost immediately.

I learned this the hard way early in my career. I had a presentation to a leadership team about a process change I'd been working on for months. I knew the material cold. I built a thorough deck. I walked through every piece of it systematically. And at the end, the most senior person in the room said, "So what are you asking us to do?" I didn't have a clean answer. I'd built the presentation to demonstrate how much work I'd done, not to ask for a specific decision. I walked out with nothing resolved and a clear lesson I've carried ever since.

## Structure as a Form of Respect

When you hand someone a well-structured presentation, you're telling them you thought about their experience before you thought about your comfort. That's a form of respect, and people feel it even if they can't articulate it.

The structure that works most reliably follows a simple logic: here's what we know, here's what changed or what's at stake, here's the question that creates, and here's my answer. Lead with the answer. Support it with evidence. End with what you need from them.

This feels wrong to most people because school trained us to build toward conclusions. Academic writing presents evidence, develops arguments, and then arrives at a finding. That format works on paper when the reader controls the pace. In a room with twenty people who have other things on their minds, building toward your conclusion means you've lost half of them before you arrive there. They stopped listening and started guessing, and they're making decisions based on incomplete information.

Lead with the answer. It feels exposed, like you're making yourself vulnerable before you've defended your position. That exposure is the point. It tells the audience exactly what they're evaluating, and it keeps their attention because they want to know if you can back it up.

## What Stays in the Room After You Leave

I've thought a lot about what actually makes something memorable, not just in presentations but in any communication. And what I keep coming back to is this: people remember feeling something. They remember being surprised, or moved, or suddenly clear about something that had been fuzzy. They remember the moment the room shifted.

They do not remember your third bullet point. They do not remember the chart on slide seven. They remember the story you told before that chart that made them care about the numbers.

A VP I worked with used to open every business review with a customer story. One specific customer, one specific experience, good or bad. Two minutes. No slides. Then she'd go into the data. And what people remembered a week later wasn't the data. It was the customer. The data made sense because of the customer. The recommendation felt urgent because of the customer. She understood that information needs an emotional container to survive in memory.

That's not manipulation. That's just how people work. If you want your ideas to stick, they need to connect to something your audience already cares about. Build that connection deliberately, before you go anywhere near the content.

The mechanics of a good presentation are learnable by anyone. The hard part is caring enough about your audience to do the work before you walk in the room.
