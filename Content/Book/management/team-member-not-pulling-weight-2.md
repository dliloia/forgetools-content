## When Someone on Your Team Stops Pulling Their Weight

Early in my career, I made the mistake almost every new manager makes. Someone on my team was underperforming. I could see it, my other team members could see it, and I did absolutely nothing for about six weeks because I kept telling myself the situation would resolve on its own.

It did not resolve on its own. What happened instead was that my best people started to get visibly frustrated, the underperforming employee got comfortable with the lower standard I was quietly accepting, and by the time I finally had the conversation I should have had in week two, the hole was much deeper than it needed to be.

I learned an expensive lesson that year: delay is its own decision. When you wait, you're choosing the status quo. And in a performance situation, the status quo is almost always getting worse.

The scenario I want to walk through is one you'll encounter more than once in your career. You have a team member, let's call them Morgan, who was strong when they joined but has been increasingly inconsistent for the past two months. Missed self-imposed deadlines. Work requiring more review cycles than anyone else's. Unexplained absences from team meetings. In your 1:1s, they tell you things are "on track," but the output says otherwise.

Your best team members have noticed. One of them came to you privately and said they'd picked up three of Morgan's tasks in the last month and were getting burned out. Another made a pointed comment in standup.

You like Morgan. That complicates it. But it doesn't change what you need to do.

The first thing I'd tell any manager in this situation is to separate what you've observed from what you've been told. You have two sources of information here: your own direct observation of the work product, the deadlines, the meeting attendance, and what your team members have told you. Both are real, but only one of them belongs in your conversation with Morgan.

When you sit down with Morgan, you're going in with your own evidence. Specific missed deadlines. Specific review cycle data. Specific meeting absences. You've written these down before you walk in. You know what you've seen, and you're naming it directly.

What you're not doing is walking in with "the team has noticed" or "people are talking." That turns a performance conversation into a referendum, and Morgan will spend the whole conversation trying to figure out who said what instead of engaging with the actual problem.

Here's what that conversation sounds like in practice. You open with something like: "I want to talk through some patterns I've been seeing over the past two months. You've missed several of your own deadlines, the review cycle on your work has been longer than we're used to seeing from you, and you weren't in two team meetings without any heads-up. I want to understand what's going on."

Then you stop. You don't soften it. You don't immediately pivot to "but I know you've been working hard." You let that land and you wait.

This is the part most managers rush past. What Morgan says in the next two minutes matters enormously. Some people are relieved when someone finally names the thing. Some people get defensive. Some people tell you something is happening in their personal life that explains everything and that you had no idea about. The response tells you which kind of conversation you're actually in.

I managed someone once who had been visibly struggling for about six weeks. I had their data, I had my observations, I went in confident that I was about to have a performance conversation. And in the first three minutes, they told me their spouse had been diagnosed with a serious illness two months prior and they hadn't told anyone at work.

That changed everything about how I approached the next 60 days. The performance expectations didn't disappear, but the plan I built with that person looked completely different than the one I'd walked in with. If I had gone in assuming I already knew the cause, I would have missed all of it.

The lesson isn't that you should assume something difficult is happening. It's that you should go in genuinely curious about the cause before deciding on the response.

Once you've had the conversation and you understand more about what's going on, you close it with clear expectations and a timeline. This is non-negotiable regardless of the cause. "I need to see X, Y, and Z in the next 30 days" is a sentence that needs to happen before you both leave the room. Vague improvement conversations are not improvement conversations. They're expensive wishes.

Write it down afterward. Send Morgan a short note summarizing what you discussed and what you agreed to. Not a formal warning, just a clear record. This protects Morgan because there's no ambiguity about what's expected. It protects you for the same reason.

The team frustration piece is a separate problem, and I want to be clear that it has to stay separate. What the rest of the team is experiencing is real. The burnout your senior contributor mentioned deserves a response. But that response is yours to own, not Morgan's. You can redistribute some of the overloaded work short-term. You can acknowledge, privately, that you've heard the concern and you're addressing it. What you can't do is involve the team in a situation that belongs between you and Morgan.

The moment you let team sentiment drive a performance conversation, you've made it political. Morgan stops being able to engage with the actual feedback and starts feeling like they're on trial.

One more thing, because it matters more than most managers realize. Your four solid contributors are watching what you do here. They're not watching because they want Morgan fired. Most of them just want to know that you see what's happening and that you're not going to let the standard erode. When they see you address the situation clearly and fairly, it actually builds their confidence in you.

When they see you let it slide indefinitely, they start wondering whether their own effort actually matters.

That's the real cost of waiting. Not just Morgan's output. The signal it sends to everyone else about what this team's standards actually are.

Have the conversation. Be direct. Be curious before you're certain. Set real expectations. And follow up, because one conversation is never the whole plan.
