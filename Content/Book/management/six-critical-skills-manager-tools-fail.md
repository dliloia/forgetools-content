Strip management down far enough and you get to something that fits on a single page. I've been doing this work for a long time, and when I stop trying to make it sound sophisticated, the whole job reduces to six things: running 1:1s that actually surface what's happening, hiring well, setting goals that matter, managing through projects without micromanaging or disappearing, giving feedback continuously, and coaching yourself.

That last one is the one nobody wants to talk about, and we'll get there. But first I want to sit with all six for a moment, because the list looks deceptively simple and the practice is anything but.

I remember the first time I tried to articulate this to a manager I was developing, a guy in his early thirties who was smart, ambitious, and deeply convinced that his job was to have the right answers. He'd built his whole career on being the expert in the room, and now he had a team of eight and the job was completely different and he knew it, but he couldn't quite see what the new job actually was. I drew the six on a whiteboard. He stared at them for a long time. Then he said, "I'm good at three of these," and named them, and he was right. And then he said, "I actively avoid one of them," and named it, and he was right about that too.

The one he avoided was coaching himself. He had no practice of looking at his own patterns, no habit of examining why he behaved a certain way under pressure, no real interest in the question. It wasn't that he was incurious, he was voraciously curious about everything outside himself. The inward look just felt threatening in a way he couldn't quite articulate.

That conversation changed the direction of his development, because we finally had language for what was missing.

The six skills don't operate independently. They're connected in ways that aren't obvious until you see them fail together. The manager who avoids giving real-time feedback tends to have the same aversion to directness in their 1:1s, tends to set goals that are vague enough that nobody can be held accountable, and tends to let project drift go unaddressed because naming it would require a hard conversation. The underlying pattern runs through all of it, but if you only look at the feedback skill in isolation you'll try to fix the wrong thing.

I've watched this play out in dozens of different configurations. The manager who hires brilliantly but can't develop the people they've hired. The manager who sets razor-sharp goals but has 1:1s so shallow that nobody on their team would ever bring them a real problem. The manager who is genuinely gifted at project management but has never examined why they need to be in control of every decision, which is itself a pattern with roots they haven't dug up yet.

Each of these is a version of the same problem: one skill is masking a deficit in another, and because nobody has the full picture, the pattern persists.

The coaching-yourself skill is the hardest to build because it requires a specific kind of honesty that doesn't come naturally to people who've succeeded by being competent and confident. Competence and confidence are assets, but they can also function as a defense against self-examination, because if you're obviously good at things, why would you dig around for what you're bad at?

I'll tell you why: because the ceiling on your growth as a manager is almost always set by the thing you can't see in yourself, not by the things you haven't learned yet. You can learn frameworks and techniques and approaches; there's no shortage of those. What you can't learn from a book is the specific way you go distant when a conversation gets emotionally complex, or the specific way you avoid naming a performance problem until it's become a personnel situation, or the specific way you over-explain your decisions in a way that signals to your team that you don't actually trust them.

Those patterns are yours. They have histories. And until you look at them directly, they run the show from backstage.

The six skills are a starting point, not a destination. Rate yourself honestly on each one, sit with the ratings for a day or two, and pay attention to which number you keep wanting to revise upward. That's usually the one to work on first.
