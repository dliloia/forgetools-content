## The Keystone

The summer I turned thirteen, I started working. Not because someone told me it would build character, but because the money mattered, and because I figured out early that if you wanted something, you went and got it. By the time I was managing other people, I'd been working long enough to have a strong prior about what competence looked like, and a deep skepticism about the difference between what managers said they did and what they actually did.

One thing I noticed early, and kept noticing across every industry and every level of organization I moved through, was that the managers who were genuinely good at their jobs all had something in common that had nothing to do with their technical skills, their strategic instincts, or the systems they used. They were good at sitting across from another person and actually seeing them.

It sounds like a soft observation. It isn't.

The 1:1 meeting is, on its surface, one of the least dramatic parts of a manager's week. Thirty minutes, a standing agenda, a predictable rhythm. Nothing about it announces itself as the place where management actually happens. But pull back far enough and you start to see that it's the keystone, not just one structural element among many but the one that holds everything else in position.

I've thought a lot about why that is, and I keep coming back to the same answer: the 1:1 is the only recurring context in which a manager and a direct report are talking to each other rather than around each other. Every other management surface, goal-setting, performance reviews, project oversight, hiring, is downstream of what the relationship makes possible. And the relationship is built, or not built, in those thirty minutes every week.

Here's what I mean concretely. I once worked with a manager, technically excellent at her job, the kind of person her peers respected and her leadership trusted, whose 1:1s were essentially project check-ins. She'd ask what was done, what was in progress, what was blocked. The conversation would last twenty-five minutes, feel productive, and accomplish almost nothing that mattered. When I asked her how she'd describe each person on her team, she could tell me what they were working on. She couldn't tell me what they were working toward, what frustrated them, what they were afraid of, or what they were capable of that they hadn't been asked to do yet.

She didn't think this was a problem because her team was performing adequately. Projects shipped. Reviews were fine. Nobody quit.

Then she had to hire someone. And I watched her interview after interview try to evaluate candidates using the same vocabulary she used with her own people: task completion, scope management, stakeholder communication. She was listening for output signals because output was all she'd ever learned to listen for. She missed a candidate who would have been exceptional because that person's strength was in exactly the dimensions she'd never been curious about, the kind of thinking that doesn't show up in a project status update.

She also set goals that year that her people forgot by March, because the goals were written to fit the role rather than to fit the person. She'd never had a conversation about what each of them actually found meaningful, so she defaulted to the job description, which is a document about the company's needs, not the person's.

And when performance review season came, two of her three reviews included feedback that surprised the recipient. Not devastating feedback, not unfair feedback, technically accurate feedback, but feedback that landed like news instead of landing like a summary of things already discussed. That's a failure of relationship, not a failure of observation.

What I kept thinking, watching all of this, was that none of her downstream problems were downstream problems. They were all the same problem, presenting at different moments in the management calendar. The 1:1 had trained everyone involved, including her, to operate at the surface. So when depth was required, nobody had practiced getting there.

The fix isn't a better goal-setting process, or a more structured hiring rubric, or a different performance review template, though all of those can help. The fix is learning to have a real conversation with another person about what's actually going on for them, what they're building toward, what's getting in their way, and whether they trust you enough to tell you the truth. That skill doesn't live in a framework. It lives in practice, repeated weekly, over time.

I've seen managers completely transform their teams without changing anything except how they ran their 1:1s. Not because the meeting itself is magic, but because the meeting is the place where the relationship either deepens or stalls, and the relationship is the infrastructure that everything else runs on.

When a manager's 1:1s are genuinely good, something interesting happens to their other skills. Their hiring instincts sharpen because they've been practicing reading people at depth, not just at the surface level of words and polish. Their goal-setting becomes more motivating because they actually know what each person on their team cares about and what would make the work feel worth doing. Their project oversight becomes more accurate because their people bring them early signals instead of waiting until the situation is too far gone to hide. Their performance reviews stop feeling like surprises because they've been having the review, incrementally, all year long, in a relationship where that level of directness is normal.

The arch doesn't get stronger by reinforcing each stone individually. It gets stronger when the keystone is right, because then every other stone knows where it belongs.

Fix that first. Everything else becomes easier to fix from there.
