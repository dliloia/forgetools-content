## The Performance Tax

There's a version of work that looks like work but isn't.

You've seen it. The person who stays late not because they have more to do, but because leaving early feels risky. The meeting that exists so people can be seen in a room together. The email sent at 9pm not because 9pm was the right time to send it, but because the timestamp signals something.

I've been working since I was 13 years old. I put myself through college. I've spent three decades watching how organizations actually function, not how they say they function. And one of the most reliable signs of a struggling culture is the presence of what I call the performance tax: the hours and energy people spend performing effort rather than producing output.

Every organization collects this tax. The question is how much.

A friend of mine described his situation to me not long ago. He's salaried. He starts early, works a full day, produces. By any reasonable standard, he's done. But his manager asks him to stay later, because his manager's boss arrives later in the day and the manager wants people visible when senior leadership is around.

There's no more work. The ask has nothing to do with output. It's pure optics management.

My friend is smart enough to recognize what this means. It means his actual productivity is not the primary variable being measured. It means he can work harder and smarter than everyone around him and still be perceived as underperforming if he doesn't play the visibility game. It means the system is not a meritocracy, regardless of what the company values document says.

That realization is corrosive. Once a person understands that the game is about appearances, they face a choice: play the game or don't. Many smart people choose to play it. They learn to look busy. They optimize for visibility rather than value. They stop asking whether their work matters and start asking whether their presence is noticed. The organization asked them to change what they're optimizing for, and they did.

This is how performance cultures die.

I've watched it happen in companies of every size. It almost always starts with well-intentioned leaders who just want to feel like their teams are engaged and working hard. But feeling like people are working hard is not the same as people actually working hard. When you confuse the two, you start rewarding the signal instead of the substance, and people figure that out quickly.

Here's the other side of this, and it matters just as much.

A few years ago, I was in a role where I had the chance to build relationships with several leaders above me in the organization. Not my direct manager. People above that level. I made a deliberate choice to invest in those relationships, not as a career move, not for visibility, but because I genuinely believed that knowing each other, really knowing each other, would make the work better for everyone.

I set up weekly one-on-ones with four of them.

Three of those relationships became genuinely important to me. Not important in the transactional way where you keep someone's contact information because they might be useful someday. Important in the way where you understand how someone thinks, where you've disagreed and worked through it, where you've seen each other under pressure and come out with more respect than you started with. One of those relationships started rocky. We disagreed philosophically on some things, enough that the early conversations had a tension to them. But we kept showing up. Week after week. And somewhere in that consistency, we started to actually understand each other. The disagreement didn't disappear. We just understood it well enough that it stopped being a wall and started being something more like a foundation.

I'm still in touch with two of those leaders today. Neither of us works at that organization anymore. That's what a real relationship looks like.

The fourth leader canceled almost every week. Not maliciously. Not with any bad intent that I could see. Just consistently not prioritizing the time. We met maybe six times in a year when we should have met fifty. And there was no relationship to speak of at the end of it. Not because anything went wrong. Because a relationship that never receives consistent investment never actually forms.

That contrast stayed with me.

The three strong relationships were not built by showing up to look good. They were built by showing up to actually connect, even when it was uncomfortable, even when we disagreed, even when I had nothing particularly strategic to gain from that specific conversation. The consistency was the investment. The investment built the real thing.

The canceled meetings didn't just fail to build the relationship. They actively told me something. That the time wasn't valued. That the connection wasn't a priority. You can intend a relationship into existence. You cannot wish one there. You have to actually show up.

The same principle applies to the performance tax. When you ask people to perform rather than produce, you're sending a message about what you actually value. People hear that message clearly, even when it's never stated out loud. And they respond to what they hear, not what the organizational values document says.

Work should be a meritocracy. Not a perfect one, not a frictionless one, but directionally one. The people who do the hardest, smartest work should advance. The people who invest genuinely in their relationships should build strong networks. The people who produce real output should be recognized for it.

Every time you reward presence over performance, you take a small piece of that meritocracy and trade it for theater. And theater, once it takes root in a culture, is remarkably hard to pull out.
