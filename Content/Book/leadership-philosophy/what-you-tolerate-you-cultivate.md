## What You Tolerate, You Cultivate

Early in my management career, I had a team member who was chronically late to every standing meeting. Not by a lot, usually five or ten minutes, but consistently, and always with a reason. Traffic. A call that ran long. A thing that came up. The reasons varied; the lateness didn't.

I didn't say anything for the first few weeks. Then I didn't say anything for the first few months. Partly because the person was otherwise solid. Partly because the meetings always started eventually and we got through them. And partly, if I'm honest, because I told myself it wasn't worth the awkwardness.

What I didn't notice was that by month four, two other people on the team had started showing up late. Not as consistently, but noticeably. And the energy at the start of meetings had shifted. The people who showed up on time had quietly concluded that punctuality wasn't actually something I cared about. I'd never said that. I'd just taught it.

The phrase that helps me think about this clearly is straightforward: what you tolerate, you cultivate. Whatever behavior you let slide, whatever standard you decline to enforce, whatever pattern you watch without intervening, you are quietly instructing your team about what acceptable looks like here. You're not announcing a standard by ignoring one. You're eroding it.

The particular cruelty of this mechanism is how invisible it is in the moment. The first exception always feels justifiable. Maybe it is. Good managers exercise judgment, and not every standard violation warrants an immediate conversation. The problem isn't the exception. The problem is that the exception becomes the expectation, and it does so silently, over weeks and months, without any visible announcement.

By the time the pattern is obvious enough to address, you aren't correcting a behavior. You're trying to reverse a shared understanding that has been in place long enough to feel permanent. The team has organized itself around the new baseline. The person exhibiting the behavior has reasonably concluded that the behavior is acceptable. And the manager, who let it get here, now has to have a much harder conversation than the one available six months earlier.

I've thought a lot about why managers avoid the early conversation. Some of it is discomfort with conflict, which is understandable even if it's costly. But a lot of it is a miscalculation about cost. The early conversation has a visible and immediate cost: awkwardness, a possible defensive reaction, the time and energy it takes to do it well. The cost of not having it is invisible and delayed, spread across months of subtle cultural drift that won't surface on any report or metric until it's severe. Humans are not good at weighing immediate costs against diffuse future ones. We get the math wrong in the same direction every time.

The way to get better at it is to change how you see the choice in the moment. You're not deciding whether to have an awkward conversation. You're deciding what standard your team is going to operate under from this point forward. That reframe doesn't make the conversation comfortable, but it does make the decision clearer.

There's a distinction worth drawing carefully here, because I've watched it collapse in both directions. Tolerance is not the same thing as patience. Patience is a deliberate developmental decision. It means you've identified a gap, you've talked to the person directly about it, there's a shared understanding of what progress looks like, and you're giving the person time and support to close the gap. There's accountability built into it even when the timeline is long.

Tolerance looks like patience from the outside. It can feel like patience from the inside. But the person on the other end is missing the most important component: a direct conversation that tells them there's a gap to close. Without that conversation, they don't experience your tolerance as patience. They experience it as confirmation that things are fine. And they'll act accordingly.

The way to tell which one you're doing is whether you've had the direct conversation. If you have, and there's a genuine agreement in place, you're practicing patience. If you haven't, you're tolerating, regardless of how it feels.

The mechanism runs in the other direction just as reliably, and that part is worth sitting with too. When you notice someone doing something right and you say so, specifically and promptly, you're not just expressing appreciation. You're teaching the rest of the team what gets recognized here. When you back someone who challenged a bad idea in a meeting, you're teaching people that dissent is safe. When you hold a standard firmly in a moment where letting it slide would have been easier, you're teaching people that the standard is real.

Culture isn't a separate initiative. It's the accumulation of every tolerance decision and every reinforcement decision you've made, stacked on top of each other over months and years. You're building it constantly whether you're thinking about it or not. The question is only whether you're building it deliberately.

Your team doesn't learn your standards from your statements of values or your management philosophy or anything you say in an all-hands. They learn your standards from what happens the next time someone doesn't meet them. If you act, the standard is real. If you don't, the standard is decorative, and everyone knows it even if nobody says it out loud.

Look at the gap between the standards you believe you hold and the standards you actually enforce. Whatever's living in that gap is what you've been cultivating. The first step toward changing it is just seeing it clearly, which is harder than it sounds, because the things we've been tolerating long enough tend to stop looking like tolerances at all. They just look like how things are.
