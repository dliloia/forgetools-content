## Blue Collar Leadership

My dad never gave me a speech about leadership.

He didn't need to. I watched him for eighteen years, and everything I needed to know was there if I paid attention.

He went to work every day at a job that required his body, not just his brain. He came home tired in a way that was honest. He treated the people around him with a kind of direct, no-nonsense respect that I didn't fully understand until much later. Not the warm, performative respect you see in corporate sensitivity trainings. The real kind. The kind that says: I see your work. I see what you're carrying. I'm not going to pretend it's easy.

Growing up in New Jersey in that environment, you absorb things without realizing it. What hard work actually looks like. How trust gets built between people who depend on each other. What it means to show up when you don't want to.

I didn't know I was being shaped into a specific kind of manager. I just thought I was growing up.

It wasn't until I stepped into corporate environments, first as an individual contributor and then as a manager, that I realized not everyone had been taught these things. Some people had been taught something else entirely. Something about authority coming from position. About delegating everything below your level. About protecting your time from tasks that weren't strategic enough.

I watched those managers. I studied them. And I noticed something consistent about the teams they built.

Those teams worked when they were being observed. When the manager wasn't in the room, the energy dropped. People did what was required and nothing more. There was compliance, but no commitment.

Then I watched a different kind of manager. The ones who had worked their way up. Who remembered what the hard jobs felt like. Who didn't flinch when something unglamorous needed to be done.

Those teams were different in a way that was almost visceral. People stayed late because they wanted to. They solved problems nobody assigned them. They told their manager the truth when things were going sideways, instead of hiding it until it was too late.

That difference came from trust. And that trust came from something specific.

It came from watching their manager do the work.

I made Eagle Scout when I was sixteen. My service project required me to lead a group of teenagers through a real physical construction project on a tight schedule with no budget, no formal authority, and no way to compel anyone to do anything they didn't want to do.

If you want a fast education in earned authority, try that.

What I learned is that you buy the right to lead by being the hardest worker in the group. Not by giving instructions. By picking up the shovel first. By being there when everyone else has found a reason to leave. By caring about the outcome more than your own comfort or reputation.

That principle traveled with me into every management role I've ever held. It doesn't stop being true when you get a better title. If anything, the title makes it more important. Because the higher up you go, the more your team is watching to see whether you still believe the work matters.

I remember a specific moment, years into my career, when I had a team that was underwater. A product launch was coming and the backlog was brutal. One of my engineers was staying late every night, grinding through it alone.

I had a choice. I could manage it. Check in, ask for updates, provide encouragement from a distance.

Instead I pulled up a chair next to him and said, "What can I take off your plate right now?"

He actually paused before answering. Like the question didn't quite compute.

I took the documentation. The tracking spreadsheet updates. The release notes that were necessary and tedious and nobody's idea of meaningful work. I stayed late with him for three nights running.

At the end of that sprint, something had changed in the team. Not just with that engineer. All of them. They had seen something and they remembered it.

Not once, in the years that followed, did I have to work hard to get that team to do something difficult. They had already decided who I was.

This is the thing about blue collar leadership that credential-based management never fully grasps. Loyalty is not given in exchange for a paycheck. Loyalty is given in exchange for proof. Proof that you understand the weight of the work. That you're not above it. That when things get hard, you're going to be there doing it alongside them, not sending emails about it from a conference room.

I've hired and evaluated more than a thousand people in my career. I've built teams from scratch and inherited teams that were broken. And I've noticed that the single most reliable predictor of whether a manager will build real loyalty is not their intelligence, not their strategic vision, not their communication skills.

It's whether they still do the work.

Not all of it. Not forever. But enough. Enough that the team never has to wonder whether their manager respects what they do every day.

There's a version of leadership that looks very impressive from a distance. The crisp presentation. The confident vision. The polished vocabulary. I've seen it a hundred times. It's not nothing. But it's not enough.

The teams that sustain excellence through difficulty, through setbacks, through the moments when everything is harder than it was supposed to be, those teams are almost always led by someone who came up the hard way and never forgot it.

Not because struggle is inherently virtuous. But because working your way up teaches you something that no classroom can: that effort is what connects you to other people who are making an effort.

When your team sees that you understand that, when they see it not in your words but in your behavior, in what you're willing to do when nobody is making you do it, something shifts.

They stop working for you.

They start working with you.

That's the difference. And once you've led a team that works with you instead of for you, you'll never want to go back to any other kind.
