## The Math Nobody Wants to Hear

I started working at thirteen. By the time I was in my late twenties, I had worn my work ethic like armor. Long hours, low PTO balance, always available. I thought that was the deal. Work hard, sacrifice comfort, deliver results.

It took me an embarrassingly long time to understand that I was operating on a false equation.

The assumption was simple: more days at work equals more contribution. Skip the vacation, bank the output. It feels true. In the short term, it is true. But it's the kind of truth that quietly destroys you over a longer horizon, and I've watched it happen to some of the best people I've ever worked with.

Here's the framework that finally made it click for me.

Think of yourself at peak condition as a single unit. Call it 1.0. Full capacity, full engagement, best decisions, real patience, sharp instincts. That's the version of you that your team deserves and that your work requires.

Now run for six months without genuine rest. No vacation, no real disconnection. Just sustained grind.

Where are you?

Most people, when I ask them this, want to say 0.95. They'll negotiate down to 0.9 if pushed. The honest answer for most people I've known is somewhere between 0.75 and 0.85. And the brutal thing about performance degradation is that it doesn't fall in a straight line. It accelerates. The drop from 1.0 to 0.9 takes months. The drop from 0.9 to 0.75 takes weeks. At some point the floor comes up faster than you can see it.

Let's stay conservative. Say you're at 0.9. You've lost ten percent of your output.

In a twenty-day work month, that's two full days of productivity that simply vanish. Not two days you took off deliberately. Two days that evaporated into slower thinking, lower-quality decisions, less presence with your team. Over six months, that's twelve lost days. Real, countable, gone.

Now compare that to a week of vacation. Five working days off. But you come back at 1.0. The net loss over six months? Five days instead of twelve. You recovered seven days of output by taking the time.

I know how that sounds to someone who's built their identity around outworking the room. I was that person. The math felt like a trick, like someone moving the goalposts. But I've lived both sides of it, and I've managed enough people to have watched it play out dozens of times.

The insidious part is that the degradation is invisible while it's happening. Your 0.85 becomes your baseline. You stop comparing yourself to your actual capacity and start measuring against your recent performance. You feel fine because you've recalibrated fine downward. You don't know what 1.0 feels like anymore because it's been so long since you were there.

I've sat across from thousands of people in interviews over the course of my career. Some of the most memorable were high performers who were running on fumes. They were capable, prepared, clearly accomplished. But there was a ceiling in the conversation. A flatness. The version of them I was talking to was not the version that had built the resume I was holding. That person wasn't in the room. What was in the room was someone who had ground the edge off themselves and didn't know it.

The return from real rest is concrete. I don't mean that in a soft, motivational-poster way. I mean the quality of your decisions is measurably better. Problems you've been staring at for weeks resolve themselves in an afternoon because you're seeing them from outside the fog. Your patience with people is genuine rather than performed. Your ideas are actually new rather than variations on the same loops you've been running.

I've come back from weeks off and done more useful work in the first three days than I did in the three weeks before I left. Not because I worked harder. Because I was actually operating at capacity.

There's also a team dimension that most people miss entirely. If you're a leader, you don't just pay the cost of your own degraded performance. You pay the cost of what you model. Your team watches you. They read the signals you send about what commitment looks like at this company. When you're always there, when your PTO balance is a point of quiet pride, when you answer messages at ten at night during what's supposed to be your time off, they learn that this is what dedication looks like here. They act accordingly.

Now you're not running at 0.85. Your whole team is running at 0.85. Multiply that across everyone who reports to you and you've created an organizational performance deficit that no amount of face time fills.

I've managed hundreds of people across more than three decades. The ones who lasted, who kept growing, who were sharp five years in rather than ground down, were the ones who understood that rest was part of the job. Not a reward for completing the job. Part of it.

Rest isn't recovery from work. It's a component of output. The leaders who treat it as optional are making a resource allocation error. They are the resource. And they're running it into the ground while calling it commitment.

The week off doesn't cost the company. Running at 85% for six months costs the company. The math doesn't care about your work ethic. It just runs.
