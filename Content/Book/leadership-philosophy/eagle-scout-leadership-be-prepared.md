## The Merit Badge Nobody Teaches in Business School

I was sixteen when I earned my Eagle Scout rank. It was 1989 in northern New Jersey, and I had no idea it would become the most useful management training I ever received.

That sounds like a punch line. It isn't.

At sixteen, you're mostly focused on knots and campfire cooking and not embarrassing yourself in front of your troop. You absorb the principles without really understanding them. Be prepared. Serve because it matters. Leave things better than you found them. You recite them, practice them, and then you go off to live your life.

Twenty years later, I'm sitting across from a manager who just lost her second high performer in three months. She's shaken. She doesn't understand what happened. As she talks, I start to hear the pattern. No regular 1:1s. No documented development conversations. No real sense of where those employees were emotionally or professionally. She'd been reactive from day one, managing the present moment with no attention to the future. The departures blindsided her because she had no system for seeing them coming.

She was unprepared. Not through malice. Through habit.

That's when the Scout principles started clicking into place for me as a framework, not just as childhood memories.

**What "be prepared" actually means**

The Boy Scouts motto is one of the most famous phrases in American culture. Most people treat it as folksy common sense. Bring an umbrella. Pack extra food. Be a responsible person.

But as I started to understand management at a deeper level, I realized the principle is far more demanding than that.

Being prepared means you have already imagined the failures. You have thought through the scenarios where things go wrong and you have done something about it before you needed to. It's not optimism. It's not pessimism. It's a disciplined practice of looking ahead.

The manager with consistent 1:1s isn't just being organized. She is running a continuous early warning system. When someone starts to disengage, she knows it in week three, not month six. She catches it while it can still be addressed. She's prepared for the conversation about flight risk because she has been having the right conversations all along.

The manager who documents isn't paranoid. He knows that memory degrades, that the detail from a conversation in February matters enormously when you're writing a performance review in November, and that human beings are notoriously unreliable narrators of their own past decisions. He keeps notes because preparation requires information, and information requires the discipline to capture it.

I started keeping a document on every person I managed early in my career. It wasn't sophisticated. A shared folder, a simple template. Current priorities. Development goals. Open feedback loops. Wins worth remembering. Concerns worth watching. I would spend ten minutes after each 1:1 updating it. That habit, compounded over months, gave me a level of clarity about my people that most managers never achieve. I was rarely surprised. And in management, being rarely surprised is a significant advantage.

**The harder question: why are you doing this?**

There's a version of leadership that's entirely about the leader.

You know it when you see it. The manager who takes credit and distributes blame. Who makes himself the center of every decision because control feels like competence. Who collects direct reports the way some people collect subordinates, as proof of status.

Scouting taught me to identify this behavior as the opposite of leadership. Service projects in Scouting aren't glamorous. You're building a trail that hikers you'll never meet will use for decades. You're repainting a community center that doesn't know your name. You show up, you do the work, and you leave. There's no applause. That's the point.

I think about that every time I have a hard conversation with someone on my team. The manager who avoids delivering honest feedback is, at some level, optimizing for his own comfort. He doesn't want the awkward moment. He doesn't want the defensiveness or the tears or the risk that the employee pushes back. So he says nothing, or he softens the message until it carries no information, and he calls it kindness.

It isn't kindness. It's a failure of service.

The most useful thing I ever did for a young employee was sit down with her and say, clearly and without hedging, that her communication style in cross-functional meetings was damaging her reputation with peers who controlled things she needed. I told her specifically what I'd observed, specifically how it was landing, and specifically what I thought she needed to change. She sat with it for a moment. Then she said: "Nobody has ever told me this before."

She went on to have one of the strongest careers of anyone I've managed. I don't think I was the reason. But I think that conversation was part of the foundation. Service means telling people what they need to hear.

**The standard that outlasts you**

The principle that sits with me most heavily now is the third one.

Every Eagle Scout project has a finish line. You complete the work, you write the report, you present it to the committee. But the real question isn't whether you finished. It's whether what you built holds up after you leave. Whether the trail drains properly in the rain. Whether the benches you built last through the winter.

Managers who operate by this principle think about their teams as things they are temporarily tending, not things they own. The work of developing people, building systems, documenting decisions, and creating a culture that doesn't depend on their personal presence is not extra credit. It is the job.

I have watched managers leave teams in rubble. No documentation. No succession thinking. No one prepared to carry things forward. They got their next opportunity and they moved on, and behind them a team spent months trying to reconstruct what was lost.

I have also watched managers leave teams that actually improved after their departure. Because they hired for capability and then got out of the way. Because they documented how decisions were made. Because they invested so consistently in the people around them that leadership was distributed, not concentrated.

That second kind of manager is rare. And they are never forgotten.

Being prepared. Serving without needing recognition. Leaving things better than you found them.

I have never found a more honest summary of what excellent management actually requires. Not in business books, not in MBA curricula, not in leadership seminars. Three principles I learned in the woods at fifteen have held up through every environment I've worked in.

They're simple enough to understand quickly. They take a career to master.
