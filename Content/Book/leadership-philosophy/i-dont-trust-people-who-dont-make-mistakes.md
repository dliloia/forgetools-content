## The Spotless Record

There's a specific kind of job candidate who makes experienced hiring managers nervous, and it's not the one with the failure on their resume. It's the one without any.

I've spent a long time sitting across from people in interview rooms, and after a while you start to notice the shape of an answer before the content lands. Someone who's been genuinely tested and has genuinely failed and has come out the other side with something real to say about it, that person has a particular quality when they talk. They're specific. They don't rush past the uncomfortable part. They hold the moment where things went wrong without trying to escape it.

The person with the spotless record often does the opposite. They talk around failure in a way that sounds like modesty but functions like avoidance. They'll tell you about a challenge, walk you carefully through their excellent response to it, and arrive at an outcome that was ultimately positive. When you press, you find out the challenge was never really theirs to solve, or the stakes were low enough that failure wasn't a real option, or the story has been edited over time until only the presentable parts remain.

I don't mean to be uncharitable about it. Most of us learn early on that admitting mistakes in professional settings is risky, and we adjust accordingly. The problem is that the adjustment makes it nearly impossible for the people evaluating us to see who we actually are.

When I'm trying to understand someone, one of the most useful things I can know is how they behave when they've gotten something wrong. Not the fact of the mistake itself, but what they do in the minutes and hours afterward. Do they go quiet while they figure out who else might share the blame? Do they move fast to fix it and slow down only to understand why it happened? Do they find a way to mention it to the people who need to know before those people find out on their own?

Those patterns don't change much across context. The person who hides a small mistake hides a large one. The person who owns a small mistake owns a large one. I've watched this play out enough times that I'd take it almost as a rule.

A few years ago I was working through a technical problem with someone on my team. Something had gone sideways in a shared system, and when we traced the change back, it pointed toward work this person had done a few days earlier. Their response was quiet and immediate: "I don't remember making that change, but I was the one working there, so it must have been me. What do we need to do to make sure this doesn't happen again?"

No drama, no maneuvering, no waiting to see if the trail might lead somewhere else. Just a clean acknowledgment and an immediate pivot to prevention.

I've thought about that response a lot since then, because it hit every note I actually care about. Ownership came first, before context or explanation. The instinct was to fix it and understand it, not to escape it. And the question at the end, what do we do to prevent this, is the question that actually matters, the one that turns a mistake into a data point instead of just a problem.

The three things I listen for in any post-failure conversation, whether I'm interviewing someone or managing them through something hard, are those same three elements in that same order. Ownership before deflection. Root cause before excuse. And some version of "what changes so this doesn't happen again."

The ratio matters as much as the content. If someone spends ninety percent of their answer explaining the circumstances and ten percent on what they learned, the ratio is telling me something. Real accountability doesn't require much setup. It usually starts with the simplest possible version of "I got this wrong" and builds from there.

The harder question is what organizations do to make this more or less likely. Because most people aren't hiding their mistakes out of bad character. They're hiding them because they've learned, correctly, that disclosure is penalized. They've watched other people come forward with problems and get treated as the problem themselves. They've internalized the signal and they're behaving rationally in response to it.

If that's the environment you're managing in, the fix isn't to tell people it's safe to be honest. The fix is to behave in a way that proves it, repeatedly, over time. You go first. When you make a mistake, you name it plainly, walk through what happened, and tell your people what you're doing differently. Not as a performance, not to seem relatable, but because it's true and because the people working for you need to see it to believe it.

Do that enough times and you'll start to get real information back. People will tell you about problems early, when they're still small and fixable. You'll stop being the last person in the room to find out when something has gone wrong. That's not a soft benefit. That's a structural advantage, and it compounds.

The people worth working with, worth hiring, worth promoting, are the ones who've been wrong before and came through it with more integrity than they had going in. The spotless record doesn't tell you who someone is. It tells you they've been careful about what you're allowed to see.

The honest mistake, handled well, is a better signal than a hundred clean quarters. I'd take it every time.
