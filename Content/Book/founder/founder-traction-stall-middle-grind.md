The trough has a specific feeling. It's not panic, exactly. It's more like the quiet after you've done a lot of hard work and looked up expecting to see progress and found the needle basically where you left it.

I know that feeling well. I've built things that worked and then watched the dashboard go still. The product was real. Users were using it. Feedback was positive. And the growth line was flat in a way that started to feel like a permanent condition rather than a temporary one.

What I noticed, sitting in that quiet, is that the temptation to build more is almost irresistible. Building is familiar. It has a feedback loop. You write the code, you ship the feature, you see the thing in the product. At the end of a day spent building, you have evidence that you did something. That evidence feels like progress whether or not it produced any actual movement.

Distribution doesn't have that. Cold outreach produces rejection or silence. Publishing posts produces indifference, at least for a while. Pitching yourself to podcast hosts or newsletter writers produces a lot of no-replies. None of it gives you the clean satisfaction of a shipped feature. So you don't do it, or you do it halfheartedly, and instead you go back to the code.

I did this for a while before I figured out what was actually happening. I was confusing motion with progress. Building is motion. It feels like working. But when the product already works and nobody knows it exists, more building doesn't close the gap. It just gives you a better product that still nobody knows about.

The realization that broke me out of it was embarrassingly simple: the metric that matters when you're in the trough isn't product quality. It's reach. If 200 people know your thing exists and 10% of them buy, you have 20 customers. If 2,000 people know it exists, same conversion rate, you have 200. The product didn't change. The denominator did. And the denominator is the only thing you can actually move when the product is already good enough.

That changes the question. Instead of "what should I build next," the question becomes "who hasn't heard of this yet, and how do I get in front of them today."

That switch is uncomfortable for people who built their identity around making things. I'm one of those people. I spent decades in operations and management, running teams, building systems. Writing publicly, pitching strangers, showing up in online forums to talk about what I'd made felt performative to me in a way that building did not. It felt like I was doing marketing rather than doing work, and I had absorbed somewhere along the way the idea that those were different categories.

They're not. Distribution is work. It's just work that doesn't produce a clean artifact at the end of the day. You write the post and you're not sure if it landed. You send the pitch and you wait. You post to a community and you watch the thread to see if anyone cares. The feedback loops are slower and messier and harder to interpret, and that's exactly why most technical founders avoid them.

The thing I've learned, slowly and through repetition, is that you have to be willing to be publicly bad at distribution before you can be good at it. There's no version of this where you get to watch from the sideline until you feel ready and then step in and perform well. You earn your distribution reps the same way you earned your product reps: by doing it, poorly at first, and getting better over time.

My first attempts at writing for an audience were stiff. I knew they were stiff. I'd spent years managing hundreds of people, conducting thousands of job interviews, running large teams through hard transitions, and I could not figure out how to translate any of that into something a stranger would stop scrolling to read. That gap between what you know and what you can communicate is real, and the only way across it is volume. You write enough that you start to hear your own voice. You post enough that you learn what resonates.

What you can't do is stay quiet until it feels right, because that feeling never comes on its own.

The hardest part of the trough isn't the flat metrics. It's the interpretation. When signups are flat and revenue isn't moving, the story your brain wants to tell is "the market is saying no." That story is seductive because it gives you a clean out. If the market is saying no, you can pivot, or quit, or tell yourself you were too early. But flat metrics during a period when you haven't distributed anything don't mean the market said no. They mean you didn't ask loudly enough.

Those are different situations with different solutions. The first one might warrant a pivot. The second one warrants a sprint.

A sprint means picking one channel, for real, and working it for seven days with distribution as the only job. Not dabbling in five channels. One. Prepare it properly, launch it, and then stay in the thread. Reply to every comment. Respond to every message. That's where the feedback hides, and it's also where the conversions come from, because people respond to founders who show up rather than drop a link and disappear.

The sprint won't make you famous. It'll probably produce a handful of signups and a few useful conversations. But those conversations will reframe something about how you talk about the product, and the signups will remind you that real humans want what you built. That matters more than the number. It keeps you going when the trough feels permanent.

Because the trough is not permanent. It just asks something of you that building didn't. Get out of the product and into the world. Switch instruments. That's the job.
