## How to Make a Hiring Decision You Won't Regret

There's a specific kind of regret that comes with a bad hire. It's different from other professional mistakes because it's slow. You don't usually know right away. The person seems fine for a few weeks, maybe longer. And then something happens, a hard deadline, a difficult team dynamic, a moment that requires them to do the exact thing you interviewed them on, and it becomes clear that what you saw in the interview was not what you got.

I've been on both sides of that moment more times than I'd like to count.

What I've learned, across hundreds of hiring decisions over a long career, is that the interview itself is rarely where things go wrong. The interview is a data collection exercise. What you do with that data afterward is where most managers make the mistake.

The debrief is where hiring decisions are actually made. And most debriefs are broken.

Here's what a broken debrief looks like. Five or six people gather after a full day of interviews. The hiring manager opens the floor. One person, usually the most senior or the most extroverted, starts talking about their experience with the candidate. They liked them. They tell a story. The room, because rooms are social systems and social systems default to consensus, begins to move in the direction of that story. Two or three other people agree. The person who saw something concerning either stays quiet or mentions it gently and gets steamrolled. A decision gets made that feels unanimous but isn't.

Six months later, you're watching that hire struggle. You remember the person who had the concern. You wonder why nobody listened.

The fix starts before the debrief even opens. Every interviewer should write down their assessment independently, before they hear a single word from anyone else. What did the candidate say? What did they demonstrate? What was missing? And then the hard question: hire or no hire, and why? Not a hedge. A verdict.

This is not about bureaucracy. It's about protecting the independent signal that each interviewer generated. Once you hear someone else's opinion, your own memory of the interview quietly starts to reshape itself toward theirs. It's not something people do on purpose, it's just how cognition works under social pressure. Writing it down before the conversation locks in the real observation.

The second thing that has to happen before any candidate walks in the door is a rubric. The most common hiring mistake I've seen is organizations that build their criteria around the candidate instead of the job. Someone impressive comes in, and suddenly the qualities they happen to possess become the qualities that matter most. Charisma becomes a must-have because the last candidate was charismatic. Or functional expertise becomes less important because the most recent candidate had raw intelligence and a good story about learning fast.

You have to know what you're measuring before you meet the person you're measuring. What does success in this role actually require? Not what sounds good in a job posting, not what the last person in the seat happened to be strong at. What would someone need to demonstrate in order for you to confidently believe they'll succeed? Write it down. Share it with the interview panel. Use it to design your questions and use it to evaluate the answers.

When you run a debrief against a rubric, the conversation changes. Instead of "I really liked them," you're asking "did they show us X or not?" That's a different and much more productive argument to have.

I learned something about split verdicts that took me years to appreciate. When the room is genuinely divided, most hiring managers try to resolve the split. Get everyone to agree. Land on a decision and move forward. The instinct makes sense, because a split feels like stalemate, and stalemate is uncomfortable.

But a split is almost always information. What did the people on each side actually observe? Were they evaluating the same thing? Did one interviewer spend 20 minutes on logistics and another spend 60 minutes on the core competency? Sometimes the split reveals that the candidate was genuinely inconsistent, strong in structured moments and weak in ambiguous ones, or technically sharp but collaboratively difficult. That's not necessarily disqualifying, but it's something you need to know about before you decide. And it's definitely something you need a plan for if you move forward.

The last thing I want to say about hiring decisions is about the reversibility of the choice, because I think this is the most underused framework in the conversation.

Before you decide, ask how bad it is if you're wrong. Some hires are low-stakes in the sense that if it doesn't work out, the recovery is real but manageable. You have a hard conversation, you part ways, you start over. That hurts and it costs time and money, but the organization absorbs it and moves forward.

Other hires are not like that. A senior leader who doesn't work out doesn't just affect their own output. They affect every person they manage, every cross-functional relationship they touch, every culture signal they send. Unwinding that mistake takes a year at minimum and some of what gets damaged doesn't fully repair.

The standard of evidence you need before saying yes should scale with that cost. For a role where the damage is deep and slow to reverse, "I'm pretty confident" is not a high enough bar. You want to be close to certain. You want the rubric to say yes, the debrief to say yes, and your own gut to say yes without any of the three overriding the others.

I've passed on candidates who were genuinely impressive because one of those three said no and I couldn't figure out why. And in most of those cases, I found out later what I'd missed. The person took a role somewhere else and struggled publicly, or a reference check turned up something the structured interview hadn't surfaced, or my own instinct, which I'd trained for years on good and bad outcomes, was picking up on something I couldn't articulate yet.

You will not get every hiring decision right. Nobody does. But you can build a process that improves your odds, makes your failures informative rather than just painful, and protects you from the specific kind of regret that comes from a decision you knew wasn't quite right but made anyway because the room was moving in one direction and it was easier to go with it.

The candidate who impressed everyone in the interview is not always the candidate who will do the job. That gap, between impression and execution, is what a rigorous decision process is designed to close.
