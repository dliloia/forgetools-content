I spent five years as an Amazon Bar Raiser. In that time, I conducted over 1,250 interviews across nearly every function in the company. Engineers, finance directors, HR leaders, operations managers, product executives. The full range of human work at a very large organization.

The Bar Raiser role has a specific design. You're independent from the hiring team. You have no stake in whether the position gets filled. You carry veto power over any hire, and your singular job is to evaluate whether this person raises the overall bar of talent at Amazon. Not whether they're good enough for the role. Whether they make the company better by being in it.

That independence gave me something unusual. I could look at candidates without wanting anything from them. No urgency. No empty seat creating pressure. Just clear observation of who someone actually was, under questioning, over the course of an hour.

After enough of those interviews, patterns emerged that I didn't expect.

The first thing that surprised me was that the separating factor between great managers and average ones was not intelligence. It was not credentials, not the prestige of the companies on their resume, not how articulate or polished they were in the room. I interviewed people with 20-year management careers who were, at their core, mediocre at developing people. And I interviewed people five or six years into management who had something the veterans didn't.

What they had was a specific kind of honesty about their own role in outcomes.

The best managers I interviewed could talk about failure the way a surgeon talks about a difficult case. Directly. Specifically. Without drama, but also without protection. They would say things like: "I made the wrong call on the launch timing. I weighted the sales team's urgency too heavily and discounted what the operations data was telling me. We paid for it. Here's what I'd do differently." That answer doesn't make someone sound weak. It makes them sound trustworthy. Because you know they've processed it. You know they've extracted the lesson. And you know they won't repeat the mistake the same way.

Average managers talked about their failures the way people describe traffic accidents. Things happened around them. The market shifted. The team wasn't aligned. Leadership changed course. All of those things might have been true. But a great manager can tell you their exact role in the outcome even when external forces were significant. They don't hide behind context.

There was a phrase I learned to monitor very carefully in management interviews: "my team."

It's a phrase that can mean two entirely different things. In one version, it reflects genuine investment and shared pride. A manager says "my team built a fulfillment process that cut error rates by 30%" and means it as context for what was accomplished together. That's fine. That's accurate.

But there's another version, and once you've heard it enough times you recognize it immediately. It's the candidate who uses "my team" as a substitution for personal accountability. Every strong result is framed as what the team accomplished. Every decision traces back to what the team decided. When you probe, "What did you specifically do in that situation?", the answer drifts toward abstractions. Vision setting. Path clearing. Creating alignment.

Those are real things. I don't want to suggest they aren't. But when a manager can only describe their work at that altitude, when they can't give you the specific conversation they had with a struggling direct report, the specific moment their judgment shifted a decision, the specific thing they personally built or changed, you're looking at someone who manages as a status rather than a practice.

The managers who stood out across 1,250 interviews had something else. They knew exactly what they did. Not just what happened on their team. What they specifically contributed. They could give you the actual number, the actual name, the actual Tuesday afternoon when they changed course and why. That specificity is not a performance skill. It's a byproduct of actually having done the work with intention.

I also paid close attention to how people handled what Amazon calls its Leadership Principles. By the time I was running Bar Raiser evaluations, I'd seen every preparation strategy candidates used. Some people came in with a prepared story for every principle. Some tried to work the same two stories into every question. Some tried to logic their way through answers in real time.

What I was never actually measuring was whether someone had the right story for the right principle. What I was measuring was whether the principle was in them or on them.

The difference shows up fast. When someone has genuinely internalized "Dive Deep" as a management behavior, they don't just tell you a story about diving deep. They dive deep during the interview itself. Their answers have layers. They remember specifics that weren't required. They can go past the prepared version of the story because it actually happened to them and they actually lived in the details.

When someone is performing the principle, the story hits the beats and then stops. The details run out exactly at the edge of the preparation. Press further, and the answers get general.

The principle I found most predictive of actual management quality was one that doesn't get discussed as much as the others: "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit." It asks two things simultaneously. First, you need the courage to hold a position under pressure, to push back on a decision you believe is wrong even when the room is against you. Second, you need the discipline to commit fully and execute wholeheartedly once a decision has been made, even if it's not the one you argued for.

That combination is genuinely rare. Most people can disagree. They voice their objection, maybe more than once, and feel like they've done their part. Fewer people can commit completely after losing the argument. Not grudging compliance. Not passive execution. Real commitment, with the same energy they would have brought if they'd won the debate.

The managers who could do both were almost always exceptional at developing people, because those same two qualities, honest advocacy and genuine follow-through, are exactly what high-performing teams need to see modeled.

I want to be clear that the people who stood out in those 1,250 interviews were not people with perfect records. They had made bad hires. They had missed targets. They had misread people and situations. Some of them had genuine failures they were still carrying.

What made them exceptional was that they had done the work of understanding what happened and why. They could tell me. And that capacity for honest self-knowledge, without self-punishment but also without self-protection, is exactly what you want in a manager when things get hard.

Because things always get hard. The question is whether your manager is going to see it clearly when they do.
