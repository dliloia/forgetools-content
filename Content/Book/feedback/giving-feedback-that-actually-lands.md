There's a manager I worked with early in my career who gave some of the most technically competent feedback I've ever heard. Clear language, calm delivery, specific examples. And almost no one on his team ever changed.

It took me a while to understand why. The feedback was right. The structure was right. But something was missing, and the team felt it even when they couldn't name it.

What was missing was any sense that he actually cared what happened next. He delivered the feedback and moved on, visibly relieved that he'd said the thing. The conversation ended, and the interest ended with it. His team eventually learned to receive his feedback the way you receive a form letter: noted, filed, forgotten.

That observation shaped how I thought about feedback for the rest of my career. The content matters. But feedback is a relational act before it's a technical one, and you can't separate the two.

The first thing most people get wrong is timing. There's a freshness window on feedback that most managers underestimate. When you sit down with someone to discuss something they did this morning, they can still feel it. They remember the room, the decision, the pressure they were under, the split-second judgment they made. They can actually examine the moment with you. When you reference something from four months ago, you're asking them to reconstruct a memory they've already categorized and moved past. You're also, whether you intend to or not, signaling that you've been keeping a file.

Feedback delivered in the moment feels like coaching. Feedback delivered six months later feels like prosecution.

The performance review is the worst place to deliver anything important. I don't say that to be provocative. The review should confirm what the person already knows. If they're hearing something critical for the first time in a formal review, you missed the window where that feedback could have actually changed something. What you're delivering now isn't development. It's documentation.

The second thing people get wrong is specificity, and this one cuts both ways. Vague feedback, "you need to be more strategic," "your communication could be stronger," gives the employee nothing to act on. It describes a destination without providing any road. But vague feedback also lets the manager off the hook from doing the hard work of locating the actual moment that triggered their concern.

Forcing yourself to be specific is a form of intellectual honesty. If you can't name the meeting, the conversation, the decision, then you have to ask yourself whether you're reacting to a real pattern or to a general impression shaped by something unrelated. Specificity protects the employee. It also keeps you accurate.

When I started using a simple framework, Situation, Behavior, Impact, the thing that changed wasn't the structure. It was that the structure forced me to find the real thing I was trying to say. Situation made me locate a moment. Behavior made me describe what actually happened rather than what I thought it meant. Impact made me articulate why it mattered beyond "it bothered me."

That last piece is underrated. When you connect behavior to impact, you're giving the employee a reason to care about changing. People are far more motivated to adjust when they understand the downstream consequence than when they understand that you noticed something. "You interrupted her twice, and after the second time she stopped contributing entirely, and we missed information we needed" lands differently than "you interrupted her twice." The behavior is the same. The gravity of it changes completely with the impact attached.

But here's the thing that underpins all of this: none of it works without a relationship that can hold the weight of an honest conversation.

I've watched structurally perfect feedback fall completely flat because the trust wasn't there. When someone doesn't believe you're on their side, their brain doesn't process the content of what you're saying. It processes the threat. They're managing their reaction, protecting themselves, figuring out what this means for their job, wondering whether this is the beginning of something serious. The actual feedback doesn't land because there's no room for it.

Trust gets built in the ordinary moments, not the feedback conversations. It gets built when you protect someone's time, when you advocate for them in rooms they're not in, when you tell them the truth about something small before you have to tell them the truth about something big. Consistent 1:1s, the kind where you actually talk about their work and their growth and not just the task list, build the kind of relationship where a hard conversation can happen without feeling like an ambush.

The last thing worth naming directly is the avoidance problem, because it's more common than most people admit. Feedback avoidance usually presents itself as consideration. "I don't want to upset them." "They seem stressed, I'll wait for a better time." "Maybe it'll correct itself." But underneath those reasons, almost always, is a simpler one: the manager doesn't want to manage the reaction.

That discomfort is understandable. But the math doesn't work in the employee's favor. Every week of silence on a real problem is a week they can't course-correct. By the time it becomes a formal performance issue, often months of avoidance have passed, and what could have been a two-minute conversation in a 1:1 is now a documented process with real stakes for their livelihood.

Giving feedback early, when it's still small, is an act of respect. It tells the person you see them clearly enough to notice, you care enough to say something, and you believe they're capable of doing better. Silence tells them none of those things. It just leaves them without the information they need to grow.
