## When You Have Something to Say to Someone Above You

For a long time early in my career, I stayed quiet when senior people did things that were clearly wrong. Not wrong in a dramatic, call-HR way. Wrong in the quieter sense, the kind that erodes a team gradually. A leader who gives public feedback in a way that shuts people down. A manager who makes decisions in a vacuum and then wonders why execution is rough. A director who keeps changing priorities and doesn't understand why the team looks exhausted.

I knew what was happening. I even knew, in some cases, that the person didn't fully see it themselves. But I said nothing.

I told myself I didn't have enough standing. I told myself it wasn't my place. I told myself I'd find a better moment, which of course never came.

What I was actually doing was prioritizing my own comfort over something that genuinely mattered. And I dressed that up as professionalism.

The first time I actually gave real feedback upward, I was terrified. The person was a VP at a company where I'd been working for about two years. Smart, well-intentioned, respected in the organization. And they had a pattern of asking for input from the team before big decisions, then making the decision they were already going to make and presenting it as if the input had shaped it. People could feel it. The team had quietly stopped participating in those conversations because the exercise felt hollow.

I asked for twenty minutes one afternoon. I sat down and I said, approximately, "I want to share something that I think might be useful. The team has noticed a pattern in how decisions are framed, and I think it's affecting how much they're willing to engage. I've talked to a few people and they feel like their input isn't actually changing anything. I don't think that's your intent, but the impact is that people are starting to opt out."

The first thing they said was defensive. Not aggressively, just reflexively. "I do take the input seriously." And I said, "I believe you. I think the problem is in how it's communicated after the fact." We talked for another ten minutes. They were quiet at the end.

Three weeks later, the framing of those conversations changed. They started explaining which parts of the decision were already fixed constraints and which parts were genuinely open. It wasn't perfect, but the team noticed. Engagement in those sessions came back.

I never found out if the VP connected the change to our conversation. It didn't really matter. The thing that mattered was that the problem got a little better, and I had been part of fixing it instead of just watching it.

What I learned from that experience, and the ones that came after, is that upward feedback fails or succeeds mostly before you open your mouth. It depends on whether you have the relationship to be heard, whether the feedback is specific enough to be actionable, and whether you've separated what you observed from what you've concluded about the person. Those three things are almost everything.

The relationship piece is the one people most want to skip, because building it takes time and there's no shortcut. But if you show up with critical information and there's no established trust, the information doesn't land as helpful, it lands as a threat. The same words carry completely different weight depending on who's saying them and what that person has demonstrated about their intentions.

The specificity piece is what separates feedback from complaint. "You're not listening to the team" is a complaint. It puts someone on the defensive immediately because it's a character statement and there's nothing to do with it. "In the last three planning sessions, when people raised concerns about timeline, the conversation moved on without those concerns getting addressed" is something a person can actually work with.

And the intent separation matters because it's true. Most of the time when a senior leader is doing something that's hurting the team, they don't know it. They're not trying to demoralize anyone. They have blind spots, just like everyone does. When you give feedback that makes clear you understand their intent is good while the impact is landing differently, you've made it safe for them to hear you without having to choose between accepting the feedback and defending their character.

The leaders worth working for want this information. They may not love it in the moment. But they understand that an organization where nobody tells them the truth is an organization making decisions on bad data, and eventually that catches up with everyone.

And the ones who don't want it? That tells you something important too.
