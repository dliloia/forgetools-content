## What Recognition Actually Requires

Early in my career I had a manager who, once a quarter, would tell me I was doing a good job. He was consistent about it. Every quarter, like clockwork, a few words of encouragement in my review. I used to leave those conversations wondering if he'd said the same thing to everyone, and whether it would have mattered if he had.

The answer to both questions, I eventually understood, was yes.

It wasn't that he was a bad manager in other ways. He kept things organized and he ran clean meetings and he handled conflict reasonably well. But he never seemed to actually see what any of us were doing at the level of the work itself. The praise was obligatory and we all knew it, and so it meant nothing, which made it, in some small way, worse than silence.

The thing about hollow recognition is that it trains people too. Just not in the direction you want.

When someone does something genuinely well and the response they get could've been given to anyone, they learn that there's no real connection between their specific actions and what their manager notices. They learn that recognition is ambient and undifferentiated, like background music. After a while they stop trying to figure out what the good work actually looks like to this person, because there's no signal coming back. They just keep showing up, doing roughly what they were doing, and collecting the quarterly "great job" like a participation trophy.

I've spent a lot of years on both sides of this. Receiving feedback, giving it, and watching managers of every level handle it well and handle it badly. The pattern that separates the good from the bad is not how generous they are with praise. It's how specific.

Specificity is not just a communication technique. It's evidence. When you tell someone exactly what they did and why it mattered, you're demonstrating that you were actually paying attention. That you saw the thing they did. That you understood why it was hard or why it was well-executed or why the timing of it mattered. That demonstration, more than the compliment itself, is what lands.

The version of feedback that stays with people long after they've left a job usually sounds something like this: "She pulled me aside after that presentation and told me that the way I handled the pushback from the CFO, staying calm and redirecting to the data instead of getting defensive, was exactly the right move. She said she'd been in rooms where people blew that moment and set the project back by months, and that I hadn't done that." That's the kind of thing people remember twenty years later. Not because it was lavish, but because it was real. Because it was tied to a specific moment and a specific choice and a specific consequence.

There's a practical structure underneath this that's worth understanding. When you give positive feedback, you need three things working together: the situation, the behavior, and the impact. What was happening, what did the person do, and what did that produce. All three matter. Most managers give the situation and skip straight to a vague judgment. "That meeting was rough and you handled it well." That's two thirds of the way there and it doesn't stick the landing because it doesn't tell the person what specifically to repeat.

The impact piece is the most commonly dropped, and it's actually the most important one. When you explain why the behavior mattered, you're not just offering recognition, you're teaching. You're showing someone the connection between a choice they made and the outcome it produced. That's the information that changes behavior over time, that's what makes good people better.

The second thing that matters, equally important and just as often ignored, is timing.

Feedback has a half-life. A short one. Something that happened on a Wednesday morning that you surface in a one-on-one the following Thursday afternoon has lost most of its power, not all of it, but most. The emotional context is gone. The person has to work to reconstruct what you're even referring to. The recognition no longer feels immediate because it isn't, and immediacy is part of what makes recognition real.

Real noticing is immediate. If something genuinely struck you as excellent in the moment, you felt that at the time. When you surface it days later in a scheduled meeting, you're reporting on a past observation, which is different from actually recognizing someone. The delay communicates, unintentionally, that this was not urgent enough to mention when it happened.

The habit worth building is to say something close to when you see it. Not a speech, not an agenda item. A conversation in a doorway, a quick message, thirty seconds after the meeting ends. The informality is not a lack of care. It's evidence of the opposite, that you noticed in real time and it was worth saying immediately.

There's a version of this that managers resist because they're afraid of playing favorites, or missing someone, or seeming like they're overcalibrated on one person. That fear leads them to keep everything general and evenly distributed, which means it's meaningless for everyone equally. The solution to that problem is not less specific feedback distributed wider. It's being genuinely attentive to each person on the team and finding the specific things worth saying to each of them regularly.

That requires actually watching people work. Knowing what's hard for them and where they're growing. Catching the moment someone does something better than they did it three months ago. That kind of attention is not additional labor on top of management. It is management. It's the core of what the job actually is.

The managers who do this well don't make it feel like an event. They make it feel like how they see things. It comes through in the ordinary course of the day, in real time, tied to real things. And because of that, when they do have harder things to say, people trust that too. The positive feedback has established that this manager is paying attention and telling the truth. That's the foundation everything else stands on.
