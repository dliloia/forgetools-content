There's a version of managing feedback that feels responsible. You observe something during the week, you make a mental note, and you save it for the one-on-one. You come prepared. You've had time to think it through. You're not firing from the hip.

I ran my teams this way for longer than I should have. It felt like discipline. In practice, it was mostly avoidance dressed up as organization.

The thing I didn't understand early in my career was that feedback has a shelf life. What you observe on Monday and deliver on Friday is not the same feedback. Something changes in the gap, and the change is almost never in your favor.

Let me explain what I mean.

When something happens in a meeting, on a call, in a hallway conversation, that moment carries information that neither you nor the other person can fully reconstruct later. The specific words someone used. The way the room responded. The look on a customer's face. All of that is context, and context is what makes feedback land instead of float.

By Friday, that context is mostly gone. You're working from a summary you've assembled in your head. The person receiving the feedback is trying to reconstruct a scene they've mentally filed away and moved past. You're both working harder than you need to for a result that's going to be fuzzier than it should be.

I had a manager early in my career who was brilliant at reading people but terrible at telling them what he saw in real time. He'd sit in on a call with you, watch the whole thing, and then bring it up ten days later in a one-on-one, by which point you'd run another twelve calls and couldn't even remember the specific one he was referencing. The feedback was often accurate. The timing made it useless. We stopped being able to trust that silence meant approval, because we knew feedback might be coming at any point, days after the fact, about something we'd already moved past.

That's the other cost of holding feedback too long. The silence between the moment and the meeting is not neutral. People fill it with their own interpretation. If they don't hear anything after a tough client call, they either assume it was fine or they carry quiet anxiety about whether something is coming. Neither is good.

When I started managing larger teams and the feedback volume got real, I had to figure out a different approach. What I landed on was pretty simple. Small, specific, observable things get said quickly, within minutes or hours of the moment if possible. Patterns, trends, developmental conversations, those go in the one-on-one where there's actual time and space.

The distinction matters. If someone interrupted a colleague twice in a project review, that's a small specific thing. Say it. Thirty seconds after the meeting ends, privately if you can, briefly regardless. "I noticed you talked over Marcus a couple times in there. Worth watching." That's it. You're not building a case. You're giving them information while the picture is still clear.

But if that same person has been interrupting people in every meeting for six weeks, that's a pattern. A pattern needs a real conversation, not a quick hallway comment. You need to understand what's driving it, whether they're aware of it, what it's costing them with the team. That lives in the one-on-one.

The problem is that most managers I've seen conflate these two things. They send every piece of feedback through the one-on-one queue because it feels more official, more considered. What they end up with is one-on-ones packed with small tactical feedback that should have been handled days ago, and not enough space for the actual developmental conversations that require depth.

I remember one manager on my team, a really sharp person, who came to me frustrated that her one-on-ones felt transactional. She'd prepare, her reports would prepare, and they'd spend the whole hour going through a list of things that happened during the week. Nobody was getting better. Nobody felt like the conversations were moving them forward.

When I asked her to walk me through her feedback process, it took about two minutes to find the issue. Everything went into the one-on-one. Every observation, every correction, every "hey that call didn't go well." The queue was so full of small stuff that there was no room for the conversations that actually build people.

We made one change: she started saying the small stuff in the moment. Not harshly, not with a lot of ceremony, just briefly and factually when it happened. Within a month, her one-on-ones had completely different energy. The tactical backlog cleared out and she had space to talk about what her people were working toward, what they were struggling with, where they wanted to go.

The trust piece of this is something I feel strongly about, because I've seen it play out enough times to believe it's real. Managers who give feedback in the moment are trusted more. Not because people enjoy being corrected, but because they always know where they stand. There's no guessing what's being held back. There's no wondering whether the silence after a rough call means everything is fine or whether something is coming.

Candor in real time is a gift, even when the content isn't comfortable. It tells the other person that you're paying attention, that you respect them enough to say the thing directly, and that you're not managing an impression of them over time in private while staying quiet to their face.

That's what real feedback culture looks like. Not scheduled, not stockpiled, not softened by a week of processing. Just honest, timely, and specific enough to be useful.
