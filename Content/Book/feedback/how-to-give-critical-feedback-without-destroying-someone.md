## The Feedback Nobody Wants to Give

There's a particular kind of conversation that managers carry around in their heads for weeks before they have it. They rehearse it in the car. They think about it in the shower. They start to bring it up and then the moment passes and they file it away for next time. By the time they finally sit down with the person, they've worked themselves into a state of anxiety that almost guarantees the conversation will go sideways.

I spent years watching managers do this, and then I spent years catching myself doing it. The avoidance doesn't come from weakness. It comes from caring about the person on the other side, which is actually a good instinct running in the wrong direction. You care enough to worry about hurting them, but the worry keeps you from helping them.

The irony is that the thing you're trying to avoid, causing harm, happens anyway. It just happens slower and without explanation.

I managed a guy once who was genuinely talented. Smart, motivated, great instincts on the work itself. And he had a pattern in meetings where he would talk over people, not aggressively, but constantly. He'd get excited about an idea and just go, and whoever had been speaking would stop and wait, and eventually stop trying. It happened so regularly that two of his peers had already started leaving him off the informal conversations where real decisions got made. He didn't know that. He had no idea.

I sat on it longer than I should have. I kept thinking the right moment would show up. What I was really doing was protecting myself from discomfort while he kept operating blind.

When I finally had the conversation, I started with what I'd seen specifically. Not a summary and not a characterization of his personality. An actual moment from an actual meeting. He went quiet for a second, and then he said something I've thought about a lot since then: "I didn't realize I was doing that."

Most people don't. That's the whole problem with feedback we withhold. We assume the other person sees what we see, that they're choosing the behavior with full awareness. They usually aren't. They're running on habits they formed years ago, operating in ways that feel natural to them, and they need someone who pays attention and genuinely cares about their development to hand them the mirror.

That's the job.

The mechanics of a good feedback conversation are not complicated, but they require you to hold a clear intention going in. The intention isn't to unburden yourself of frustration. It isn't to make the person feel the weight of what they did. The intention is to create understanding that moves something forward. Everything else follows from that.

You start with a specific behavior, not a trait. Telling someone they're "not a good communicator" gives them nowhere to go because they don't experience themselves as a bad communicator. They experience themselves as someone trying to get their point across. But telling them that in Tuesday's meeting they cut off the same person three times before she stopped speaking gives them something concrete to hold. That's a behavior. Behaviors can be examined. They can be changed.

Then you ask before you explain. This was the hardest thing for me to learn because it requires genuine patience in a moment when you've already prepared what you want to say. But "what was going on for you in that moment?" does something that launching into your explanation never can. It creates the possibility that you're about to learn something. It signals that this is a real conversation. And it gives the other person a chance to arrive at the insight themselves, which means they're far more likely to actually act on it.

I've had people, in the space of answering that question, realize exactly what the problem was and articulate it more clearly than I ever would have. When that happens you're not delivering feedback anymore. You're just confirming what they already understand, and that's a completely different conversation.

After that, you say why it matters. Not why it bothered you. Why it creates a real problem for the work, for the team, for the person's own trajectory. People can dismiss personal reactions, but they can't easily dismiss consequences that connect directly to things they care about.

The conversation ends with a path, something specific and observable. And then, critically, you follow up. Not as oversight but as evidence that you're paying attention to the growth and not just to the failure. A short note two weeks later, a moment of genuine recognition when you see the change, these are not extras. They're the proof that the feedback was an investment and not a verdict.

What changed how I thought about all of this was recognizing that the managers I most respected in my own career didn't treat hard feedback as a high-stakes event. They treated it as a normal part of how they showed up for the people around them. There was no buildup, no ceremony, no weeks of avoidance. Something happened, they addressed it clearly and without cruelty, and the relationship continued.

When it's normal, it stops being frightening. And when it stops being frightening, people stop hiding things from you, and they stop hiding from themselves.

That's the whole point of this. Not a single conversation executed cleanly. A relationship where real things can be said, heard, and actually used.
