The conversation I kept having in my head went something like this: I knew exactly what I wanted to say. I'd worked out the words, the tone, the specific example I was going to use. I'd thought about it on the way to work, rehearsed it during a meeting I wasn't really present for, and revised it again before I knocked on the door. And then I'd walk in, start talking, and watch the other person's face close.

Not because I was wrong. Usually I wasn't wrong. But because I'd walked into their space without warning and started telling them something about themselves they weren't ready to hear. The content was fine. The delivery was a problem. And I kept doing the same thing, wondering why nothing was changing.

The shift happened for me when I started treating feedback as a conversation you have to set up, not just a message you have to compose.

There's a well-known framework called SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact. It's useful. It gives you a structure for what goes inside the feedback. The situation sets the context. The behavior is the specific, observable thing the person did. The impact describes what changed because of it. If you're not using SBI, you should be.

But SBI doesn't tell you how to open the door. It assumes the door is already open. Most of the time, it isn't, and that's where things fall apart.

What I eventually worked out, through repetition and failure, is that there's a choreography to effective feedback. The content matters. The structure matters. But the sequence is what determines whether any of it gets received.

The first move is the one that changed everything for me: ask permission.

Two words. "Can I?" Full question: "Can I give you some feedback?" And then you stop talking and wait for yes.

The reason this works isn't psychological manipulation. It's just how people function. When someone grants you permission to say something, they've shifted their posture. They've agreed, out loud, to receive information. The defensive reflex, the one that fires before you finish your first sentence when you lead with an observation about someone's behavior, doesn't trigger the same way when they've said yes first. The gate is open. You're not climbing over a wall.

The second move is to describe a behavior, not a person. "You're dismissive" is a character claim. People dispute character claims. They defend against them. The conversation becomes an argument about who they are, which is not useful. "When you walked into the meeting and started talking without checking in" is something a camera could have recorded. It's specific, it's bounded in time, and it doesn't require the other person to accept a verdict about their identity to engage with it.

Third, name the impact in terms of outcome, not feeling. "It cut our conversation short and we didn't finish what we needed to finish" is concrete. It describes what actually changed in the world. That's harder to dispute than an emotional interpretation, and it keeps the conversation anchored in something you can both look at together.

The fourth move is where most people stumble. Instead of handing over the solution, you ask: "What do you think we could do differently next time?"

I cannot overstate how much this step matters.

When you give someone the answer, they nod. Maybe they write it down. By Thursday it's gone. When you make them generate the answer, even part of it, they own it in a way that sticks. The act of thinking it through, of articulating it in their own words, encodes it differently.

The obstacle is the "I don't know." It comes up constantly, and most people fill the silence immediately, usually by offering the solution they had ready the whole time. That's a mistake. What I've learned to do is say "Think about it. Just one idea." And then wait. The silence is uncomfortable. I let it be uncomfortable. Most of the time, in the next ten or fifteen seconds, the actual answer appears. What came before was the first vague guess thrown out to make the discomfort stop. What comes after the silence is real.

If they genuinely can't get there, then you offer something. But you hold the space first, every time.

The fifth move is simple: confirm what you agreed on, and thank them. Not a performance of gratitude. Not excessive warmth. Just "Let's try that next time. I appreciate you being open to this." You're landing the next action, and you're acknowledging that sitting with feedback gracefully is something worth noticing.

The whole sequence takes sixty to ninety seconds when it goes well. The first time I timed it, I was surprised. All that rehearsal, all that dread, for a conversation that was over before I'd finished a cup of coffee.

What I've come to understand is that most of the difficulty in giving feedback lives in the anticipation, not the delivery. The anticipation is hard because we imagine the worst version of the conversation: the defensiveness, the argument, the damaged relationship. The Permission-First sequence short-circuits most of that by changing the conditions before the first substantive word is spoken. You're not launching at someone. You're opening a door and waiting for them to step through it.

There's one place this breaks down. If the relationship is already badly damaged, if the trust is gone and both people know it, then the permission question can land as sarcasm. "Can I give you some feedback?" reads as hostile when the history between two people has made that kind of formality feel like a setup. In that case, no feedback framework helps until the relationship itself is addressed. The sequence is a tool for professional conversations that have a functioning foundation. It doesn't rebuild foundations.

But for the conversations most managers are avoiding, the ones sitting in the back of the mind for weeks because the choreography feels too hard, this is the sequence. Ask permission, describe the behavior, name the outcome, invite them to solve it, confirm and thank.

The shape is what does the work. Get the shape right.
