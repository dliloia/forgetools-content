## When You Say It Matters as Much as What You Say

I learned this the hard way, the way most managers learn the things that stick.

I was running a team and had a direct report who had a specific habit in customer meetings. He would answer questions that were still being asked. A customer would start a sentence and before they finished it, he'd be in. Half the time he was right about where the question was headed. The other half, he wasn't, and the customer would pause, slightly thrown, and recalibrate. Some of them stopped asking questions altogether.

I noticed it the first week. I told myself I'd bring it up in his next 1:1. Then the 1:1 came and we had other things to cover, and I filed it away. I watched it happen three more times over the following two weeks. By the time I actually said anything, I had a month of examples.

The conversation was a disaster. Not because I delivered it badly, I think I was actually pretty careful with the words. It was a disaster because he had no idea what I was talking about. He couldn't access the behavior I was describing. It had been too long. He'd had fifteen other customer conversations since the ones I was referencing, and none of them felt notable to him. He got defensive, and I didn't blame him. From where he was sitting, he'd been doing his job for a month without a problem, and his manager was now telling him there'd been a problem all along.

We recovered from it. But I had lost four weeks of potential development time for someone I was actively trying to grow.

I made the same mistake less frequently after that. Not because I invented a new philosophy, just because I felt the cost of delay in a way that was hard to forget.

The thing about feedback timing that most management training gets wrong is that it frames real-time feedback as an emotional intelligence skill. Something about meeting people where they are, being responsive to feelings, all of that. And while none of that is wrong exactly, it misses the more important point. Timing is a precision tool. You use it correctly because it works, not because it's kind.

When you give someone feedback about something they did ten minutes ago, they can still feel it. The decision they made, the moment they made it, the alternative they didn't take. They're close enough to the experience that your perspective can actually attach to it. You're not asking them to reconstruct something. You're adding a layer to something they're still holding.

Delay strips that away. By Friday, they're holding a story about the week, not a memory of Tuesday's meeting. And stories are harder to change than memories.

I've thought about this in terms of how we actually form habits, which is the real goal of most developmental feedback. Habits change at the point of behavior, not in retrospect. If someone makes a decision and immediately gets new information that reframes that decision, the brain updates. The update is connected to the neural pathway that produced the behavior. If the reframing comes three days later in a scheduled meeting, it's processed as information about the past, not as an update to an active pattern.

That's why real-time feedback works at a level that delayed feedback simply cannot match, even when the delayed version is more polished, more carefully worded, better framed.

There's also something that happens to a manager who consistently holds feedback. You start to see your people through the accumulated weight of everything you haven't said yet. You're in a meeting with someone and in the back of your mind you're aware of the three things you're holding about them. That awareness creates a low-grade distance. You're managing someone and keeping notes on them at the same time, and those aren't the same posture.

Real-time feedback clears the slate. You say the thing, they hear it, you both move forward. The relationship stays clean. The 1:1 becomes a conversation instead of a debrief.

None of this means you should say everything the moment you think it. Some feedback needs time. Some situations need privacy that a shared hallway can't provide. Sometimes you're too close to something to be useful to someone else, and waiting until you've settled is the right call. Judgment about when to wait is part of the skill.

But the default should be soon, not later. The bias should be toward the moment, not the meeting.

If I could go back and give myself one piece of advice in my first few years managing people, it would be this: the feedback you're saving for the 1:1 is costing you more than you think. Say it now, say it simply, and move on. The meeting is for everything the moment can't hold.
