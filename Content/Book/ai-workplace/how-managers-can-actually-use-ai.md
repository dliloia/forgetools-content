## The Tuesday Afternoon Test

There's a pattern I've watched play out across the last few years, and I've seen it in enough organizations now that I think it's worth naming directly.

Someone on a leadership team decides the company needs to "embrace AI." There's a kickoff meeting. Maybe a consultant comes in. Managers are encouraged to explore tools. A Slack channel gets created. Someone shares an article about ChatGPT. Then, about six weeks later, almost nothing has changed about how anyone actually does their job.

The managers who got the most out of that initiative were not the most enthusiastic in the kickoff meeting. They were the ones who went back to their desks and immediately tried to use the tool on something real.

That's the test I've started using with myself, and it's the one I'd offer anyone who wants to close the gap between talking about AI and actually working with it. Not "can I articulate a vision for how AI will transform our industry?" but "did I use AI on something real this Tuesday afternoon?"

I had a performance conversation to prepare for a while back that I was not looking forward to. The person had real strengths. They also had a persistent blind spot that was starting to affect the people around them, and every previous attempt I'd made to address it had landed badly. Not because I'd been dishonest, but because the framing kept triggering defensiveness before the actual substance of the conversation could land.

I'd been a manager long enough to know that this kind of conversation has a shape. You need to establish trust before you can introduce tension. You need to anchor in specific behavior rather than character. You need to leave the person with a path forward that feels achievable, not a verdict they're being handed.

I knew all that. I still wasn't happy with how I'd written it up.

So I tried something I'd been doing more of. I described the situation to Claude. Not the person's name, nothing identifying. Just the shape of the problem: what I needed to communicate, why it kept not landing, what a successful outcome would look like. Then I asked it to help me think through the framing.

What it gave me wasn't a script. It was a resequencing. A different place to start the conversation, one that established common ground before introducing the difficult feedback instead of the other way around. It also surfaced an assumption I'd been making that wasn't necessarily true, which had been making my framing come across as a conclusion rather than an opening.

I used about a third of what it gave me. The rest I adjusted or discarded. But the conversation went better than any of the previous attempts had.

The reason wasn't that the AI wrote a magical script. The reason was that it helped me think through the problem from a different angle, faster than I would have gotten there alone, and without the social complexity of asking a colleague to pressure-test my approach to a sensitive personnel situation.

That's the actual value, and it's different from what most of the public conversation about AI in the workplace describes.

Most of what gets written about AI for managers is pitched at the macro level: AI will change everything, here are the five ways AI will transform leadership, here is why you need an AI strategy. That framing is mostly useless for a manager trying to get through a hard week. It describes the landscape without telling you where to put your feet.

The practical reality is that AI is useful for managers in a handful of specific, unsexy ways. Preparing for conversations that have a history of going badly. Getting a competent first draft of something you hate writing, a job posting, a policy document, a written warning. Processing a dense meeting into clear action items. Thinking through a complex decision without pulling a stakeholder into it who has a stake in the outcome.

None of that is glamorous. All of it saves real time or produces better output.

What AI cannot do is the part that matters most about managing people. It cannot sit across from someone who is struggling and sense what's actually going on beneath what they're telling you. It cannot make the judgment call about who is ready for more responsibility and who needs a different kind of support first. It cannot build the years of trust that make hard feedback possible. It cannot be you.

The failure mode I've seen in managers who try to use AI heavily is the same failure mode that shows up with every tool that makes a task easier: they start delegating the thinking instead of just the execution. I've seen managers produce performance reviews that were entirely AI-generated, signed with their name, sent to people who deserved real human reflection. That's not using AI to do your job better. That's using AI to avoid your job while appearing to do it.

The tool should sharpen your thinking. The moment it starts replacing it, you've got a problem.

The managers who are actually building an advantage right now are not the ones running the most sophisticated AI workflows. They're the ones who have quietly made the tool part of how they work, the same way a good manager has always made notes after a difficult meeting, or talked through a hard decision with a trusted peer. Small habits, consistently applied, producing better output over time.

That's what it actually looks like. Not a transformation. Just Tuesday afternoon, a real problem, and the willingness to pick up the tool and use it.
